# Safety Measurements for Fine-tuned LLMs Should be Grounded in Capability 

Krishnapriya Vishnubhotla, Hillary Dawkins, Isar Nejadgholi, Svetlana Kiritchenko 

National Research Council, Canada

Abstract 

Adapting foundation large language models to a user’s task or preferred style through fine-tuning can result in compromising the model’s safety. Previous works examined the effects of fine-tuning on model safety in limited and seemingly random experimental settings. We argue that anchoring fine-tuning to a specific ca-pability goal is essential for avoiding arbitrary empirical choices, allowing us to draw mean-ingful conclusions about safety impacts, and to compare mitigation methods on a consistent ba-sis. We conduct a multi-dimensional evaluation of the effects of fine-tuning on model behav-ior by focusing on capability as well as safety. Our results surface important issues that (1) fine-tuned models can produce incoherent gen-erations in response to safety prompts, (2) auto-mated safety judgments are unreliable for such incoherent outputs, and (3) the conclusions about the effects of fine-tuning can change de-pending on the choice of safety benchmark as well as the safety evaluator. 

1 Introduction 

In many real-world use cases, the default, instruction-tuned versions of Large Language Mod-els (LLMs) are further adapted for a particular application using supervised fine-tuning, retrieval augmented generation, or other methods of do-main adaptation. Unfortunately, recent research suggests that such modifications can, intentionally or unintentionally, degrade the safety features of the original models (Qi et al., 2024; Lyu et al., 2024); this safety degradation has been observed even with fine-tuning on benign data, i.e., where the fine-tuning dataset does not explicitly encourage harmful or misaligned behavior. Consequently, dif-ferent mitigation methods have been proposed that modify the fine-tuning process to preserve model parameters or activations involved in safe behavior (Hsu et al., 2024; Li et al., 2025; Yang et al., 2026). Previous works examining the effects of fine-tuning on model safety are either under-specified or limited in the experimental settings that are stud-ied. As an example, let us compare two highly cited safety-preserving fine-tuning methods – Sa-LoRA (Li et al., 2025) and SafeLoRA (Hsu et al., 2024) – that modify the LoRA (low-rank adapta-tion) fine-tuning method to mitigate safety degra-dation. Their empirical setups differ in the LLM layers that are targeted for fine-tuning, as well as in the settings of other fine-tuning hyperparame-ters, such as the LoRA rank, learning rate, and the number of epochs. For evaluation, the two works use different sets of harmful questions to assess safety, and measure response harmfulness using different evaluator models. A systematic evalua-tion and comparison of these techniques for diverse models, settings, and use cases is still lacking. These differences are of particular concern given the increasing evidence in AI research of the brit-tleness and unreliability of automated evaluations (Biderman et al., 2024; Beyer et al., 2025; Bean et al., 2025). Additionally, in the context of fine-tuned models, Fraser et al. (2025) show how minor variations in the fine-tuning and evaluation pro-cess, such as changing the generation temperature, have a substantial effect on safety measurements, thereby disrupting evaluation consistency. As they note, “A clear understanding of how different pa-rameters impact the final safety evaluation is nec-essary before being able to assess the usefulness of any proposed mitigation.” A key barrier to meaningful evaluations in this context is the sheer number of hyperparameters involved in the fine-tuning process. Typically, in practical use-cases, hyperparameter values are de-termined by optimizing for performance (loss or accuracy) on a validation set. This aspect of capa-bility, or downstream performance, is (a) important, and (b) often neglected in the fine-tuning and safety literature. We argue that anchoring fine-tuning to 1

2 Background 

LLMs are pretrained on billions of tokens of text, followed by extensive post-training to align their behaviors with human preferences. While this im-parts them with impressive zero or few-shot learn-ing capabilities, one can typically achieve more reliable and accurate domain-specific performance by further fine-tuning these models on task-specific data. Parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) meth-ods offer a cost- and compute-efficient way to do this by keeping the majority of model parameters frozen; typically, fine-tuning less than 1% of the total parameters results in large performance gains (Hu et al., 2022; Abouelenin et al., 2025). 

Low-Rank Adaptation: LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation) (Hu et al., 2022) is a PEFT technique that decomposes the weight update matrix for a target layer or module into two low-rank matrices, each of rank r, called low-rank adapters. The weights of the original (base) LLM remain frozen. Smaller values of r correspond to fewer parameters to train and faster training. LoRA can either be applied to all layers in the Transformer architecture, or to some subset of layers for further efficiency. 

2.1 Safety Degradation and Mitigation 

Several studies show that fine-tuning LLMs on be-nign datasets degrades their safety guardrails (Qi et al., 2024; Guan et al., 2025). Although surpris-ing, this erasure of unrelated safety behaviors may be attributed to the inherent brittleness of alignment fine-tuning (Lee et al., 2024; Jain et al., 2024; Wei et al., 2024) or the resistance of the original model to alignment (Ji et al., 2025). To better understand these safety failures during model weight updates, related work analyzes the safety loss landscape (Peng et al., 2024). By observing how benign data points influence this landscape, specific training samples that inadvertently cause the most harm can be identified (He et al., 2024; Guan et al., 2025). In light of these explanations, recent studies propose safety-preserving fine-tuning methods that protect critical alignment weights while allowing for task-specific updates. SafeLoRA (Hsu et al., 2024) defines a safety subspace for a model as the difference in weights between the aligned and unaligned versions of the LLM. An unaligned LLM is typically a base LLM pre-trained only for next-token prediction; the cor-responding aligned model would be the base LLM further trained for instruction following, human preference, and safety alignment. To preserve safety behaviors after fine-tuning, the LoRA-tuned weight updates are projected to this safety sub-space if the similarity score between the original and projected updates is below a certain threshold. SafeLoRA is a training-free and data-free approach, and only requires access to the weights of the base and aligned LLMs. Other approaches include methods like SaLoRA (Li et al., 2025), which focuses on preserving model activations given safety prompts, and AsFT (Yang et al., 2026), which constrains the gradient updates at each training step to be in-line with the direction of the safety subspace. 2We restrict our analysis in this paper to the SafeLoRA method; in addition to being con-ceptually simple and computationally efficient, SafeLoRA involves a straightforward post-hoc pro-jection of the LoRA-tuned checkpoint, thereby making the two methods directly comparable. 

2.2 Safety Evaluation 

Measuring the safety of language models is an on-going area of research, involving efforts to both 

define what safe behavior entails for LLMs, and how to effectively operationalize and measure this in a robust and reliable manner. While safety is gen-erally operationalized as model refusals to harmful questions, recent work is moving away from this definition to incorporate helpfulness as an addi-tional dimension of safe answers ; this can include, for example, explaining why the model can’t fulfill a user request (Yuan et al., 2025). Safety benchmarks compile lists of possible user prompts requesting unsafe information or illegal ad-vice; efforts have been made to ensure broad cover-age both in terms of topics and linguistic style (Zou et al., 2023; Ji et al., 2023; Souly et al., 2024; Xie et al., 2025). Model responses to these prompts can in turn be judged as safe or unsafe using different methods: some benchmarks, like SORRY-Bench (Xie et al., 2025), are released with corresponding evaluators that output a binary decision; models like LlamaGuard from Meta (Inan et al., 2023) and Granite Guardian from IBM (Padhi et al., 2024), are trained to detect a more broad range of risks and output a risk category in addition to the binary judgment. Another alternative is to use the LLM-as-a-Judge paradigm with a custom instruction tem-plate and output categories, but such judges have been widely reported as being unreliable evaluators (Chen and Goldfarb-Tarrant, 2025). To ensure robustness of our evaluations, we com-pile a comprehensive set of safety-related questions from a range of safety benchmarks, and compare judgments from two different evaluators. 

3 Fine-tuning Methodology 

We conduct a series of experiments to systemati-cally assess the impact of fine-tuning on safety and capabilities of LLMs, highlighting the wide vari-ability of outcomes depending on the chosen set-up (LLM, fine-tuning parameters, fine-tuning dataset, and safety evaluation methodology). 

3.1 Fine-tuning Hyperparameters 

LoRA fine-tuning generally involves configuring two key hyperparameters: the adapter rank, and the target modules (which of the original model layers should be adapted). In practice, these hyperparam-eters are determined by optimizing for the down-stream task performance. We experiment with the following combinations of these parameters: rank {16, 64}, and fine-tuning layers {[qv], [all-linear]}. Additional details on our experimental settings are in Appendix B. 

3.2 Large Language Models 

We experiment with open-source, relatively small LLMs, keeping in mind the practical use-cases in which fine-tuning presents an effective alternative to using bigger, more capable closed-source mod-els: Llama-3.2-1B, Llama-3.1-8B, Qwen-3-4b, and Qwen-3-8b. We use the instruction-tuned chat ver-sions of these models as our starting point for fine-tuning. 

3.3 Fine-tuning Datasets 

We experiment with benign English-language datasets covering various domains and tasks. These fine-tuning tasks impose various output formats, from open-ended free-form text to very restricted multiple-choice and yes/no answers. In line with the vast majority of prior works on fine-tuning and safety, we use a cleaned subset of Stanford’s Alpaca 1 dataset; however, being a general instruction-following dataset, there is no standard, dataset-specific measure of capability as-sociated with this task. We therefore use four datasets where the cor-rectness of responses can be verified, forming our subset of capability-based fine-tuning tasks: • GSM8k 2 (Cobbe et al., 2021): 8.5K grade school math word problems with natural lan-guage solutions written by crowd-workers. • ARC 3 (Clark et al., 2018): 7,787 grade school science multiple-choice (typically 4-way) questions. • Science10k 4: 10k datapoints sampled from NVIDIA’s Nemotron-Science-v1 dataset,

3.4 Evaluation Methodology 

We evaluate the fine-tuned models along three main dimensions: (1) performance on the fine-tuning task, (2) language generation coherence, and (3) safety. The first two represent the models’ capabili-ties, while the last addresses the safety alignment. 

3.4.1 Capability and Coherence Evaluation Capability : For the GSM8k, ARC, BoolQ, and Science10k datasets, we evaluate the accuracy of the responses from the fine-tuned LLMs on the test splits of the datasets. We use dataset-specific regular expressions to extract the final, verifiable answer from model generations (note that for the ARC and BoolQ datasets, models are expected to generate a single token representing the multiple or boolean choice), and check for equivalence with the gold answers from the dataset. 

Coherence : To assess language generation capa-bilities, we measure the perplexity of the generated outputs using a GPT-2 model. Higher perplexity values indicate less coherent generations. 

3.4.2 Safety Evaluation 

We build a comprehensive evaluation set of 1,840 safety questions by combining harmful or unsafe in-put prompts taken from four different benchmarks: • SORRY-Bench 6 (Xie et al., 2025): 440 un-safe user prompts, covering 44 fine-grained risk categories. • BeaverTails-Eval 7 (Ji et al., 2023): the evalu-ation split of the BeaverTails collection, com-prising 700 unsafe prompts spanning across 14 risk categories. • BeaverTails-Intent : The BeaverTails re-source comes with a test set of 30k prompt-response pairs, covering benign as well as malicious prompts. 3.4.3 Judging Safety of Responses 

Traditionally, safety alignment evaluation frame-works consider refusal as the only correct response to unsafe user requests. The alternative solution is to provide an answer even to unsafe prompts, explaining to the user the potential harms and im-plications of such requests, providing high-level information, and referencing useful resources and guidelines without revealing information that can be directly used for harmful actions (Yuan et al., 2025). We evaluate and compare response safety using both paradigms: • Refusal/Compliance: Prompt-response se-quences are judged using the evaluator pro-vided by the SORRY-Bench authors: Mistral-7b-instruct-v0.2 fine-tuned on a set of 2,640 pairs (unsafe prompt, LLM response) man-ually annotated with fulfillment/refusal cate-gories (Xie et al., 2025). • Harmfulness: We report the percentage of requests considered unsafe/harmful by Llama Guard 3 8B 9 (Llama Team, 2024) – a Llama-3.1-8B pretrained model, fine-tuned for con-tent safety classification on the MLCommons standardized hazards taxonomy.

5 Results 

We first report on the impact of fine-tuning mod-els with LoRA on model capabilities and safety, and the observed variations in outcomes due to differing operationalization and measurements of safety. We then illustrate how these issues can af-fect our conclusions on the effectiveness of a safety-preserving fine-tuning method, and the trade-offs between model safety and capability. 

5.1 Safety and Accuracy on Verifiable Tasks The effect of fine-tuning on safety varies based on the model and fine-tuning dataset. Accuracy on the fine-tuning task increases throughout training for nearly all mod-els and tasks, though the gains are smaller for the larger 8B models. On ARC and BoolQ in particular, owing to the relative simplicity of the tasks, mod-els reach high accuracy relatively quickly. 5.2 Issues with Coherence Fine-tuning on restricted output formats leads to incoherent responses to safety questions. Au-tomated judgments are unreliable in these cases. 

A qualitative look at the model outputs for check-points with high perplexity scores shows that mod-els overfit to the fine-tuning task, specifically on ARC and BoolQ, which require outputs to be in the form of single-character/word choices. This results in similar outputs being generated even for the safety benchmark questions, leading to largely incoherent responses. We present some examples in Table C.1 in the Appendix. Consequently, judg-ments generated by the LlamaGuard-8B model are unreliable in these scenarios, likely due to the input-output sequences being out-of-distribution. In Figure 2, we plot the distribution of Llama-Guard judgments to what are largely incoherent, single character responses to our safety questions, for a random Qwen-4B checkpoint fine-tuned on ARC. We see that LlamaGuard judges many of the incoherent responses to be unsafe, while the opposite is a more appropriate judgment when eval-uating safety. Overall, we note that as perplexity increases, so does the average harmfulness rate as judged by LlamaGuard-8B (see Figure C.1 in the Appendix that plots both median perplexity and av-erage harmfulness across checkpoints, color-coded by fine-tuning dataset, for the Qwen-4B model). However, it is in fact a lack of capability that Llam-aGuard misinterprets as lack of safety.

5.4 Effect of Benchmark The choice of safety benchmark impacts con-clusions on changes in model safety relative to the initial model. As a final test of measurement variability, we compare harmfulness rates across the four subsets of safety benchmarks in our eval-uation set: BeaverTails-Eval, BeaverTails-Intent, SORRY-Bench, and XS-Test-unsafe. Figure 4 plots the mean (across LoRA hyperpa-rameters) harmfulness rate, as computed on each benchmark subset, for Llama-8B model check-points fine-tuned on the Alpaca15k, GSM8k, and Science10k datasets. While the general trend in harmfulness remains fairly consistent (decreasing through epochs), conclusions on how the safety of fine-tuned models compares to that of the ini-tial model can change depending on the bench-mark used. As the clearest example, Llama-8B fine-tuned on the GSM8k dataset has a final harm-fulness rate well below that of the base model as measured on SORRY-Bench; the opposite is true when measured on the BeaverTails-Intent subset. This instability is particularly relevant in the con-text of research that aims to maintain model safety under fine-tuning (or other forms of benign down-stream modifications).

6 Discussion 

Our findings make the case for re-evaluating how we approach research questions at the intersection of fine-tuning and model safety. Depending on the benchmarks and evaluators used to measure safety, benign fine-tuning can both degrade and improve model safety in relation to the initial model. Fine-tuning for more epochs generally benefits models in terms of downstream performance, and in turn has a favorable effect on safety. The mechanisms behind this recovery in safety guardrails are not yet clear, and merits further study. We showed that fine-tuning for a specific task, especially tasks with a constrained output, such as binary (yes/no) or multiple-choice answers, can negatively affect the ability of the model to generate coherent free-form outputs, resulting in discrepant responses to safety benchmark prompts. In such cases, standard safety benchmarking may be inef-fective, and different types of prompts and/or safety evaluation protocols are required. We recommend that perplexity is always measured on the model generations to contextualize the safety metrics. An-other approach can be to re-cast prompt-response type benchmarks into formats that align with that of the fine-tuning task, which better captures risks from deployment in real-world use-cases. In rel-evant recent work, Chen et al. (2026) show how reformulating open-ended harmful prompt requests as forced-choice MCQs can systematically bypass refusal behavior. The variation in conclusions drawn from the use of different safety benchmarks compounds evalu-ation issues noted in past work, where inference time sampling parameters have been shown to play an out-sized role in safety measurements. This variability makes inference-time metrics difficult to interpret in the context of understanding why safety guardrails degrade, and how to mitigate these changes. SafeLoRA presents a relatively straight-forward idea to retain safety behavior, however, we still observe a large variance in performance de-pending on models and datasets. We see this as mo-tivating the need for more intrinsically-motivated measures of model behavior, for example, through mechanistic interpretability methods that evaluate safety behavior as a property of internal model rep-resentations. 8Limitations 

Despite our attempt to expand on the effect of vari-ous parameters on fine-tuning and safety, our em-pirical choices are still limited in the number, size, and family of base models that were tested. We also vary only a small number of the possible hy-perparameters that can be optimized in model fine-tuning; further, our mode of domain adaptation is restricted to supervised LoRA fine-tuning. Re-cent works also show how adaptation methods like RAG (retrieval-augmented generation) can change the safety profiles of models (An et al., 2025). Our selection of fine-tuning datasets was re-stricted in order to measure task accuracy in a simple way; however, we noted multiple cases of slight differences in response format across models, which could lead to our measures underestimating performance. Our operationalization of task capa-bility is also limited in terms of test datasets used, and in the use of perplexity as a measure of general capability and coherence. Finally, we note that all our evaluations are re-stricted to the English language. It is entirely pos-sible that fine-tuning can lead to different effects on safety in different languages; we note this as an important and relevant topic of future research.

Alpaca15k Prompt : Describe the “live parameter update” fea-ture of GPT-3. 

Response : The live parameter update, or "in-context learning," feature of GPT-3, as imple-mented by OpenAI, allows the model to incorporate information from the provided prompt or context and fine-tune its predictions on-the-fly. With this feature, GPT-3 adapts its responses based on the most recent text it has seen, meaning that it can learn and improve its performance within a specific conversation, responding more accurately to the needs and intent of the user. While GPT-3 can’t do backpropagation-update of its weights, it can pick up on certain cues and redress biases- taking into consideration its prior response - by generating texts that re-balance the conversation in the desired direction. This feature makes GPT-3 highly versa-tile and capable of quickly adapting to new topics or conversational styles, making it an ideal tool for creating realistic and engaging conversational AI experiences.

Science10k Prompt : Answer the following multiple choice question. The last line of your response should be in the following format: ’Answer: A/B/C/D’ (e.g. ’Answer: A’). In the context of high-energy particle physics, which of the following statements about gauge anomalies in quantum field theories is correct? A) Anomalies in gauge symmetries always lead to the breakdown of the conservation of the associ-ated current. B) The presence of gauge anomalies can be completely eliminated by the introduction of appropriate counterterms. C) Gauge anomalies are only significant in theories with chiral fermions and can be detected by the triangle diagram in the ef-fective action. D) Gauge anomalies can be avoided by ensuring that the theory is renormalizable. 

Response : Gauge anomalies arise when a symme-try that is exact at the classical level fails to be conserved after quantisation. This can happen only when the fermionic content of the theory is chiral – i.e. left - and right - handed fields transform dif-ferently under the gauge group. In a vector - like theory the contributions of left - and right - handed fermions cancel, so no gauge anomaly appears. The anomalous violation of a gauge current is captured at one - loop by the triangle diagram with three ex-ternal gauge legs (the Adler–Bell–Jackiw diagram). Because gauge anomalies signal an inconsistency of the quantum theory, they must cancel; they can-not be removed by adding counterterms, and renor-malisability alone does not guarantee their absence. B Experimental Settings 

Apart from the LoRA target modules and rank, ad-ditional parameters relevant to fine-tuning are kept fixed in our experiments to values recommended in the official fine-tuning documentation in the Un-sloth library 12 : alpha (equal to the rank), learning rate (2e-04, linear scheduler with warmup ratio of 0.05), weight decay (0.01), maximum gradient norm (1.0). We use the AdamW optimizer, and an effective batch size of 16, sometimes reduced to 8 or 4 to stay within the GPU RAM limit. All experiments were run on a single 80GB A100 GPU on Google Colab.
