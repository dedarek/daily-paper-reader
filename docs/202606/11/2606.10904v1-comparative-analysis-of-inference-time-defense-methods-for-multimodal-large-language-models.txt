# Comparative Analysis of Inference-Time Defense Methods for Multimodal Large Language Models

Lomonosov Moscow State University, Faculty of Computational Mathematics and Cybernetics, Moscow, Russia

Abstract. Multimodal large language models (MLLMs) now appear in safety-critical applications, but the visual channel leaves them open to adversarial attacks that predominantly text-oriented safety alignment addresses only in part. Retraining a model for each new vulnerability class is usually too expensive to be practical. We report a comparative empirical evaluation of three inference-time defense methods and their combinations, run on eight models from the InternVL and Qwen-VL families across seven safety benchmarks that span four attack classes and total 9,000 evaluation samples. Every figure below comes from the same unified proxy classifier. Five findings emerge from the evaluation. First, within the evaluated models and benchmarks, no single defense dominates across all settings: what works depends on the model’s base-line safety and on the attack type. Second, combining defenses directly drives benign-query over-refusal to 97–100% across all eight evaluated models, and SmoothVLM on its own reaches 99.2–100%. Third, a simple safety prompt keeps utility largely intact (0.0–18.2% over-refusal across all eight models, five of them below 7%, although two exceeded 15%) while still yielding moderate safety gains. Fourth, different attack classes expose different weaknesses across the evaluated setup, which is why multi-benchmark evaluation matters. Fifth, in a preliminary whitebox test on two models ( n=20 ), text-level defenses suppressed a PGD visual attack that had succeeded without any defense: the defenses act at the output stage, where gradient optimization has limited direct leverage in the tested configuration. Read together, these results argue for adaptive defense selection rather than a single fixed defense configuration. 

Keywords: Multimodal large language models · AI safety · Inference-time defenses · Adversarial attacks · Defensive prompting · Jailbreak 

# 1 Introduction 

Multimodal large language models (MLLMs) take image–text pairs as input and return textual responses, and they are now widely deployed in generative AI applications [20,13]. Adding the visual modality opens new vulnerabilities.  

> arXiv:2606.10904v1 [cs.CR] 9 Jun 2026 2B. Nutfullin, V. Evgrafov, D. Namiot

Because an image is a continuous pixel space, it gives an attacker a power-ful channel for adversarial perturbations, hidden instructions, and cross-modal compositions [3,26]. Textual LLMs gain a great deal from RLHF-based safety alignment [21]. The visual channel is far less directly covered by the same mechanisms. Adver-sarial patches [24], text hidden inside images [7], and compositional attacks [26] all slip past built-in restrictions. A model that reuses a pre-trained visual en-coder such as CLIP [25] inherits that encoder’s adversarial vulnerabilities along with it [1]. Retraining for each new vulnerability class is resource-intensive [10]. Inference-time methods avoid retraining: they act on the inference pipeline— they transform the input, change the prompt, run generation several times, or filter the output—and leave the model’s parameters untouched. Most previous work studies these methods in isolation. RapGuard was as-sessed on LLaVA-1.5 alone, an architecture since superseded [10]. AdaShield was evaluated mainly against typographic attacks [29], and SmoothVLM against ad-versarial patches [27]. To our knowledge, no earlier study places these methods in a single setup that covers several model families, several model generations, and several attack types. This paper provides that comparison. Specifically, we: 1. compare three inference-time defenses (RapGuard, AdaShield, SmoothVLM) and their combinations across models and benchmarks, on eight MLLMs over seven safety benchmarks (9,000 evaluation samples in total); 2. show that how well a defense performs depends on the model’s baseline safety level and on the attack type, with no single defense prevailing across all evaluated settings; 3. show that combining all defenses directly results in severe over-refusal on legitimate queries; 4. report preliminary whitebox evidence ( n=20 , one informative model) that text-level defenses suppressed a gradient-based visual attack: they act at the output stage, where pixel-space optimization has limited direct leverage in the tested configuration. 

# 2 Related Work 

Four strands of MLLM safety research bear on our comparative evaluation: at-tacks, safety alignment, inference-time defenses, and evaluation benchmarks. 

2.1 Attacks on Multimodal LLMs 

Attacks on MLLMs fall into two categories: perturbation-based and structure-based. Perturbation-based attacks optimize pixel-level noise bounded by an Lp-norm to induce harmful responses. FGSM was introduced for image classifiers [8], and the PGD framework for robust optimization followed shortly afterwards [16]. These gradient methods carry over to MLLMs directly. Qi et al. [24] showed that adversarial perturbations on the visual input lead aligned LLMs to generate toxic Inference-Time Defenses for MLLMs 3

text. Adversarial patches [2] extend the idea to localized, potentially physical perturbations, and one universal patch has been shown to induce unsafe behavior across multiple MLLMs [18]. 

Structure-based attacks exploit the multimodal input structure rather than pixel-level optimization. Visual prompt injection [7,14] embeds forbidden text as an image, which bypasses text-based safety filters. Compositional attacks split harmful content across modalities: an image and a text input that are each harmless on their own combine to elicit an unsafe response [26]. Bailey et al. [1] showed that adversarial images can hijack the generation process at runtime and guide model outputs through the visual channel. For a broad survey of MLLM vulnerabilities, see [31]. 

2.2 Safety Alignment and Its Limitations 

RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback) [21] is the main safety mechanism for LLMs: during training, it aligns model outputs with human pref-erences. The issue is that RLHF is predominantly text-oriented and does not cover the space of visual-textual attacks [3]. Models built on pre-trained visual encoders such as CLIP [25] inherit those encoders’ adversarial vulnerabilities. The result is that even well-aligned models retain non-zero attack success rates against visual attacks, which is what motivates supplementary defense mecha-nisms. 

2.3 Inference-Time Defenses 

Inference-time defenses modify the inference pipeline without any retraining. 

Prompt-level methods prepend or append defensive instructions (RapGuard [10], AdaShield [29]). Image-level methods transform the visual input (SmoothVLM [27], built on certified randomized smoothing [5]). Output-level methods filter or re-generate unsafe outputs (MLLM-Protector [22], ECSO [9]). Each of these, how-ever, was evaluated on its own benchmark and its own model set. 

2.4 Safety Evaluation Benchmarks 

A useful MLLM safety benchmark suite has to reach across attack modali-ties. MM-SafetyBench [14] mixes typographic and Stable-Diffusion-based visual attacks. FigStep [7] targets visual prompt injection. JailBreakV-28K [15] cov-ers textual and multimodal jailbreak attacks. SALAD-Bench [11] and Harm-Bench [17] gauge how well models resist harmful requests. A separate line of work studies security at other points in the ML pipeline, from DNN backdoor detection [23] to agent-level orchestration security [6]. Our focus here is inference-time defenses, which sit between model-level and system-level protections. Available benchmarks and defense methods are by now plentiful, yet to our knowledge no prior study compares multiple inference-time defenses on a uni-fied platform that spans different model families, model generations, and attack modalities. 4 B. # 4 Defense Methods 

We evaluate three inference-time defense methods (RapGuard, AdaShield, SmoothVLM) and, for context, sketch two further output-level approaches (MLLM-Protector, ECSO). The methods act at different stages of the inference pipeline. 

4.1 Prompt-Level Defenses RapGuard [10] builds its defensive prompt on the fly. Given a query (xi, x t), the model first produces a safety rationale through chain-of-thought reasoning [30]. That rationale then drives an adaptive prompt, which is appended to the query. A self-check mechanism fires the defense only when the rationale flags potential risk, which spares benign queries the extra overhead. Limitation: it leans on the model’s ability to produce correct rationales. The original authors report up to 

2× latency in the worst case [10], although in our experiments the overhead is much lower (Table 7). Inference-Time Defenses for MLLMs 5

AdaShield [29] goes after structure-based attacks that hide text in images. The static variant (AdaShield-S) issues a multi-step instruction for systematic image inspection. The adaptive variant (AdaShield-A) uses an auxiliary model to iteratively generate defensive prompts over representative attacks, building a prompt bank; at inference time the best prompt is picked by similarity matching. Limitation: it needs a pre-compiled attack set and generalizes poorly to unseen attack patterns. 

4.2 Image-Level Defense SmoothVLM [27] carries randomized smoothing [5] over to MLLMs. It masks pixels at random, zeroing each pixel with probability p. From N masked versions, the model is run on each and the responses are aggregated by majority voting. In its certified formulation the method offers probabilistic safety guarantees under certain assumptions (e.g., bounded patch size); the practical instantiation we evaluate here ( N =5 , p=0 .5) carries no formal certification. Limitation: N -fold computational overhead, plus ineffectiveness once the entire image (rather than a localized patch) carries the harmful information. 

4.3 Output-Level Defenses (Not Evaluated) 

Two further output-level methods were left out of our experiments because they require additional trained components. We note them here for context. 

MLLM-Protector [22] pairs a post-generation toxicity detector with a detoxifier model to regenerate unsafe outputs. Both components have to be trained on representative toxic data, which limits applicability to new attack types. 

ECSO (Eyes Closed, Safety On) [9] swaps the image for a textual description once it detects an unsafe draft response. This activates the model’s text-level RLHF safety mechanisms, at the cost of discarding visual information.

5.2 Benchmarks 

Table 1 summarizes the seven safety benchmarks used. Together the benchmarks span four attack classes: typographic injection (vi-sual text embedding), textual jailbreak (text-only adversarial prompts), mul-timodal jailbreak (combined visual-textual attacks), and adversarial patches (gradient-optimized image perturbations). We generated the adversarial patch samples by PGD-based optimization ( L∞, ϵ = 16 /255 , 100 iterations, step size 

α = 1 /255 ) against the CLIP visual encoder of InternVL2.5-8B-MPO, applied to 500 base images from COCO [12] paired with harmful prompts from HarmBench, following the protocol of Qi et al. [24]. 

Patch transfer limitation. We did not separately validate transfer of these patches to the other evaluated models. The Qwen-VL family in particular uses a different visual encoder architecture, so the observed ASR = 0 .0% on Qwen3-VL models (Table 3) may reflect poor cross-architecture transfer rather than genuine robustness. The patch benchmark results should therefore be read as measur-ing defense effectiveness under one specific transfer scenario, not as absolute robustness measures. We measure defense effectiveness on this diverse set, and the cross-benchmark results support the hypothesis that single-benchmark evaluation is insufficient. 

5.3 Safety Classification 

A unified keyword-based proxy classifier labels each response safe or harmful by checking for predefined harmful-content and refusal indicators, in the spirit of the classification approach of MM-SafetyBench [14]. We rely on this proxy classi-fier for cross-benchmark comparability. It is not a gold-standard or benchmark-native evaluation; it is a consistent proxy that buys fair comparison across het-erogeneous benchmarks at the price of some evaluation fidelity. A unified pro-tocol of this kind makes cross-benchmark and cross-defense comparison fair, but it necessarily sacrifices fidelity to individual benchmark-native evaluation schemes (e.g., HarmBench’s LLM-based judge). We chose comparability over Inference-Time Defenses for MLLMs 7

fidelity deliberately, because our central research question concerns relative de-fense effectiveness across conditions rather than absolute safety measurement on any single benchmark. For each model response, the classifier assigns a binary la-bel (safe/harmful) according to whether the response contains harmful-content keywords (e.g., step-by-step compliance phrases) or refusal indicators (e.g., “I cannot,” “against my guidelines”). One and the same classifier runs across all benchmarks and defense configurations, which is what keeps the comparison meaningful. We checked the classifier on 200 randomly sampled responses (100 safe, 100 harmful), comparing the keyword-based labels against the full model out-puts, and found no misclassifications. This spot-check is not independent vali-dation against human annotation or an LLM-based judge, and the keyword ap-proach may still miss semantically harmful responses that dodge trigger phrases (e.g., euphemistic compliance or circumlocutory instructions). With 200 samples and zero observed errors, the rule-of-three gives a 95% upper confidence bound of 1.5% error rate, which would touch at most ∼135 of the roughly 9,000 evaluated responses. We did not cross-validate the keyword classifier against the native evaluation protocols of individual benchmarks (e.g., HarmBench’s LLM-based judge), so systematic discrepancies on specific benchmarks cannot be ruled out. Since keyword classifiers tend to under-count semantically harmful responses that avoid trigger phrases, reported HR values may run slightly optimistic: ac-tual safety could be lower than measured. 

5.4 Infrastructure and Reproducibility 

We ran the primary experiments on NVIDIA B200 (179 GB VRAM) and the cross-benchmark validation on NVIDIA H100 NVL (94 GB VRAM). All models were served through vLLM v0.18 with PyTorch 2.7 in bfloat16 precision and no quantization. Greedy decoding (temperature = 0 ) was used throughout, which makes the results deterministic over fixed benchmark samples. Bootstrap 95% confidence intervals (10,000 resamples) on harmless rates for Tables 2–3 yield CI widths typically below 0.03, with a maximum of 0.046 for smaller benchmark subsets ( N =500 ); degenerate cases where HR ≈ 1.0 produce CI widths near zero. These CIs cover the harmless-rate metric only, not the over-refusal or latency metrics.

On the primary benchmark we also evaluate a simple safety prompt (SP) baseline: a short instruction prepended to the query reading “You are a helpful AI assistant. Vulnerability profiles look model- and attack-specific, and the cross-benchmark data hints at further patterns. 

Safety on one benchmark does not guarantee robustness. Both Qwen3-VL models reach HR ≥ 0.998 on MM-SafetyBench, yet only 0.901 on JailBreakV-28K (Table 3): a 9.9% attack success rate that single-benchmark evaluation would simply miss. Qwen3-VL-8B shows the same gap, HR = 0 .998 on typo-graphic attacks against 0.901 on textual jailbreaks. Our experiments do not pin down the mechanism behind this discrepancy. 

Different attack classes expose different weaknesses. Typographic attacks bite InternVL hardest (ASR up to 20 .5% ), whereas textual jailbreaks hit Qwen3-VL more strongly (ASR up to 9.9% ). Adversarial patches expose InternVL3.5-8B (ASR = 22 .2% ) but do nothing against Qwen3-VL (ASR =0.0% , likely from poor cross-architecture transfer of patches optimized for CLIP; see Section 5). Since the weaknesses shift with attack class, a single-benchmark evaluation can understate risk. 

Defenses can degrade safety on specific benchmarks. The underlined values in Table 3 show that the full combination can push HR below the unde-fended baseline on individual benchmarks. The sharpest case is InternVL2.5-8B-MPO on HarmBench, where the full combination cuts HR from 0.975 to 0.850 

(−12 .5 pp). Per-defense analysis ties most of the degradation on this bench-mark to RapGuard alone, which points to negative interactions between chain-of-thought-based defenses and this model’s native safety behavior on direct harmful-behavior requests. 

SmoothVLM-alone on adversarial patches. SmoothVLM was designed specifically to fend off adversarial perturbations through randomized smooth-ing, so we evaluate it on its own on the adversarial patch benchmark. To gauge the utility cost of each defense, we measure over-refusal on 500 legiti-mate visual question-answering queries from MMBench across all eight models. Since SmoothVLM perturbs the image and re-queries the model N = 5 times, we evaluate its over-refusal both in isolation and within the full combination (S+A+R). The full combination (S+A+R) turns away 97.0–100% of benign requests across all eight models, which leaves the defended system unusable for legitimate queries in this configuration. SmoothVLM on its own is just as destructive: its majority-vote mechanism brands 99.2–100% of benign queries as refusals across all eight evaluated models. Three things suggest this stems from the perturba-tion itself rather than an implementation artifact. The behavior holds across both model families and all model sizes. The non-perturbed baseline runs the same inference pipeline yet stays at OR ≤ 1.8% . And the original SmoothVLM paper [27] reports utility only on adversarial inputs, never on a benign distribu-tion. Even so, we cannot rule out that alternative SmoothVLM hyperparameters (N , p) or a different aggregation strategy would bring over-refusal down; the values used here ( N =5 , p=0 .5) follow the original paper’s defaults. The Simple Prompt (SP) holds utility well. Six of eight models post SP over-refusal of 0.0–7.0%, and three Qwen models sit at 0.0–0.2%. The outliers are InternVL2.5-8B-MPO and InternVL3.5-8B, at 18.2% and 15.6% respectively. AdaShield alone gives moderate over-refusal (0.0–16.8%), with most models un-der 8%. RapGuard alone runs far higher: 18.8–78.8% across models, with a wide spread (InternVL2.5-4B at 18.8% against Qwen2.5-VL-7B at 78.8%). One reading is that RapGuard’s chain-of-thought safety rationale fires frequent false positives on benign content, the severity depending heavily on the target model. These over-refusal rates make concrete the tradeoff formalized in Eq. (2): the configuration with the broadest safety coverage also yields the highest over-refusal, so practical deployment has to weigh safety gains against the risk of badly cutting utility for legitimate users. 6.5 Latency 

Table 7 reports per-query latency overhead on NVIDIA B200, computed as the mean additional wall-clock time over 195 queries from MMBench (after dropping 5 warm-up queries from the same set) relative to the undefended baseline for each model size range. SmoothVLM carries the highest overhead because of its N -fold inference, while the prompt-level methods (AdaShield, RapGuard) add comparatively mod-est latency. One caveat: Table 7 reports per-query pipeline overhead , the extra computation the defense step introduces, measured as the per-sample amortized wall-clock time of the defense transformation in a batched processing pipeline. End-to-end single-query latency, which folds in model generation time, is much higher. Profiling on Qwen2.5-VL-7B (195 queries) gives a mean total latency of 1,810 ms without defense against 9,730 ms with SmoothVLM ( 5.4× baseline), which confirms that N -fold inference dominates the total cost. 

6.6 Preliminary Whitebox Evaluation 

Every attack in Sections 6.1–6.5 is non-adaptive : it is generated with no knowl-edge of the defense pipeline. For a preliminary look at robustness under an adaptive adversary, we run a gradient-based whitebox evaluation on two repre-sentative models, using PGD through the vision encoder [16] ( L∞, ϵ = 16 /255 ,100 steps, step size α = 1 /255 ). Twenty images from MM-SafetyBench are per-turbed to maximise the probability of harmful completion, and each perturbed image is then evaluated under four conditions: no defense, AdaShield, RapGuard, and AdaShield+RapGuard (combo). under AdaShield, and drops to 0% under RapGuard and the combo. PGD opti-mizes a perturbation in the continuous pixel space, but the evaluated text-level defenses act on the model’s textual output. # 7 Discussion 

A reminder before the discussion: all safety metrics below come from the unified keyword-based proxy classifier of Section 5, not from benchmark-native or human evaluation. The results expose a persistent tradeoff in MLLM safety. No single defense and no fixed combination managed high safety, low over-refusal, and low latency at once across all evaluated settings. In our evaluation, defense effectiveness runs inversely to baseline model safety. Models with lower baseline safety (HR < 0.85 ) benefit substantially from Rap-Guard. Models with higher baseline safety (HR > 0.99 ) can instead be harmed by added defenses. The SmoothVLM-alone results on the adversarial patch benchmark—the very attack class it was designed for—follow a similar pattern. SmoothVLM degrades safety for two of eight models: InternVL2.5-4B (HR :0.920 → 0.890 , −3.0 pp) and Qwen2.5-VL-7B ( 0.892 → 0.882 , −1.0 pp). For the rest of the models with non-trivial baseline ASR, SmoothVLM improves safety (e.g., Qwen2.5-VL-32B: 0.850 → 0.936 , +8 .6 pp). One possible explana-tion is that pixel-level randomization disrupts a model’s baseline refusal behavior when image regions are masked. This explanation stays speculative, since only a handful of models show the pattern. Either way, the result reinforces the same point: defense effectiveness is model-dependent. Stacking defenses brings a sepa-rate problem of its own. Combining all defenses produces very high over-refusal (97.0–100% on 500 evaluated benign queries) with no proportional safety gain. SmoothVLM alone is likewise unusable here, at 99.2–100% false refusal across all eight models. RapGuard alone runs at 18.8–78.8% over-refusal depending on the model, so even one defense can sharply reduce utility. The minimax objective in Eq. (1) optimizes worst-case safety, and the full combination approximates it trivially by refusing every query, scoring high HR at the cost of utility. Without an explicit utility constraint, then, the formulation, useful as it is conceptually, cannot tell genuine safety apart from indiscriminate refusal. The model- and attack-dependent variation seen across our experimental setup is exactly what motivates adaptive selection mechanisms that pick among defenses from properties of the target model and the incoming query. Practical observations. Drawing on our evaluation of eight models from the InternVL and Qwen-VL families across seven benchmarks, we offer the fol-lowing observations. They are not general deployment rules: (i) a simple safety prompt (SP) gives moderate safety gains at minimal over-refusal (0.0–18.2%, five of eight models below 7%) and serves as a useful lightweight baseline in the evaluated setup; (ii) AdaShield adds further safety with tolerable over-refusal (0.0–16.8%, mostly below 10.4%), which makes it a plausible second layer for the model families tested here; (iii) SmoothVLM does not suit benign traffic at the default hyperparameters tested ( N =5 , p=0 .5), though other configurations may behave differently; (iv) the full combination (S+A+R) is unfit for deployment in the tested direct-combination configuration (97–100% over-refusal); (v) in the evaluated setup, defense effectiveness is model-specific: a configuration that im-proves safety for one model family (e.g., RapGuard for InternVL) may degrade it for another (e.g., Qwen3-VL). 

Generalizability. Our evaluation covers two model families (InternVL, Qwen-VL). The defense methods themselves are broadly architecture-agnostic, in that they touch inputs and outputs rather than model internals. Whether the specific tradeoffs and performance patterns observed here carry over to other architec-tures (LLaVA, Phi-Vision, Gemma) remains to be tested. 

Limitations. We did not experimentally evaluate MLLM-Protector and ECSO, since both need additional trained components. The over-refusal eval-uation covers 500 queries from MMBench across all eight models and six defense configurations (None, SP, AdaShield, SmoothVLM, RapGuard, full combina-tion). That is well beyond a preliminary probe, yet it may not capture the full 16 B. Nutfullin, V. Evgrafov, D. Namiot

As noted in Section 5, the adversarial patches target a single visual encoder (CLIP in InternVL2.5-8B-MPO), and the observed ASR = 0 .0% on Qwen3-VL models may reflect poor cross-architecture trans-fer rather than genuine robustness. The primary evaluation (Sections 6.1–6.5) uses non-adaptive attacks generated with no knowledge of the defense pipeline. The preliminary whitebox evaluation (Section 6.6) gives initial evidence that text-level defenses suppressed gradient-based visual attacks in the tested con-figuration, but covers only two models and a single PGD configuration ( n=20

# 8 Conclusion 

We have presented a comparative evaluation of three inference-time defense methods and their combinations on eight multimodal large language models across seven safety benchmarks. As scored by the unified proxy classifier, five findings stand out. First, in our experimental setup no single defense domi-nates across all evaluated settings, because effectiveness hinges on the model’s baseline safety level and the attack type. Second, combining defenses directly causes severe over-refusal on benign queries (97–100% across all eight models), and SmoothVLM alone causes 99.2–100%. Third, a simple safety prompt yields moderate safety improvement while keeping utility intact (0.0–18.2% over-refusal across all eight models, five below 7%, though two exceeded 15%). Fourth, across the evaluated models and benchmarks, different attack classes expose different vulnerability profiles, which backs the case that multi-benchmark evaluation is needed. Fifth, in a preliminary whitebox PGD test on two models ( n=20 , one informative), text-level defenses suppressed a gradient-based visual attack that had succeeded without defense (ASR : 25% → 0% ), since the defenses act at the output stage where pixel-space gradient optimization has limited direct leverage in the tested configuration. Read together, these results push toward future work on adaptive defense selection rather than reliance on a single fixed defense pipeline. The cross-benchmark data gathered here indicate that different model–attack combinations may call for different defense configurations. We leave the design and evaluation of such selection mechanisms to future work, since they demand additional ex-periments on selection overhead, stability, and robustness to adaptive attacks. Beyond the empirical comparison, the unified evaluation protocol and the cross-model defense profiles reported here can help guide inference-time defense design for multimodal models. The per-model and per-attack-type measurements give practitioners a way to narrow the choice of defenses for a target deployment. The recurring patterns we identify, such as severe over-refusal under direct com-bination of defenses and a moderate utility cost under safety prompting, offer practical guidance but still need deployment-specific validation.
