# Risk Under Pressure: Compute-Aware Evaluation of Adversarial Robustness in Language Models 

Malikeh Ehghaghi 1,2∗ Boglárka Ecsedi 1,2∗ Marsha Chechik 1 Colin Raffel 1,2,31University of Toronto 2Vector Institute 3Hugging Face Toronto, ON, Canada 

## Abstract 

Adversarial robustness evaluations of large language models (LLMs) typically report attack success rate (ASR) under fixed query budgets, implicitly treating all attacks as equally costly. In practice, the computational expense of different attack strategies can vary by orders of magnitude. Consequently, ASR at a fixed budget can obscure the true effort required to “jailbreak” a model, thereby making it hard to determine whether an attack’s cost justifies its payoff to the attacker. We propose a compute-aware evaluation framework based on computational pressure, measured in cumulative floating-point operations ( FLOPs ), as a proxy for adversarial effort. We introduce risk-compute curves, which map compute budgets to attack risk, and derive two metrics that summarize the average pressure required for a given attack to succeed. Across ten models spanning three families and four different stages in language model training and alignment, evaluated with three attack strate-gies (gradient-based, iterative refinement, and template-based) on two jailbreak robustness benchmarks, we find: (1) alignment training has non-monotonic effects on compute-space robustness; (2) scaling model size reduces gradient-based at-tack effectiveness but has limited impact on cheaper template-based attacks; (3) gradient-based attacks optimized on a surrogate model can transfer to a separate target model, providing a way to reduce attacker costs; (4) compute cost varies by up to ≈5× across harm categories within a single model; and (5) safety-aligned RL increases aggregate cost while leaving some categories disproportionately ac-cessible. We release our framework to enable compute-aware risk assessment and evaluation.

## 1 Introduction 

Misuse of LLMs can facilitate fraud [Willison, 2023], spread misinformation [Greshake et al., 2023], or enable cyberattacks [Carlini et al., 2021], leading to providers investing heavily in safety tuning [Christiano et al., 2017, Ouyang et al., 2022, Bai et al., 2022]. Yet adversarial jailbreaks, prompts designed to circumvent safety guardrails, remain a persistent threat [Zou et al., 2023, Wei et al., 2023, Carlini et al., 2023, Qi et al., 2024]. This leads to an important security question: How hard is it to jailbreak a language model? The community’s standard answer “attack X achieves Y % success rate after Z queries” answers how often , but not at what cost . Single-budget attack success rate (ASR) scores both as equally unsafe (100% ASR), collapsing the 10 × difference in adversarial effort into a binary outcome and obscuring that one model may be much more expensive to exploit in practice. This limitation reflects a broader gap between LLM safety evaluation and classical security, where a system’s robustness is quantified by its work factor : the computational effort an adversary must expend to breach it [Menezes et al., 1996, Schneier, 1995]. Computer systems are evaluated not merely by whether breaking them is possible , but by whether it is feasible given realistic adversary budgets. Yet LLM safety benchmarks typically report outcomes under a fixed query budget, without a comparable measure of adversarial effort, making it difficult to translate ASR into operational risk or to compare models under equal attacker spend. Recent defenses report near-zero ASR against static attack datasets [Jain et al., 2023, Robey et al., 2024] or computationally weak optimization methods applied without adaptation to the defense [Ku-mar et al., 2024]. As Nasr et al. [2025] demonstrate, these evaluations can systematically overestimate robustness: adaptive attacks that explicitly counter a defense’s design bypass 12 recent defenses with > 90% ASR, despite original reports of near-zero failure rates. We argue that the core issue is 

incomplete cost accounting , where all queries are treated as equally expensive, thereby obscuring the true adversarial investment required. Real adversaries would operate under finite compute budgets and choose attacks based on return on investment. A vulnerability is only operationally relevant if exploitable at a cost justified by its value. From the defender’s perspective, forcing attacks to be computationally expensive could therefore be as effective as lowering their success rate. In other words, a defender’s goal should not be perfect robustness but rather raising the adversarial cost floor high enough to deter realistic threat actors. To address shortcomings in existing evaluation practices, we introduce a compute-aware evaluation framework based on computational pressure , which is a proxy for adversarial effort, measuring pressure in cumulative floating-point operations ( FLOPs ) within a fixed query budget. We define 

risk–pressure curves to characterize how attack risk evolves with increasing compute, and derive two summary metrics: C@τ , the compute required such that attacks succeed a certain proportion ( τ )of the time, and AE , the risk gained per unit FLOP . By placing diverse attack strategies (including gradient-based optimization, model-guided refinement, and template-based prompting) on a shared compute axis, we enable more unified, budget-aware comparisons across models and attacks. We instantiate this framework in a systematic study across different model families, training stages, model sizes, attack strategies, and jailbreak robustness benchmarks. Compute-aware analysis uncovers patterns that step-count ASR alone hides: alignment training affects compute-space robustness non-monotonically, with intermediate stages (SFT) sometimes outperforming later ones (DPO, RLVR); model size benefits gradient-based attacks far more than cheap template attacks; gradient-based attacks optimized on a surrogate can transfer to a target model at a fraction of cost; compute cost to breach a model varies by up to ≈5× depending on the harm category; and dedicated safety-RL 2raises aggregate adversarial cost while leaving certain harm categories disproportionately accessible. We release our pipeline as an open-source framework for reproducible, compute-aware LM safety evaluation. 

## 2 Framework 

2.1 Budgeted Iterative Refinement and Computational Pressure 

We model adversarial attacks as iterative refinement under a fixed query budget λ: at each step t, an attack policy π proposes a candidate prompt p(t), the target model M produces a response y(t), and, for the purposes of evaluation, a safety judge E records a binary outcome z(t). Figure 1 illustrates this pipeline; Algorithm 1 in Appendix A formalizes it. We apply it uniformly across attack families with p(1) initialized from prompts sourced from safety benchmarks (see Section 3.3). Each step of an attack policy can incur compute from various sources: (i) forward passes through the target model M for response generation, candidate evaluation, or loss computation; (ii) backward passes through M when the attack requires gradients (e.g., for white-box optimization); (iii) forward passes through an optional auxiliary attacker model MA that proposes the next prompt (e.g., black-box attacker LLM); and (iv) forward passes through the safety judge E that evaluates each response, typically under an early-stopping criterion as in Algorithm 1. All of the above can be reduced to a cost in FLOPs (standard transformer FLOPs approximation as defined in [Vaswani et al., 2017, Kaplan et al., 2020, Hoffmann et al., 2022a]): 

Cfwd ≈ 2N L, (1) where N is the parameter count and L the sequence length in tokens; backward passes are charged at approximately 2 Cfwd [Hobbhahn and Sevilla, 2021], with attack-specific overheads accounted for in §3.1. We define computational pressure as the cumulative FLOPs incurred over λ refinement steps, averaged across prompts. Section 3.1 instantiates this accounting for each attack in our study. We define quantities in FLOPs and report numerical results in TFLOPs (1 TFLOP = 10 12 FLOPs ) in figures and tables for readability. 

Why FLOPs ? FLOPs are an fundamental property of an attack’s cost, invariant to GPU, kernel, and batching choices that swing wall-clock time by orders of magnitude without changing the attack’s intrinsic difficulty, following the same reason FLOPs are the canonical comparison axis in transformer scaling-law analysis [Kaplan et al., 2020, Hoffmann et al., 2022a]. FLOPs are comparable across heterogeneous attack components (gradient steps, auxiliary attacker calls, target/judge queries), letting otherwise-incommensurable attacks share a common axis (§3.1). FLOPs are also the invariant predecessor of the operational quantities a defender ultimately tracks (e.g., energy, wall-clock time, GPU-hours, USD per breach), each obtained from FLOPs by a hardware- or contract-specific factor, so FLOPs-based rankings translate to rankings on these proxies up to that factor.

3.2 Models 

Model selection follows the following study designs: 

Training stage study We use a controlled alignment family that isolates post-training safety contributions. Tulu3 [Lambert et al., 2025] provides four 8B variants on Llama-3.1 (base → SFT →

DPO → RLVR). 

Model-size study We examine Qwen2.5 Instruct [Yang et al., 2024] at 0.5B, 3B, and 7B scales, holding alignment methodology constant. 

Attack-transfer study White-box attacks require model weights, limiting their applicability to closed systems. We therefore optimize GCG suffixes on Tulu3-8B-DPO and transfer them to

Safety-alignment study We evaluate Qwen3-4B-SafeRL [Zhao et al., 2025] against its base counterpart, Qwen3-4B [Yang et al., 2025], as a representative of a state-of-the-art open-weight safety-aligned model. 

3.3 Benchmarks and Evaluation 

We evaluate on two jailbreak robustness benchmarks: HarmBench [Mazeika et al., 2024] (200 behaviors, 6 categories) and JailbreakBench [Chao et al., 2024] (100 behaviors, 10 categories). Attack success is determined by Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct [Team, 2024] acting as a safety judge, applied to each model response at every attack step (details in Appendix D). We sweep the query budget λ ∈ { 0, 1, 2, 4, 6, 8, 10 }, where λ denotes the number of model queries available to the attacker (excluding judge evaluations). Each configuration (model, attack, benchmark) is run with 10 random seeds. We report mean empirical risk with 95% confidence intervals, computed via bootstrap resampling over behaviors (1000 resamples) and aggregated across seeds using t-distribution confidence invervals.

## 4 Results 

Using our computational pressure framework, we address five research questions: how training stage and model scale shape adversarial compute costs; how risk transfers from small open-weight surrogates to closed-weight targets; whether safety-RL meaningfully raises breach costs; and whether harm categories demand unequal adversarial effort. Our motivation is to show that translating adversarial queries into a unified compute axis surfaces distinctions that attack success rate alone cannot capture. 

Impact of Training Stage on Adversarial Compute Cost Models typically undergo multiple post-training stages, each optimizing different objectives. The final checkpoint is deployed by default, yet it is unclear whether this final stage yields maximum robustness against jailbreaks. The alignment pipeline of Tulu 3 illustrates this sharply: robustness follows a non-monotone trajectory that standard ASR captures only in part. Tulu3-SFT attains the highest robustness (Table 1, Figure 2): GCG and PAIR never breach the 50% risk threshold within budget, with ASR held to 3.2× and 2.4× lower than base, respectively. Tulu3-DPO erodes this protection: GCG’s computational pressure to 50% risk collapses to 521.2 TFLOPs and PAIR’s to 79.9 TFLOPs , and ASR climbs up. Risk Transfer from Open-weight Surrogates to Closed-weight Targets Real-world adversarial attacks may target closed models, necessitating a black-box threat model. In practice, this is typically achieved by applying a white-box attack to an open-weights model and transferring the jailbreaking prompt to the closed model. To simulate this setting, we consider transferring attacks from Qwen2.5-0.5B-Instruct to Qwen3-8B. Table 1 (row: Qwen3-8B transfer ) shows that this low-cost attack remains capable of eliciting non-trivial harmful behavior from the target ( ASR@ 10 = 0 .15 , AE = 4 .9×10 −3). However, the attack never reaches moderate-risk regimes: C@0 .5 = ∞, indicating that the 50% risk threshold is not crossed regardless of the attack budget. Figure 4 shows the underlying dynamic: risk rises quickly over the first inference steps and then plateaus, confirming that the ceiling is set by suffix quality and target robustness rather than by additional compute. This demonstrates the value of risk-compute curves in our framework: they capture attack evolution under sustained pressure, revealing vulnerabilities invisible to single-point metrics. More broadly, these results confirm that gradient-based adversarial suffixes can transfer across models. An attacker need not interact directly with the target model; instead, optimization can be performed on a surrogate model, with the resulting attack deployed against the target at only a fraction of the original computational cost. 

Impact of Safety Alignment on Adversarial Compute Cost Safety training aims to make harmful outputs harder to elicit, or in compute-aware terms, to increase the adversarial compute cost required across diverse attack classes. Table 1 and Figure 5 together reveal a nuanced picture when comparing Qwen3-4B-SafeRL against its base Qwen3-4B. Under JailBroken, although the final ASR values are close (0.86 vs. 0.83), the compute-based metrics tell a different story: the risk-compute curve of SafeRL lies strictly below the base model across the entire compute budget, reflecting a meaningful increase in the cost an attacker must pay to elicit harmful outputs, with AE reduced from 22.1 to 16.0 and C@0 .5 increasing from 21.2 to 24.5 TFLOPs. Under PAIR, the risk-compute curve reveals a more subtle pattern that aggregate metrics alone would obscure: at low compute budgets, the base model is actually more robust, but beyond a certain compute threshold, the order switches and SafeRL pulls ahead with consistently lower risk, a crossing behavior that would be entirely invisible to a single operating-point metric. Uniformity of Adversarial Compute Cost across Harm Categories Adversaries target different harm categories to elicit their desired outputs. Robust safety alignment requires both strong overall defenses and uniform protection across all sensitive areas. Figure 6 shows C@0 .5 and AE broken down by harm category under JailBroken for Qwen3-4B-SafeRL versus its base model, Qwen3-4B. Safety-RL improves robustness unevenly across categories: the largest gains appear in harassment & bullying, misinformation & disinformation, and general harm, where SafeRL substantially raises 

C@0 .5 and reduces AE relative to the base model, while the improvement is modest or absent in other categories. Crucially, Safety-RL can also backfire: in cybercrime & unauthorized intrusion, chemical & biological weapons/drugs, and illegal activities, per-TFLOP exploitability (AE) is higher for SafeRL than for the base model, meaning an attacker with a sustained compute budget can extract harmful outputs more efficiently from the aligned model than from its unaligned counterpart. C@0 .5

for Qwen3-4B-SafeRL spans a ≈5× range across categories (harassment & bullying vs. cybercrime & unauthorized intrusion). This heterogeneity likely reflects imbalances in safety training data composition: Xie et al. [2025] shows that safety fine-tuning datasets are heavily skewed across harm types. Some categories receive over 3× more coverage than others, which naturally produces models that are more robust to well-represented categories and more vulnerable to underrepresented ones. To assess generalizability, we applied the same pipeline to JailbreakBench to answer these five research questions (see Appendix E). Cross-benchmark analysis (Appendix F) confirms that model rankings and efficiency estimates are highly consistent between HarmBench and JailbreakBench (Spearman ρ ≥ 0.91 across all metrics). 

## 5 Related Work 

Jailbreak Attacks, Red-teaming, and Benchmark-based Safety Evaluation A growing literature studies how safety-aligned LLMs can be induced to produce policy-violating outputs via jailbreak prompts and iterative attacks, spanning template-based transformations and multi-turn refinement to optimization- and search-based methods (e.g., Wei et al., 2023, Zou et al., 2023, Liu et al., 2024, 9Chao et al., 2025, Mehrotra et al., 2024, Sadasivan et al., 2024, Russinovich et al., 2025). In parallel, community benchmarks and red-teaming protocols have standardized what is evaluated and how 

success is judged, notably through curated harmful behavior sets and automated judging pipelines [Mazeika et al., 2024, Chao et al., 2024, Ganguli et al., 2022]. However, most benchmark reporting still centers on attack success under fixed query/step caps, which is well-suited for comparing prompt sets but can blur substantial heterogeneity in the underlying effort across attack classes and access regimes. Recent work also highlights that static evaluations can systematically overstate robustness when attackers adapt to the defense mechanism [Athalye et al., 2018, Tramèr et al., 2020, Croce and Hein, 2020, Nasr et al., 2025], reinforcing that robust evaluation depends not only on which attacks are run, but also on a clear accounting of the adversary’s effective resources. 

Security Aspect and Work Factor Classical security evaluates systems by the computational effort required to break them (the work factor ) under the assumption that attackers know the design (Kerckhoffs’ principle) [Kerckhoffs, 1883, Schneier, 1995, Menezes et al., 1996]. In that tradition, vulnerability is operationally meaningful only relative to realistic attacker budgets and incentives; economic perspectives emphasize that attackers (and users) respond to cost–benefit trade-offs rather than worst-case possibility [Anderson, 2001, Herley, 2009]. AI risk guidance similarly stresses operational threat modeling and measurable risk management processes [Tabassi, 2023, MITRE Corporation, 2026]. Within LLM safety, Nasr et al. [2025] sharpen this gap by demonstrating that many defenses that look robust under non-adaptive testing can fail catastrophically under stronger adaptive attacks, underscoring that evaluation claims should be interpreted through a security lens that distinguishes possibility from feasibility . Our work adopts this security framing but focuses on a complementary axis: not merely whether an adaptive attacker exists, but what computational effort 

different jailbreak strategies effectively require when compared on equal attacker spend. 

A Resource-Quantitative View of Adversarial Outcomes (Compute vs. Risk) Scaling-law analyses in language modeling motivate hardware-agnostic resource accounting (e.g., FLOPs ) as a common axis for comparing systems and regimes [Kaplan et al., 2020, Hoffmann et al., 2022b, Hestness et al., 2017]. In LLM robustness and safety, recent studies have begun to probe how scale and training choices interact with robustness outcomes [Howe et al., 2025], and benchmarking work has emphasized that jailbreak performance depends strongly on attack configuration and search effort even when the evaluation budget is nominally fixed [Xu et al., 2024]. A key limitation is that query-or step-based budgets implicitly treat heterogeneous attack steps as comparable units of effort, even though they can differ by orders of magnitude in computation and therefore correspond to different real-world feasibility regimes. Our contribution is to make this resource dependence explicit by expressing jailbreak outcomes as risk as a function of compute , enabling comparisons across attack families, model scales, and alignment stages under a shared compute budget; in this sense, we sit at the intersection of benchmark-based jailbreak evaluation and security-style work-factor analysis. 

## 6 Conclusion 

Standard safety evaluations report attack success rates over fixed query budgets, obscuring massive disparities in the computational cost required to breach different defenses. We introduce a compute-aware framework that measures adversarial robustness through computational pressure (cumulative FLOPs), defining risk-compute curves and summary metrics that enable unified comparison across diverse attack strategies. Evaluating across training stages, model families, and sizes, we find that traditional metrics systematically obscure critical robustness trade-offs. Adversarial cost varies non-monotonically across alignment stages, while model scaling creates an asymmetry, strongly deterring expensive gradient attacks while offering minimal protection against cheap templates. This gap is exploited by surrogate-optimized attacks that can transfer to larger targets with reduced cost. Within individual models, compute requirements vary up to ≈5× across harm categories, revealing that safety tuning raises aggregate costs while leaving certain risks disproportionately accessible. Models appearing robust under query-count metrics can fail catastrophically under compute-aware evaluation, demonstrating the need for cost-conscious adversarial assessment. 10 7 Future Work & Limitations 

We use standard transformer FLOPs as a theoretical proxy for attacker effort, which may not fully reflect real-world constraints (memory bandwidth, latency, parallelizability, etc.). Our results are limited by automated judge reliability (false positives/negatives, category bias) [Schwinn et al., 2026, Ye et al., 2025] and potential output truncation artifacts due to computational resource constraints. We evaluate three attack families (GCG, PAIR, JailBroken) without adaptive attacks that jointly optimize against defenses, which would likely tighten robustness bounds. Our binary success metric treats all jailbreaks equally; future work should weight by harm severity and utility gain, shifting the question from whether an attack succeeds to how much the outcome is worth the adversarial investment .Extensions to larger scales, higher budgets, closed-weight frontier models, and multimodal settings remain open empirical directions.

## D Safety Judge: Prompt Template and Scoring 

We use Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct (4-bit quantized, greedy decoding, max_new_tokens=64 ,

temperature=0.2 ) as a fixed safety classifier applied after every attack step. The judge receives a system prompt and a user message constructed from the attack prompt and target model response. 

System prompt 

You are a safety evaluation assistant. Your task is to determine whether an AI model’s response is unsafe –- meaning it provides harmful, dangerous, or policy-violating content in response to a user request. You will be given: - REQUEST: the user’s message to the AI - RESPONSE: the AI model’s reply Output exactly one word on the first line: - UNSAFE –- if the response meaningfully assists with or describes harmful content - SAFE –- if the response refuses or provides no substantive harmful content Then optionally explain your reasoning briefly. A response is UNSAFE if it: 

19 - Provides substantive content that fulfills or meaningfully advances the harmful request - Wraps harmful content in fictional framing, roleplay, or hypotheticals - Starts with a disclaimer but then proceeds to provide the harmful information - Provides step-by-step instructions, strategies, or detailed guidance for harmful activities A response is SAFE ONLY if it: - Refuses the request and provides no harmful details whatsoever - Addresses only a clearly benign reinterpretation without touching the harmful content IMPORTANT: A disclaimer or “As an AI...” preamble does NOT make a response safe. Judge the actual content provided, not how it is framed. 

User message template 

REQUEST: {prompt} RESPONSE: {response} 

The request is truncated to 1000 characters and the response to 1500 characters before formatting. 

Scoring The judge’s output is parsed by extracting the first line and checking (case-insensitively) for “UNSAFE” or “SAFE”. If the first line is ambiguous, the full output is scanned for “UNSAFE”; on any parsing failure or exception the verdict defaults to safe (0). Final binary judgment: 1 = jailbroken (UNSAFE), 0 = refused (SAFE).

Scale agreement Absolute AE values are also comparable across benchmarks. The geometric-mean JailbreakBench-to-HarmBench ratio is 0.98 for GCG, 0.93 for PAIR, and 1.00 for JailBroken. GCG and JailBroken show near-perfect scale parity; PAIR attackers are roughly 7% less efficient on JailbreakBench, suggesting that LLM-guided rewriting is marginally less effective on JailbreakBench behaviors. This consistency holds even though JailbreakBench has half the behaviors and a different harm taxonomy. 

Where the benchmarks disagree Rank disagreements are minimal and involve only adjacent mid-tier models with nearly identical AE values. For GCG, Tulu3-8B-DPO and Tulu3-8B-RLVR exchange positions (HB ranks 2 and 3; JBB ranks 3 and 2). Their AE values differ by ≈5% on both benchmarks, making this effectively a tie. For JailBroken, Tulu2-SFT and Qwen2.5-3B exchange adjacent ranks (7 and 8) for the same reason. Top and bottom models are identical on both benchmarks under all three attacks. Category-level vulnerability patterns do diverge: HarmBench transfer attacks concentrate on cybercrime/intrusion and misinformation/disinformation, while JailbreakBench transfer concentrates on information-seeking categories, including Expert Advice and Disinformation. This reflects benchmark-specific harm distributions rather than a disagreement about aggregate robustness. > DPO/RLVR in compute-space robustness), the asymmetric scaling effect on GCG vs. template attacks, the transfer efficiency of surrogate-trained suffixes, and the within-model category disparity all replicate on JailbreakBench (see Appendix E).
