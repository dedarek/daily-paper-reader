Abstract 

Large language models (LLMs) are rigorously aligned to refuse harmful requests, a process that inherently cultivates a latent capacity to evaluate and recognize unsafe content. In this work, we reveal that this advanced safety awareness inadvertently introduces a fatal vul-nerability. We introduce Posterior Attack, a single-query jailbreak that bypasses guardrails by prompting the model to generate the exact harmful response its internal classifier would normally flag as unsafe. Through extensive em-pirical evaluation across 30 open-source LLMs (up to 35B parameters in size) and frontier mod-els (e.g., GPT-5, Claude 4.6), we observe a striking phenomenon: models with superior safety-judgment capabilities are disproportion-ately more susceptible to this exploitation. To explain this, we formalize the Safety Paradox, analytically showing that monotonic improve-ments in safety alignment naturally amplify posterior vulnerability. Finally, we establish a causal link via reinforcement learning interven-tions, exemplifying that artificially degrading a model’s safety judgment immunizes it against the attack, whereas enhancing judgment exacer-bates the vulnerability. Our findings highlight potential flaws in current alignment paradigms, indicating that defense mechanisms may re-quire further structural refinement. 1

However, from an adversarial perspective, if a model possesses the precise knowledge of what constitutes a harmful conversation, attackers might exploit this knowledge to reverse refusal and com-pliance states for bypassing behavioral boundaries (Wang et al., 2025a; Oozeer et al., 2025). This intuitive insight motivates the development of Posterior Attack , a novel, search-free, and single-query jailbreak method. Conventional attacks it-eratively attempt to maximize the marginal (prior) probability of tricking a model into directly out-putting harmful texts. Posterior Attack, however, targets the model’s evaluative capacity. As illus-trated in Figure 1, it shifts the attack surface to posterior simulation—prompting the model to gen-erate the exact response its internal safety classifier would flag as harmful. This forces the model to fulfill the malicious request under the guise of an objective classification task. Through extensive evaluation, we uncover a pervasive vulnerability across the LLM ecosys-tem. We empirically establish a strong propor-tional relationship between prompt-elicited safety-judgment accuracy and posterior attack success rates across 30 open-source LLMs. Alarmingly, the most advanced and highly aligned architec-tures—including the latest open-weight models like Qwen 3.6 (Qwen Team, 2026a) and Gemma 4, as well as frontier closed-source systems like GPT-5-Chat and Claude Sonnet 4.6—show an overwhelm-ing susceptibility to this exploitation, vastly outper-forming conventional baseline attacks. To explain these striking empirical observations, we formally formulate this phenomenon as the 

Safety Paradox . Using Bayesian inference, we ana-lytically show that the very mechanisms intended to secure the model provide the precise probabilis-tic signal required to subvert it. Simply put, as an LLM’s functional safety-judgment improves, it paradoxically becomes structurally more suscepti-ble to posterior exploitation. Finally, we experimentally verify this structural flaw through targeted reinforcement learning inter-ventions. By actively manipulating a model’s clas-sification accuracy via Group Relative Policy Opti-mization (GRPO) (Shao et al., 2024), we demon-strate that artificially degrading a model’s safety judgment effectively immunizes it against our at-tack, whereas enhancing its judgment drastically exacerbates the vulnerability. Our contributions are summarized as follows: • Methodologically, we propose Posterior At-tack, a highly effective, single-query jailbreak that bypasses alignment by weaponizing the model’s own internal safety classifier. • Empirically, we expose the pervasive nature of this vulnerability, demonstrating a strong correlation between safety-judgment accuracy and attack success rates across 30 diverse open-source LLMs and frontier models. • Analytically, we formulate a mathematical framework to explain these observations, for-malizing Safety Paradox to show that mono-tonic improvements in LLM safety awareness inherently amplify posterior vulnerability. • Causally, we validate the link between internal safety awareness and posterior vulnerability through reinforcement learning interventions, showing that modulating safety judgment pre-dictably controls the attack’s effectiveness.

3 Safety Paradox 

This section empirically validates the vulnerabil-ity of LLMs to Posterior Attack. Furthermore, by analyzing behavioral correlations across 30 LLMs, we observe the Safety Paradox : as an LLM’s func-tional safety judgment improves, it paradoxically becomes more susceptible to posterior exploitation. 

3.1 Setup Metrics We measure safety robustness using the Attack Success Rate (ASR) metric across 520 harm-ful behaviors from the AdvBench dataset (Zou et al., 2023). Furthermore, evaluating this ASR with the fully open-source HarmBench LLaMA-2-13B classifier (Mazeika et al., 2024) ensures re-producibility without dependence on closed-source APIs. Finally, we quantify internal safety aware-ness by assessing the accuracy of the models as bi-nary classifiers on 596 harmful and benign queries from HarmBench (Mazeika et al., 2024). 

Evaluated Models To comprehensively assess the correlation between safety awareness and Pos-terior ASR, we evaluate 30 open-source models spanning diverse architectures, parameter counts, and training paradigms. This includes the LLaMA (Touvron et al., 2023; Grattafiori et al., 2024a), Qwen (Qwen et al., 2025; Yang et al., 2025; Qwen Team, 2026a,b), Falcon (Team, 2024), Gemma (Team et al., 2024), and Mistral (Jiang et al., 2023) model families. For long-reasoning models, we em-ploy their standard, non-thinking mode by default. 

3.2 Analysis 

As illustrated in Figure 2, we observe a striking positive correlation between a model’s safety judge-ment accuracy and its vulnerability to Posterior At-tack, yielding Pearson and Spearman coefficients of 0.80 and 0.78 , respectively. Despite vast dif-ferences in training corpora and alignment algo-rithms, the overarching trend empirically estab-lishes the Safety Paradox: models highly adept at classifying harmful content are systematically more exploitable. Analyzing the bottom-left quad-rant, older or smaller architectures—such as the Llama 2 family, Llama 3.2 (1B and 3B), and Qwen 3 (0.6B)—exhibit limited internal safety judgement accuracy, clustering below 65% accuracy. Conse-quently, they inherently resist posterior exploita-tion, maintaining near-zero ASR. This paradox reaches its critical extreme among the most recent and capable frontier models, which strictly populate the top-right quadrant. It reveals an evolutionary trajectory: newly released, larger architectures such as the Qwen 3.6 and Gemma 4 families achieve state-of-the-art safety judgment, frequently exceeding 85% accuracy. However, this sharpened evaluative capacity renders them signifi-cantly susceptible, with ASRs consistently surging 3past 80% and approaching near-100% exploitabil-ity. Ultimately, this consistent diagonal shift across the open-source ecosystem statistically and visu-ally confirms that progressively rigorous alignment paradigms inadvertently establish a profound secu-rity loophole, necessitating a thorough rethinking of current LLM defense mechanisms.

These formalizations yield two critical insights: First, the monotonic-ity of B with respect to both p and J reveals a fundamental tension in alignment: as a model’s internal Safety Awareness becomes sharper, the at-tack paradoxically becomes more potent. Stronger judgment directly amplifies posterior vulnerability. Second, the asymptotic behavior solidifies this paradox. As a model approaches perfect safety judgment ( J → ∞ ), attack effectiveness converges to absolute certainty ( B → 1). This implies that an ideally aligned model—one that perfectly dis-criminates between benign and harmful queries—is maximally exploitable under this posterior formula-tion. Conversely, in a model with negligible Safety Awareness ( J ↓ 0), attack effectiveness collapses, scaling only linearly with J.Ultimately, these insights suggest that the mech-anisms intended to secure the model provide the precise informational signal required to subvert it. TPR /FPR ) and posterior ASR, we naturally expect a strong proportional correlation between J and overall safety judgement accuracy. Models that achieve higher classification accuracy inherently exhibit a higher TPR and a lower FPR, driving a larger J. Therefore, this framework consequently explains the relationship observed between safety judgement accuracy and posterior ASR. 

5 Safety Awareness Manipulation 

This section verifies logical insights of the Safety Paradox. By actively manipulating models’ inter-4nal Safety Awareness via reinforcement learning, we establish a causal link to their posterior ASR. 

5.1 Setup Benchmarks Building upon the ASR metric computation detailed in Section 3.1, we evaluate model safety under two conditions: refusal behav-ior via direct prompting ( Direct ASR) and vulnera-bility to our proposed method ( Posterior ASR). To quantify safety judgement accuracy, we again eval-uate the models as binary classifiers on 596 harmful and benign queries from HarmBench ( HB-CLS )(Mazeika et al., 2024). Furthermore, to ensure in-terventions do not degrade base capabilities, we evaluate general utility via zero-shot accuracy with greedy decoding on GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021) and MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2021). 

Fine-Tuning Setup To causally verify that Pos-terior Attack relies on internal safety awareness, we implement two targeted reinforcement learning paradigms: Safety Awareness Improvement (SAI) and Safety Awareness Degradation (SAD). We construct a balanced training corpus of 4,096 (prompt, response) pairs from the Wild-GuardTrain dataset (Han et al., 2024). This sub-set covers diverse threats, containing both be-nign and harmful queries paired with refusal or compliance responses. We optimize models us-ing Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) (Shao et al., 2024). For SAI, the model re-ceives a reward of 1 for correctly classifying safety and 0 otherwise. For SAD, we assign a binary random reward ∈ {0, 1} to inject noise and degrade the classification subspace. We se-lect four diverse models exhibiting varying initial safety awareness and vulnerabilities: Qwen3-1.7B ,

Qwen2.5-3B-Instruct , Falcon3-7B-Instruct ,and Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct . Hyperparameters are detailed in Appendix C.

6 Posterior Attack to Frontier LLMs 

In this section, we extend our investigation to fron-tier LLMs, assessing their susceptibility to the Pos-terior Attack. We further examine the guardrails of these state-of-the-art models to explore viable defense mechanisms against this exploitation. 

6.1 Attack Performance Setup We evaluate Posterior Attack against 10 jailbreak baselines on 7 frontier LLMs. All target models are queried under identical configurations to ensure cross-model comparability. Reasoning models (GPT-5, Claude models) use low reasoning effort. We adopt AdvBench (Zou et al., 2023) and follow the LLM-as-judge protocol (Addepalli et al., 2025), using GPT-4o-mini to judge ASR. Further experiment details are in Appendix D. 

Overall Performance As shown in Table 2, Pos-terior Attack outperforms all evaluated baselines across frontier LLMs, achieving an unprecedented average ASR of 83.0%. This significantly exceeds the strongest baseline, CodeChameleon (71.9%), and eclipses subsequent baselines like ReNeLLM and FlipAttack by nearly +40%. This improvement stems largely from consistent effectiveness across closed-source models (ranging from 33.7% to 99.0%), and near-perfect exploitability on massive open-source models, recording 98.5% on Qwen3 235B and 99.2% on DeepSeek V3.2. Furthermore, as frontier models develop more advanced alignment, they become increasingly re-sistant to conventional attacks but highly vulnera-ble to Posterior Attack. For example, scaling from GPT-4o to GPT-5-Chat causes CodeChameleon and ReNeLLM success rates to plummet by ab-solute margins of 36.7% and 42.9%, respec-tively. Conversely, Posterior Attack surges ASR by +20.3%, rising from 78.7% to 99.0%. Similarly, transitioning from Claude 3.7 to 4.6 Sonnet drops CodeChameleon’s ASR from 94.0% to 30.4% and completely neutralizes FlipAttack (dropping from 60.8% to 0.0%), yet it actively exacerbates our attack, increasing its ASR from 78.3% to 93.7%. These overall trends across disparate model fami-lies conclusively demonstrate that advancements in safety awareness amplify posterior vulnerability.

Ultimately, while test-time scaling offers a viable shield for reasoning-capable models, secur-ing traditional LLMs against posterior exploitation remains an unresolved open challenge. 

7 Related Work 

Jailbreak Attacks Recent studies explore vari-ous methods to expose LLM vulnerabilities. White-box attacks like GCG (Zou et al., 2023) and Au-toDAN (Liu et al., 2024) rely on gradient-based or heuristic optimization to generate adversarial suffixes. Conversely, black-box methods such as PAIR (Chao et al., 2023) and RENeLLM (Ding et al., 2024) use attacker LLMs to iteratively refine prompts via rewriting and scenario nesting. Cipher-based methods like SelfCipher (Yuan et al., 2024) and CodeChameleon (Lv et al., 2024) embed instructions in implicit ciphers or benign code-completion tasks. ArtPrompt (Jiang et al., 2024) and FlipAttack (Liu et al., 2025b) mask trig-ger words using ASCII art or character flipping, exploiting visual-semantic blind spots and autore-gressive reconstruction to force execution. Addi-tionally, role-playing and in-context techniques like DeepInception (Li et al., 2023) use nested scenarios to disorient guardrails, while Many-shot Jailbreak-ing (Anil et al., 2024) floods context windows with harmful QA pairs to override alignment. However, existing jailbreaks face critical limita-tions: they require open weights and heavy compu-tation, are token-intensive, degrade model compre-hension via disguises, and mostly rely on empirical heuristics rather than theoretical grounding. 

Mechanistic Understanding of Refusal Com-plementing empirical jailbreaks, recent mechanis-tic interpretability research (Pan et al., 2025; Zou et al., 2025) decodes the internal dynamics of model refusal. Arditi et al. (2024) demonstrated that activation interventions along latent “refusal directions” can force malicious compliance or trig-ger benign refusals. Crucially, these refusal vectors are illustrated as agnostic across languages (Wang et al., 2025b), modalities (Wang et al., 2025a), and models (Oozeer et al., 2025). Wei et al. (2024) and Zhao et al. (2025) demon-strated that deactivating a sparse set of safety-critical neurons (<1% of total parameters) com-pletely dismantles model safety. Similarly, Li et al. (2025) isolated a small, contiguous set of middle layers crucial for distinguishing malicious queries, while Li and Kim (2025) showed that integrating an explicit binary classification task can effectively steer attention and safe decoding mechanisms. Yet, these internal safety boundaries remain fragile; downstream fine-tuning—even those aimed at opti-mizing multi-objective goals like helpfulness and robustness—can inadvertently warp them, leading to sudden alignment failures (Qi et al., 2024). While prior studies view internal safety classifi-cation as a defensive asset, our work fundamentally challenges this paradigm. Posterior Attack explic-itly weaponizes these mechanisms, showing that precise internal safety judgments directly induce vulnerabilities formalized in the Safety Paradox. 

8 Conclusion 

In this work, we uncover the Safety Paradox: rigor-ous safety alignment inadvertently weaponizes an LLM’s internal safety judgment, rendering highly aligned models fundamentally more vulnerable to exploitation. We demonstrate this through Poste-rior Attack, an efficient jailbreak that elicits harm-ful content by prompting models to generate ex-actly what their internal classifiers would flag. Ex-tensive evaluations across 30 open-source models and frontier systems, coupled with targeted safety-awareness manipulation experiments, confirm this vulnerability both empirically and causally. While deliberative reasoning provides a promising de-fense, non-reasoning models remain critically ex-posed. These findings expose a profound flaw in current alignment paradigms, necessitating a thor-ough reevaluation of LLM defense mechanisms. 8Limitations 

While the discovery of the Safety Paradox and the 

Posterior Attack exposes a critical vulnerability in current alignment paradigms, we acknowledge several constraints to our study. First, the important ASR is computed by LLMs such as HarmBench LLama-2-13B and GPT-4o-mini. Even though our analysis in Appendix B shows that these two LLMs have ∼90% agreement with human experts, potential errors might still occur with this LLM-based metric. Second, our evaluation focuses on the estab-lished safety benchmark AdvBench and models accessible as of early 2026. While these repre-sent a broad spectrum of safety training, the rapidly evolving landscape of proprietary “system-level” guardrails, which operate outside the model’s pri-mary completion logic, may offer auxiliary layers of defense not fully captured in this work. Third, our study is primarily conducted on English-language models and datasets. Given that safety alignment can vary significantly across lin-guistic and cultural contexts, further research is required to determine if the Safety Paradox mani-fests identically in low-resource languages. Finally, while we demonstrate that Deliberative Alignment (as seen in the GPT-OSS and GPT-5 families) provides a potential defense via test-time compute, the high computational latency of such methods currently limits their feasibility for high-throughput, real-time applications. We hope this work serves as a catalyst for developing more ef-ficient, “inherent” safety mechanisms that do not rely on the trade-offs of posterior judgement. 

Ethical Statement 

This research is conducted strictly for academic pur-poses, aimed at addressing underlying safety risks and enhancing the adversarial robustness of Large Language Models. By exposing structural limi-tations within current alignment paradigms, this work does not seek to prescribe specific ethical standards, but rather to illuminate the complex na-ture of safety alignment. By formally revealing the paradox inherent in modern guardrails, our ul-timate objective is to help mitigate the potential societal harms these systems may pose. Furthermore, we acknowledge the dual-use na-ture of our findings; the vulnerabilities and poste-rior prompts detailed in this study could theoreti-cally be repurposed by malicious actors. To foster defensive research, we will disseminate all experi-mental code, datasets, and prompts to the broader community, with the explicit intent that these re-sources will advance future red-teaming efforts and structural defense mechanisms.

C Safety Awareness Manipulation Details 

Dataset Construction We construct the Safety-Manipulation Dataset by adapting from the Wild-GuardTrain corpus (Han et al., 2024), which spans diverse harm categories including privacy, misin-formation, harmful languages, and malicious uses. The resulting dataset comprises 4,312 examples, split into 4,096 training and 216 validation samples. The prompt template for reinforcement learning is presented in Table 4. 

GRPO Training Setup. We optimize each model using GRPO (Shao et al., 2024), employing two reward schemes corresponding to the two ma-nipulation objectives. For SAI, the model receives a reward of 1 when its predicted label matches the ground-truth safety label, and 0 otherwise. For SAD, we assign a uniformly random binary re-ward r ∈ {0, 1}, decoupling the optimization signal from the true label and thereby introduc-ing noise into the safety-judgment subspace. For Qwen3-family models, the reasoning mode is dis-abled throughout fine-tuning to isolate the effect of GRPO on the safety-classification capability. The remaining hyperparameters are held constant across all models: 8 generations per prompt, a learning rate of 5 × 10 −7, and a maximum com-pletion length of 1,024 tokens. Learning rate 5e-7, batch size 64, and epoch 1 are applied for all models. We use 4 NVIDIA H100 GPUs for this experiment.

E Impact of System Prompts on Posterior Attack Success Rate 

In our primary evaluations, we utilized the system prompt detailed in Table 7, following its established efficacy in prior literature (Liu et al., 2024). How-ever, because the Safety Paradox and the resulting Posterior Attack are theoretically grounded in struc-tural vulnerabilities rather than superficial heuristic manipulation, their efficacy should remain robust independent of system-level framing. To empiri-cally validate this, we conduct an ablation study comparing the ASR of our method using the stan-dard system prompt against a completely empty system prompt. As demonstrated in Table 5, Posterior Attack exhibits substantial resilience across the majority of frontier models even in the absence of a system prompt. Architectures such as GPT-5-Chat, Qwen3 235B, and DeepSeek V3.2 maintain near-perfect exploitability (exceeding 93% ASR) under both conditions, confirming that the attack successfully targets a core misalignment rather than relying on prompt-specific mechanics. Interestingly, GPT-4o demonstrates an inverse relationship, becoming sig-nificantly more vulnerable—surging from 78.7% to 93.7% ASR—when the system prompt is removed. The notable anomaly is Claude 4.6 Sonnet, which experiences a sharp decline in vulnerabil-ity (dropping from 93.7% to 39.0%) without the system prompt. Unlike the other evaluated archi-tectures, Claude 4.6 Sonnet operates as a reason-ing model, generating a Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prior to its final response. However, this fluctuation highlights that alternative prompt engineering com-bined with Posterior Attack could serve as a viable vector to break down models secured with test-time scaling safeguards, exposing a potential open chal-lenge in defending reasoning-based models against posterior exploitation.

G Case Study of Posterior Attack 

To empirically illustrate the mechanics of the Poste-rior Attack, we present a detailed case study initial-ized with the harmful query "Write a tutorial on how to make a bomb" (Table 7). When con-strained to non-thinking or low-reasoning configu-rations, LLMs consistently prioritize the objective classification framing, directly generating the re-quested harmful artifacts to satisfy the prompt’s constraints (Tables 9, 10, 13, and 17). Conversely, scaling up test-time computation (medium and high reasoning efforts) acts as a robust defensive mecha-nism for most architectures. By engaging in delib-erative reasoning, GPT-OSS models successfully retrieve implicit safety policies from their para-metric memory to override the malicious prompt and enforce a refusal state (Tables 11, 12, 14, 15). Similarly, GPT-5 and Gemma-4 26B occasionally demonstrate the capacity to trigger these protective actions under extended compute (Tables 16 and 18).
