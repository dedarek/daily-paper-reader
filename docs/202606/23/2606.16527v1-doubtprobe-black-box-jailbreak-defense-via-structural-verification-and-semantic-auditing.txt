## Abstract 

As large language models (LLMs) are increasingly deployed in user-facing systems, black-box jailbreak defense has become an important practical problem. Existing defenses often rely on known-attack coverage, prompt-level semantic judgment, or local run-time control, yet these paths can become unstable under evolving prompt packaging, expression rewriting, and structure manipula-tion. We observe that many black-box jailbreaks do not remove the harmful goal, but reorganize the information needed to express and execute it, thereby evading safety alignment while remain-ing recoverable during generation. Motivated by this observation, we propose DoubtProbe , a dual-branch inference-time defense framework that combines structural verification with semantic au-diting and formulates black-box jailbreak defense as consistency checking under controlled transformation. The structural branch extracts a structured representation from the original request, re-constructs the request under representation constraints, and detects information-preservation failures between the original and recon-structed requests; the semantic branch audits the original prompt directly. We evaluate DoubtProbe against representative black-box defenses on jailbreak and benign-request benchmarks, and further test backbone transfer from Qwen2.5-72B to Llama-3.1-70B. Re-sults show that DoubtProbe achieves a stronger and more stable defense–utility trade-off: on Qwen2.5-72B, it reduces the JBB attack success rate from 0.293 to 0.100 and the CodeAttack attack success rate from 0.152 to 0.001, while maintaining false positive rates of 0.022 and 0.016 on AlpacaEval and OR-Bench; the same pattern re-mains stable on Llama-3.1-70B. These findings show that structural inconsistency signals provide a practical and generalizable basis for black-box jailbreak defense, especially when combined with semantic auditing.

## 1 Introduction 

Large language models (LLMs) are increasingly deployed as user-facing generative interfaces and core components in real-world systems, making the tension between safety alignment and jail-break evasion a central deployment challenge [ 6, 12 ]. Existing jail-break attacks now extend far beyond manually written role-play and context-reframing prompts to include optimized adversarial suffixes, transferable prompts, and structure-heavy attacks built on code-like or symbolic constructions [ 4 , 27 , 31 , 36 , 41 , 52 , 67 ]. This evolution leaves black-box defenses facing an open and continu-ously changing attack surface. In this setting, a practically useful defense must stop genuinely unsafe requests while avoiding unnec-essary refusals of benign use. Existing black-box defenses take diverse forms, yet their deci-sions often rely on a small set of dominant control paths, including distribution-fitted safeguards, prompt-level semantic judgment or steering, and runtime control during generation. Distribution-fitted safeguards such as guard models and input classifiers can provide strong coverage on attack patterns close to their training distribu-tions, but the jailbreak space is open-ended and rapidly evolving, making full coverage difficult and often encouraging more conser-vative boundaries that increase benign refusals [ 6, 11 , 13 , 30 , 60 ]. Prompt-level judgment, steering, and runtime-control methods can be effective on many explicit attacks, yet their signals may still be disturbed by wrapping, indirection, role-playing, discourse-level reframing, or local generation behavior [ 21 , 37 , 43 , 49 , 55 , 64 ]. As attacks begin to target these dominant decision paths directly, the stability of defense degrades. Ultimately, existing methods still lack a more stable defense principle that can handle unseen attacks without over-relying on coverage of known attack patterns or on direct semantic recovery of the prompt’s true intent. Our key ob-servation is that many black-box jailbreaks do not remove the harmful goal; instead, they reorganize the information needed to express and execute that goal. Prior work has shown that in-the-wild jailbreak prompts often repackage harmful requests through

role-playing, prompt injection, and privilege escalation [ 41 ]. Other studies further show that harmful requests can be hidden through prompt decomposition, code-completion framing, and symbolic-construction tasks [ 18 , 36 ]. Together, these findings suggest that jailbreak attacks often preserve the harmful goal while changing how it is organized and interpreted. To evade safety alignment, such attacks avoid presenting the harmful request as a direct and coherent instruction, while still retaining task-defining harmful el-ements, cross-step dependencies, and compositional structures that allow the target model to recover the unsafe task during generation. This observation suggests a defense signal different from direct intent extraction. If a jailbreak relies on packaging or rewriting to hide the harmful goal, the harmful content distributed across the prompt through decomposition, reordering, or structural em-bedding may not be stably preserved when the request passes through a representation-constrained extraction and reconstruc-tion process. Since the reconstruction is derived only from the structured representation, these omissions can propagate to the re-constructed request and appear as inconsistencies between the orig-inal and reconstructed requests. DoubtProbe uses this information-preservation failure as a concrete defense-side signal under black-box constraints. Based on this formulation, we propose DoubtProbe , a dual-branch inference-time defense framework for black-box deploy-ment. One branch performs structural verification: it converts the input prompt into a structured representation, derives a constrained reconstruction from that representation, and audits whether salient information is preserved across the transformation. The other branch performs semantic auditing on the original prompt to han-dle cases whose harmful meaning remains directly recoverable even when the surface form appears stable. The two branches are com-bined in a unified decision layer, allowing structural inconsistency signals and semantic safety judgments to cover complementary failure modes under the same deployment protocol. We evaluate DoubtProbe on JBB, CodeAttack, AlpacaEval, and OR-Bench against representative black-box baselines. The results show a stronger and more stable trade-off between attack robust-ness and benign usability, and this pattern remains consistent when the defense-side backbone changes from Qwen2.5-72B to Llama-3.1-70B. These results indicate that inference-time defenses based on the preservation of explicit content blocks and their organiza-tional structure in the original request, particularly when combined with semantic-level verification, provide a practical approach for building more general and reliable black-box jailbreak defenses. The contributions of this work are as follows: 

• We present a new perspective on black-box jailbreak de-fense by checking whether critical information in the ex-plicitly provided instructions and content elements of the original request is preserved after controlled simplification and reconstruction. 

• We propose DoubtProbe , a dual-branch inference-time defense framework that combines structural verification with semantic auditing under black-box constraints. 

• We conduct evaluations across heterogeneous jailbreak benchmarks, benign-request benchmarks, latency analy-sis, backbone replacement settings, and adaptive attacks, showing the effectiveness and stability of DoubtProbe .

• We report strong empirical gains: DoubtProbe reduces macro attack ASR from 0.237 to 0.033 and CodeAttack ASR from 0.152 to 0.001, with a benign macro FPR of 0.019. 

## 2 Background 2.1 Threat Model 

Attack Scenario. We study jailbreak attacks against text-only large language models (LLMs) in a black-box setting[ 39 , 41 , 67 ]. In this setting, an adversary interacts with a defended LLM system only through the user input interface and attempts to bypass the input-side defense by crafting malicious or adversarially wrapped prompts. The goal of jailbreaking is to circumvent the model’s safety alignment and induce the target model to generate harmful, toxic, or otherwise disallowed content. We focus on direct user-prompt jailbreaks , including attacks based on obfuscation, role-playing, contextual reframing, symbolic indirection, and structure-heavy prompt construction[ 8, 17 , 20 ]. Attacks outside this scope, such as multimodal jailbreaks[ 26 , 34 ], training-time poisoning[ 47 , 57 ], or prompt injection through external retrieval corpora, tools, or agent environments[10, 62, 68], are not considered in this paper. Let the target model be denoted by M, and let 𝑄 denote an input prompt submitted by the adversary. Upon receiving 𝑄 , the system dispatches it simultaneously to the target model and to the defense architecture. The target model may begin generating a response 𝑅 ∼ M ( 𝑄 ) immediately, while the defense architec-ture analyzes the same request in parallel and outputs a decision 

DEF (𝑄 ) ∈ Pass , Block . If the generation side finishes first, the generated content is buffered and withheld from the user until the defense decision is returned. If the defense side finishes first and out-puts Block , the ongoing generation is truncated and the response is replaced by a refusal; otherwise, the buffered or subsequently completed response is released. Under this deployment protocol, generation and defense-side auditing proceed concurrently. As a re-sult, the end-to-end latency is determined primarily by the slower branch, plus the buffering and release overhead, instead of the combined cost of running generation and defense in sequence.

denote the defense decision, and let JUDGE (𝑅 ) ∈ { True , False }

denote whether the response 𝑅 satisfies the harmful target criterion. Then the adversary’s objective is to maximize the probability that the prompt is passed by the defense and the resulting response is judged harmful:

Constraints and Assumptions. We assume the following setup in our jailbreak threat model: 

• The adversary has only black-box access to the defended system: it can submit arbitrary textual prompts and observe the final system outcome, including the released model response when a request is passed, but it cannot access model weights, gradients, hidden states, safety classifier internals, or defense-side intermediate representations. 

• Upon receiving a prompt 𝑄 , the system dispatches it si-multaneously to the target model and to the defense ar-chitecture. No partial generation or intermediate output is exposed to the adversary before the defense decision is finalized. 

• The defender has access only to the incoming prompt and its own internal analysis results, but no privileged access to the target model’s internal activations or decoding dynamics. 

## 2.2 Jailbreak Attacks and Defense Challenges 

Jailbreak attacks are heterogeneous in both how they are con-structed and how they evade safeguards. Existing attacks can be broadly grouped into several representative families, including man-ual or discourse-driven jailbreaks, optimization- and search-based attacks, and automated or structure-heavy attacks. From a defense perspective, these attacks expose two dominant failure modes: some depend primarily on observable structural manipulation, while oth-ers preserve a largely coherent surface form but alter interpretation through discourse-level strategies. 

Manual and discourse-driven jailbreaks. A first family con-sists of manually written or discourse-driven jailbreaks. Represen-tative instances include DAN-style prompts, forbidden-question prompts, and template-based jailbreaks [ 4, 41 , 52 ]. These attacks often retain a coherent natural-language form and may not exhibit conspicuous structural anomalies. Instead, they use role-playing, hypothetical framing, instruction reinterpretation, and contextual steering to guide the model toward unsafe behavior. Closely related are direct harmful-goal prompts, which do not necessarily conceal malicious intent through elaborate wrappers but still serve as an important stress test for whether a defense can block clearly unsafe requests without depending on narrow template matching[4, 27]. 

Optimization and search-based jailbreaks. A second family consists of jailbreaks that are automatically optimized or searched over the prompt space. Representative examples include gradient-based suffix attacks, transfer-oriented optimization variants, and random-search jailbreaks [ 1 , 24 , 28 , 67 ]. These attacks do not rely on a fixed handcrafted template. Instead, they exploit blind spots in the alignment behavior of the target model by iteratively identify-ing prompt forms that increase the likelihood of unsafe generation. Some of them produce highly artificial suffixes or adversarial ar-tifacts [ 1, 67 ], whereas others remain relatively fluent while still being optimized against the defended system [ 24 ]. Their concrete surface forms can vary substantially across prompts, targets, and optimization procedures, which makes exhaustive coverage difficult for detectors trained on limited attack distributions. 

Automated and structure-heavy jailbreaks. A third family combines iterative model-generated attacks with structure-heavy artifact attacks. Representative examples include PAIR-style iter-ative jailbreaks and CodeAttack-style prompts based on ciphers, code-like list or stack constructions, and other explicit symbolic transformations [ 5, 15 , 36 ]. Although these attacks differ in surface appearance, they share an important property: the harmful func-tionality often emerges through the joint interpretation of multiple components. This makes them a strong stress test for defenses, since effective detection requires reasoning over composition, ordering, and structural preservation, and approaches relying on explicit semantic cues are often insufficient for effective detection. Existing black-box defenses are not uniform in design. From the perspective of defense signal and control locus, they can be loosely grouped into three recurring families: distribution-fitted safeguards, prompt-level semantic judgment or steering, and decoding-time or stochastic runtime control. These families are not mutually exclu-sive in every implementation, but they capture three common ways in which black-box systems attempt to block jailbreaks. From the perspective of DoubtProbe , the first two most directly expose the limitations that motivate our design, while the third further shows that the current black-box defense space is broader than prompt classification or prompt-level semantic auditing alone. 

Coverage limits of distribution-fitted safeguards. One ma-jor family of black-box defenses builds safety primarily through distribution-fitted safeguards, including guard models, input classi-fiers, and related detector-style defenses trained on curated attack data [ 23 , 50 ]. Representative examples include LlamaGuard-style systems and other detector-based safeguards such as WildGuard, ShieldGemma, and Aegis [ 9 , 11 , 13 , 60 ]. Their core limitation is coverage: the jailbreak space is open-ended, evolves rapidly, and is difficult to enumerate in advance [ 4, 30 , 41 ]. As a result, even when such systems perform well on attacks similar to those seen during training, they may lag behind newly emerging prompt patterns or overfit to specific artifacts [ 11 , 30 ]. Broadening coverage may also encourage more conservative decision boundaries, which can in turn increase benign refusals [38, 46]. 

Interpretive fragility of prompt-level semantic judgment or steering. Another major family of black-box defenses relies primarily on prompt-level semantic judgment or steering, includ-ing LLM-as-a-judge style pipelines, self-defense mechanisms, and related runtime prompting strategies [ 32 , 43 , 49 , 55 , 64 ]. These ap-proaches depend on correctly recovering hidden malicious intent from the input itself, or on steering the model toward a safer inter-pretation of that input. Their reliability can degrade when attacks alter interpretation through indirection, wrapping, role-playing, or discourse-level reframing, because the critical defense decision then depends on the same interpretive process that the attack is de-signed to manipulate [ 3, 41 , 43 , 44 , 49 , 52 , 61 ]. This family remains fragile when semantic recovery itself becomes the attack surface. 

Local-control limits of decoding-time and stochastic run-time defenses. A third family intervenes during generation through decoding-time control or stochastic runtime procedures. Repre-sentative examples include SafeDecoding, RAIN, and SmoothLLM [ 21 , 37 , 58 ]. These methods constrain generation through token-level adjustment, self-evaluation and rewind, or perturbation-and-aggregation procedures. Their strength lies in runtime controllabil-ity, but their decision signal is still tied to generation-time behavior Trovato et al. 

and may remain sensitive to target-model behavior or perturba-tion assumptions[ 16 , 63 , 65 ]. These methods expand the black-box defense design space. 

## 2.3 Design Motivation 

These challenges reveal a common pattern in black-box jailbreak attacks: adversaries often retain the original malicious objective while modifying its expression. Through role-playing, hypotheti-cal framing, instruction reinterpretation, symbolic composition, or structural modification, attack requests can be crafted to evade exist-ing defenses while still triggering unsafe outputs in the generation model. Based on this observation, we define the defense objective as the explicitly provided content and its organizational structure in the original request that are relevant to downstream harmful behav-ior, and verify whether this critical information remains preserved through controlled simplification and reconstruction, thereby pro-ducing a defense signal. Let 𝑄 denote the original request, let 𝑆 denote a structured rep-resentation extracted from 𝑄 , and let ˆ𝑄 denote a reconstruction derived only from 𝑆 . The central question is whether the salient information in 𝑄 survives the transformation 𝑄 → 𝑆 → ˆ𝑄 . We are particularly concerned with content elements, operational relations, and composition patterns that matter for downstream harmful be-havior. When a jailbreak depends on packaging, obfuscation, sym-bolic composition, or adversarial rewriting, some of these critical ingredients may fail to enter 𝑆 in full. The resulting omission can propagate to ˆ𝑄 and appear as inconsistency between 𝑄 and ˆ𝑄 . Un-der black-box constraints, such inconsistencies provide a concrete signal for defense-side auditing. 

Proposition 1 (Unrecoverable Omission under Constrained Reconstruction). Let 𝑆 = 𝑓 (𝑄 ) be a structured representation extracted from a request 𝑄 , and let ˆ𝑄 = 𝑔 (𝑆 ) be a reconstruction produced using only 𝑆 . Let 𝜙 (𝑄 ) denote any salient attribute of 𝑄 

that is relevant to downstream harmful behavior, such as a content component, an operational relation, or a composition pattern. If there exist two requests 𝑄 1 and 𝑄 2 such that 

𝑓 (𝑄 1) = 𝑓 (𝑄 2) but 𝜙 (𝑄 1) ≠ 𝜙 (𝑄 2),

then no reconstruction procedure that conditions only on 𝑆 can guarantee preservation of 𝜙 for both inputs simultaneously. 

Proof sketch. Since 𝑔 only receives 𝑆 , both 𝑄 1 and 𝑄 2 induce the same reconstruction condition. Therefore, the reconstruction process cannot deterministically preserve two different values of 𝜙 from the same 𝑆 . For stochastic 𝑔 , the conditional distribution 𝑝 ( ˆ𝑄 | 𝑆 ) is still identical for both inputs, so preservation of 𝜙 cannot be guaranteed for both with probability 1. Hence, once a salient attribute is omitted in 𝑆 , it becomes unrecoverable under constrained reconstruction. This proposition motivates the hybrid design of DoubtProbe .Structural verification targets failures that manifest as omission, attenuation, or distortion under constrained transformation, while semantic auditing complements it on attacks whose surface form remains largely coherent. The resulting system does not assume that a single mechanism can uniformly solve all jailbreaks; instead, it combines a constrained consistency-checking path with a semantic judgment path under the same black-box deployment setting. 

## 3 DoubtProbe 

## 3.1 Overview 

DoubtProbe is a hybrid black-box jailbreak defense that com-bines structural verification with semantic auditing. Given an input prompt 𝑄 , the system launches two analysis paths in parallel. The first path performs structural verification by transforming 𝑄 into a structured representation 𝑆 , reconstructing a prompt ˆ𝑄 from 𝑆 ,and checking whether salient information is stably preserved un-der the transformation 𝑄 → 𝑆 → ˆ𝑄 . The second path performs semantic auditing, which directly evaluates the original prompt for unsafe intent that may remain structurally coherent but semanti-cally manipulative. At deployment time, this defense-side branch parallelism is combined with system-level concurrency. While the target model begins response generation, the defense side executes its analysis in parallel, and the generated content is buffered until the final defense decision is returned. The structural branch is implemented by three components. A 

Structurer extracts the structured representation 𝑆 from 𝑄 . A Recon-structor derives ˆ𝑄 from 𝑆 . A Comparator then evaluates consistency among 𝑄 , 𝑆 , and ˆ𝑄 to detect suspicious information loss, attenua-tion, or distortion. In parallel, the semantic audit module produces an independent semantic verdict on 𝑄 . The final defense decision is made by a unified decision layer that aggregates the structural verdict and the semantic verdict. This design allows DoubtProbe 

to address complementary failure modes under the same black-box deployment setting. Figure 1 presents the overall workflow and deployment protocol of DoubtProbe .

## 3.2 Structural Verification Pipeline 

The structural verification pipeline transforms the original request into a constrained representation-and-reconstruction process. For-mally, given an input prompt 𝑄 , the pipeline first extracts a struc-tured representation 𝑆 , then derives a reconstructed prompt ˆ𝑄 using only 𝑆 , and finally evaluates whether salient information is stably preserved under the transformation 

𝑄 → 𝑆 → ˆ𝑄. 

Its output is a structural verdict indicating whether the request exhibits suspicious omission, attenuation, or distortion under this constrained transformation. A key design choice is that the intermediate modules in this branch do not need to perfectly recover hidden malicious intent. In particular, the structural analysis may be misled by adversarial wrapping or obfuscation. This behavior is explicitly accommodated by the design of the branch. When critical content is excluded from 𝑆 , the omission propagates to ˆ𝑄 and becomes observable as inconsistency between 𝑄 and ˆ𝑄 . The branch uses this propagated inconsistency as evidence of deception. 

Structurer. The Structurer maps 𝑄 to a structured represen-tation 𝑆 . Its role is to decompose the request into typed content blocks and preserve explicit surface-level cues that are recoverable under constrained access. This design is consistent with prior work on prompt decomposition and text-to-structure extraction, where complex inputs are transformed into explicit intermediate repre-sentations for downstream processing and verification [ 18 , 42 , 54 ]. This design makes the structural branch complementary to se-mantic auditing. When harmful intent is directly expressed, the semantic branch can often block the request without requiring sub-stantial structural inconsistency. When harmful intent is hidden inside wrapping, symbolic composition, or other structure-heavy forms, the structural branch can detect the downstream trace of that concealment through a mismatch between 𝑄 and ˆ𝑄 . The full system therefore does not depend on a single branch to solve all attack types uniformly. 

## 3.3 Semantic Audit Module 

The semantic audit module provides a complementary defense path that operates directly on the original request 𝑄 . Its purpose is to identify requests whose unsafe intent is explicitly expressed or remains recoverable through direct semantic analysis. In such cases, structural inconsistency may be weak or absent, yet the request should still be blocked on semantic grounds. Unlike the structural branch, this module does not rely on re-construction. It evaluates the semantic risk of the original prompt itself and produces a semantic verdict. The role of this branch is not to replace structural verification, but to complement it on requests whose surface form remains stable while their harmful meaning is directly available to semantic interpretation. Its output is a se-mantic verdict 𝑣 sem ∈ { Pass , Block }. The full prompt template is provided in Appendix C.

## 4 Experimental Setup 

We evaluate DoubtProbe under the black-box jailbreak setting defined in §2.1. Our experiments use three complementary jailbreak benchmarks and two benign-request benchmarks, and compare 

DoubtProbe against representative black-box defenses under a unified pass/block protocol. In this section, we describe the datasets and attack setup, the evaluation metrics, the latency measurement protocol, the compared baselines, and the ablation settings used throughout the experiments. 

## 4.1 Datasets and Attack Setup 

Benchmarks. Based on the heterogeneous jailbreak families dis-cussed in §2.2, we use three attack benchmarks and two benign-request benchmarks in our evaluation. We use JailbreakBench (JBB) [ 4 ] as the main source of harmful-goal prompts and artifact-based jailbreak prompts. Its artifact portion includes artifact prompts derived from Don’t Say No (DSN) [ 66 ], Greedy Coordinate Gradient (GCG) [ 67 ], JailbreakChat (JBC) [ 52 ], Prompt Automatic Iterative Refinement (PAIR) [ 5 ], and Adaptive Random Search (ARS) [ 62 ]. We use JailbreakHub [ 41 ] as the source of DAN prompts and CodeAt-tack [ 36 ] as a complementary stress-test benchmark for structure-heavy prompts that are less directly captured by discourse-driven or artifact-style prompt sets. On the benign-requests, we use Al-pacaEval [ 19 ] and OR-Bench [ 7 ]. Table 1 summarizes the bench-mark datasets used in this paper. These benchmark choices are also broadly consistent with the evaluation settings adopted by several representative defense methods considered in our compari-son [6, 12, 49]. In total, the attack evaluation suite includes 100 harmful-goal prompts from JBB-Behaviors, 582 JBB artifact prompts, 390 DAN prompts from JailbreakHub, and 2600 CodeAttack instances. For benign-request evaluation, we use 805 non-empty instructions from AlpacaEval and a fixed 775-prompt benchmark instance generated under the OR-Bench workflow. 

Attack Setup. For harmful-goal prompts from JBB-Behaviors [ 4], we use 100 explicit harmful requests. For DSN [ 66 ], we use the white-box prompt artifacts. For GCG [ 67 ], we use both transfer and white-box prompt artifacts. For JBC [ 52 ], we use the manu-ally written jailbreak prompts. For PAIR [ 5 ] and ARS [ 62 ], we use the black-box prompt artifacts released in JBB. For DAN [ 41 ], we use template-instantiated DAN-style prompts constructed from jailbreak templates and harmful questions released in JailbreakHub. For CodeAttack [ 36 ], we use four attack categories, namely ci-pher, list, stack, and string, and additionally include one plain split as a control condition. Each category contains 520 prompt instances. For AlpacaEval [ 19 ], we use all 805 non-empty instructions. For OR-Bench [ 7], we use one fixed 775-prompt benchmark instance and reuse the same prompt set across all compared methods. 

## 4.2 Evaluation Metrics 

LLM Judge Setup. We use two external LLM judges with differ-ent decision criteria for attack and benign-request evaluation. For attack, the evaluation must distinguish genuinely harmful compli-ance from safe redirection, refusal, or other non-harmful responses. For benign-requests, the evaluation must determine whether the system actually refuses to provide useful information for a harmless request. We use GPT-4o for attack judgment and Claude 3.5 Son-net for benign-request judgment; validation details are provided in Appendix E. The full judge prompts are provided in Appendix G. 

Attack Metric. For attack evaluation, we use attack success rate (ASR) to measure defense failure under jailbreak prompts [ 6,41 ]. A jailbreak attempt is counted as successful only if the final system output is judged as harmful by the attack judge. Thus, lower ASR indicates stronger defense performance. ## 4.3 Latency Measurement Protocol 

To ensure a fair comparison, we measure latency as black-box end-to-end time from the invocation of the defense interface to the return of the final PASS /BLOCK decision and, if applicable, the re-turned response. With the exception of the LlamaGuard baselines, all methods are instantiated on the same self-hosted Qwen2.5-72B inference endpoint running on our server, with concurrency fixed to 1 and temperature fixed to 0. LlamaGuard baselines are queried through the OpenRouter API. To reduce implementation-side vari-ance as much as possible, all methods are evaluated through the same architecture wrapper and measurement harness used in the main experiments. We therefore interpret the reported numbers as deployment-level end-to-end overhead under our evaluation setup. We evaluate latency under two scenarios. The benign scenario is constructed from AlpacaEval instructions, and the harmful sce-nario is constructed from JBB goals_harmful prompts. For each scenario, we sample five prompts with a fixed random seed. For each architecture, we first run one warm-up prompt and then perform three repeated measurements per prompt. To reduce time-drift bias caused by backend load variation, architecture order is interleaved across prompts during execution. We interpret the resulting latency numbers as deployment-level system overhead. Accordingly, the measurement covers all defense-side processing involved in the end-to-end pipeline. 

## 4.4 Baselines 

We compare against one no-defense reference and nine external defense methods, corresponding to 12 evaluated baseline variants in total. As the no-defense reference, we use No defense (Qwen2.5-72B) [ 2]. The compared baselines cover the three black-box defense families discussed in §2.2: distribution-fitted safeguards, prompt-level semantic judgment or steering, and decoding-time or stochas-tic runtime control. For prompt-level semantic judgment or steering, we include SelfDefend [49], Self Reminder [55], ICD [53], and Goal Prioritiza-tion [ 64 ]. SelfDefend uses a shadow LLM to analyze harmful content or intent in parallel with the target model; we evaluate its direct and intent variants. Self Reminder encapsulates the user query in a safety-oriented system prompt; we evaluate two implementations, namely Self Reminder-HP, corresponding to the original system-mode reminder, and Self Reminder-OP, an optimized prompt variant from the official repository. ICD prepends a small number of refusal demonstrations as in-context examples. Goal Prioritization steers the model to prioritize safety over helpfulness. For decoding-time or stochastic runtime control, we include RAIN [ 21 ] and SmoothLLM [ 37 ]. RAIN introduces self-evaluation and rewind during generation. SmoothLLM perturbs multiple copies of an input prompt and aggregates the resulting model behavior. For distribution-fitted safeguards, we include LlamaGuard2 [ 45 ], LlamaGuard3 [ 25 ], and LlamaGuard4 [ 29 ], which are safeguard models for prompt and response safety classification. 

## 4.5 Ablation Settings 

We evaluate three internal variants of DoubtProbe to analyze branch complementarity and the effect of comparator-level seman-tic integration. DoubtProbe -SV is a structural-verification-only variant that keeps the Structural Verification Pipeline and removes the Semantic Audit Module. DoubtProbe -SA is a semantic-audit-only variant that keeps the Semantic Audit Module and removes the Structural Verification Pipeline. DoubtProbe -CI is a comparator-integrated variant that does not use an independent Semantic Audit Module. Instead, semantic reasoning is incorporated into the Com-parator itself, which makes the final decision by jointly considering semantic rules and structural verification rules. These variants are used to examine whether the gain of the full system comes from complementary branch behavior across attack families, and whether comparator-level semantic integration can replace an in-dependent semantic branch. 

## 5 Evaluation 

We evaluate DoubtProbe from three perspectives. First, we exam-ine whether the full system achieves a favorable defense–utility trade-off when compared with strong baselines on jailbreak and benign-request benchmarks. Second, we analyze how DoubtProbe 

behaves across different attack families and what its representative successes and failures reveal about the remaining limitations of the method. Third, we study whether the Structural Verification Pipeline and the Semantic Audit Module provide complementary capabilities through ablation analysis. Accordingly, our evaluation is organized around the following research questions: 

RQ1. Does the full DoubtProbe improve the defense–utility trade-off over strong baselines on jailbreak and benign-request benchmarks? 

RQ2. How does DoubtProbe behave across different attack families, and what do representative successes and failures reveal about its remaining failure modes? 

RQ3. Do the Structural Verification Pipeline and the Semantic Audit Module contribute complementary capabilities, and how do the ablation variants compare with the full system?

## 5.3 RQ3: Ablation Analysis of Branch Complementarity 

We evaluate three internal variants of DoubtProbe to examine whether the gain of the full system comes from complementary branch behavior across attack families. We further examine whether comparator-level semantic integration can replace an independent semantic branch, and whether structural verification and semantic auditing need to remain separate in the full system. DoubtProbe -SV removes the Semantic Audit Module, DoubtProbe -SA removes the Structural Verification Pipeline, and DoubtProbe -CI incor-porates semantic reasoning into the Comparator without using a separate semantic branch. Table 6 summarizes the ablation results. The full system achieves the lowest macro attack ASR among the four variants. Its macro attack ASR is 0.033, compared with 0.108 for DoubtProbe -SV, 0.060 for DoubtProbe -SA, and 0.048 for 

DoubtProbe -CI. On the benign-requests, its macro FPR is 0.019. This value is lower than that of DoubtProbe -CI and higher than those of DoubtProbe -SV and DoubtProbe -SA. Single-branch vari-ants and comparator-level integration therefore do not reproduce the overall balance of the full system. The residual errors of the ablation variants are distributed dif-ferently across attack types. DoubtProbe -SV remains relatively strong on optimization/search-based attacks and on CodeAttack. Its optimization/search average is 0.038, and its CodeAttack result is 0.004. Its DAN and PAIR results rise to 0.423 and 0.342. Doubt-Probe -SA shows the opposite pattern. It reduces DAN to 0.326 and keeps PAIR at 0.049, but its CodeAttack result rises to 0.087. The two branches are strongest on different attack types. The contrast between PAIR and CodeAttack makes this differ-ence clearer. Both belong to the structure-heavy family, but their ab-lation patterns differ. DoubtProbe -SV remains effective on CodeAt-tack but is much weaker on PAIR. DoubtProbe -SA shows the re-verse pattern, matching the full system on PAIR while degrading on CodeAttack. These results indicate that the structure-heavy family is not fully uniform, and that the two branches address different forms of risk within it. 

DoubtProbe -CI is closer to the full system than either single-branch variant. Its macro attack ASR is 0.048, compared with 0.108 for DoubtProbe -SV and 0.060 for DoubtProbe -SA. It also remains strong on optimization/search-based attacks and on CodeAttack, with values of 0.008 and 0.006. At the same time, its manual/dis-course average is 0.160, and its benign macro FPR rises to 0.023. Incorporating semantic rules into the Comparator is therefore use-ful, but it does not replace an independent semantic branch. Overall, the ablation results support the two-branch design of 

DoubtProbe . The full system combines strengths across attack types. This advantage weakens when either branch is removed or when semantic reasoning is compressed into the Comparator.

## 6 Discussion and Limitations 

Observed Defense Pattern. Based on our analysis, DoubtProbe 

exhibits a stable defense pattern across benchmarks, attack families, and defense-side backbones. The full system repeatedly reaches a favorable operating regime in which attack robustness remains strong and benign-request cost stays moderate. This behavior is most evident on optimization/search-based and structure-heavy jailbreaks, where the complementary effect of structural verification and semantic auditing is most visible. The same pattern remains visible after replacing the defense-side backbone with Llama-3.1-70B. The remaining difficulty is concentrated in a smaller set of families, especially manual and discourse-driven ones, and the residual cross-backbone differences mostly align with the hardness of the corresponding undefended baselines. Taken together, these observations indicate that the main contribution of DoubtProbe 

lies in the stability of its two-branch defense pattern and in the overall complementarity between structural and semantic signals. 

Limitations of DoubtProbe . The results also clarify the bound-ary of the method. First, the defense is not equally strong on all jailbreak families. Requests with explicit construction artifacts or strong optimization traces tend to expose clearer structural or se-mantic evidence, whereas fluent attacks with stable surface seman-tics remain more difficult. This helps explain why families such as DAN still contribute a large portion of the residual ASR. Second, the absolute operating point of the full system still depends on the defense-side backbone. The backbone-replacement results show that the overall pattern remains stable, but some defended families stay higher under a stronger undefended baseline. Third, our threat model is limited to text-only black-box jailbreak defense on the input side. The present study does not cover multimodal prompts, retrieval-time injection, tool-use environments, or computer-use agents. These settings may introduce additional sources of ambigu-ity that are not exposed in the benchmarks. 

Future Work. Several directions follow naturally from these limita-tions. A first priority is to improve stability on fluent and discourse-driven jailbreaks, especially cases in which harmful intent survives with limited structural distortion. A second direction is to extend the current two-branch pipeline beyond text-only settings, includ-ing multimodal inputs and tool-augmented agent environments. A third direction is to reduce defense-side overhead and move from the current prompt-based implementation toward a more compact learned pipeline, while preserving the interpretability of structural and semantic signals. We view these directions as important steps toward making black-box jailbreak defense more robust in realistic deployment settings. Trovato et al.

## 7 Related Work 

Jailbreak Attacks. Jailbreak attacks on LLMs are heterogeneous in construction and evasion strategy, and existing attacks can be broadly grouped into three representative families. The first family consists of manual or discourse-driven jailbreaks , which rely on human-written templates or rhetorical manipulation rather than explicit optimization. Representative examples include DAN-style prompts, forbidden-question prompts, and other template-based manual jailbreaks [ 4 , 8, 41 , 52 ]. These attacks are often human-readable and may preserve a coherent surface form while steering the model toward unsafe behavior through role-playing, hypo-thetical framing, instruction reinterpretation, or other discourse-level strategies [ 3, 20 , 44 , 61 ]. Closely related are direct harmful-goal prompts, which do not necessarily conceal malicious intent through elaborate wrappers but still serve as an important baseline for testing whether a defense can block clearly unsafe requests without narrow template matching[ 4 , 27 , 35 ]. The second fam-ily consists of optimization- and search-based jailbreaks, which automatically search over the prompt space rather than relying on fixed handcrafted templates. Representative examples include gradient-based suffix attacks, transfer-oriented optimization vari-ants, refusal-suppression attacks, and random-search jailbreaks [1 , 17 , 22 , 24 , 66 , 67 ]. These attacks exploit blind spots in alignment behavior by iteratively identifying prompt forms that increase un-safe generation, and their concrete surface forms can vary sub-stantially across prompts, targets, and optimization procedures [ 59 ]. The third family consists of automated or structure-heavy jailbreaks, where harmful functionality emerges through model-generated iterations or explicit structural transformations. Repre-sentative examples include PAIR-style iterative jailbreaks [ 5, 28 ]and CodeAttack-style prompts based on code-like structures or sym-bolic transformations [ 18 , 36 ]. Compared with manual templates, these attacks are harder to capture with simple pattern matching, since effective detection often requires reasoning over composition, ordering, and structural preservation beyond direct semantic cues. 

Detection and Defense of Jailbreak Attacks. Detecting jail-break attacks while preserving benign utility has become a central problem in LLM safety [ 38 , 46 , 50 ]. Existing defenses can be broadly grouped into three lines. The first line treats jailbreak defense as a distribution-fitting problem, where safety relies on decision bound-aries learned from known attack-like or policy-violating inputs. Representative examples include classifier-based safeguards such as Llama Guard [ 13 , 29 , 45 ], open moderation models such as Wild-Guard [ 11 ] and ShieldGemma [ 60 ], and training-based defense mechanisms such as SelfDefend [ 49 ], SecAlign [ 6], Constitutional Classifiers [ 40 ], and Bergeron [ 33 ]. The second line operates at the prompt level by reminding, auditing, rewriting, or steering the model’s semantic interpretation of the input. Typical methods include Self Reminder [ 55 ], SelfDefend [ 49 ], ICD [ 53 ], Goal Pri-oritization [ 64 ], Backtranslation [ 51 ], RePD [ 48 ], and Defensive Prompt Patch [ 56 ]. Rather than learning a separate strong classifier, these methods make the model more likely to recognize unsafe intent or prioritize safety over helpfulness [ 32 , 65 ]. However, they can become unstable when attacks rely on discourse reframing, fictional scenarios, or complex semantic packaging [ 43 , 63 ], and because they increase the model’s safety sensitivity and refusal tendency rather than independently verifying the input, they are also more prone to over-refusal and higher benign-request false positive rates [ 7, 38 , 46 ]. The third line intervenes during gener-ation rather than relying primarily on learned input filtering or prompt-level judgment. Representative methods include SafeDe-coding [ 58 ], SmoothLLM [ 37 ], RAIN [ 21 ], and SemanticSmooth [14 ], which control the generation trajectory through safety-aware decoding, random perturbation and aggregation, or self-evaluation with rewind. Although these runtime methods can serve as an additional layer against unseen attacks, they usually incur extra inference cost and latency, and they do not explicitly verify whether the malicious payload in the input has actually been neutralized. 

## 8 Conclusion 

We study black-box jailbreak defense for large language models, with a focus on improving robustness under heterogeneous and previously unseen attacks while keeping benign false positives at a usable level. To this end, we propose DoubtProbe , a two-branch inference-time defense pipeline whose central idea is structural verification: instead of requiring the model to perfectly resist de-ception at the parsing stage, it allows adversarial wrapping to pass through compression and reconstruction, and then detects the re-sulting element loss and mismatch through comparison with the DoubtProbe : Black-Box Jailbreak Defense via Structural Verification and Semantic Auditing 

original request, together with semantic auditing. Experiments on JBB, CodeAttack, AlpacaEval, and OR-Bench show that Doubt-Probe achieves a stronger and more stable defense–utility trade-off than representative black-box baselines, and this overall pattern remains stable when the defense-side backbone is replaced from Qwen2.5-72B to Llama-3.1-70B. These results suggest that struc-tural verification can serve as a robust first-principles anchor for black-box jailbreak defense, and provides a promising path toward more general and reliable defenses beyond attack-specific patching.

## B Ethical Considerations 

This work studies defenses against jailbreak attacks on large lan-guage models. As a result, the evaluation necessarily involves harmful prompts, adversarially wrapped requests, adaptive attack pipelines, and representative failure cases. These elements create potential ethical risks, including dual-use concerns, unnecessary exposure to harmful operational details, and possible misuse of released artifacts to improve attacks. We take several steps to reduce these risks. First, the goal of this work is defensive: the paper proposes and evaluates a black-box jailbreak defense framework rather than a new attack method. The attack-side experiments are included only to assess defensive robustness under controlled evaluation settings. Second, the bench-mark data used in the paper come from existing public research resources and are used only for scientific evaluation rather than for operational deployment or attack development. Third, when pre-senting example prompts, responses, traces, and released result files, we apply minimal redaction to harmful operational content where needed, so as to reduce unnecessary disclosure while preserving the information required for scientific evaluation and reproducibility. We also seek to reduce risks in artifact release. The anonymous artifact is intended to support evaluation of the defense pipeline, prompts, configurations, and reported results, rather than to facil-itate harmful use. In particular, the released repository includes pre-computed evaluation outputs, but harmful model replies are minimally redacted where necessary. More generally, we priori-tize releasing defensive components, evaluation scripts, derived summaries, and minimally redacted outputs over unnecessary re-distribution of more directly reusable harmful content. The scope of the paper is explicitly limited. Our threat model focuses on text-only black-box jailbreak defense on the input side. It does not cover multimodal prompts, retrieval-time injection, tool-use environments, or computer-use agents. The proposed method should therefore not be interpreted as a complete safeguard for broader deployment settings. We state these boundaries explicitly to avoid overclaiming the defensive coverage of the method. Finally, this work remains subject to a residual dual-use risk: clearer characterization of defense failure modes may also help future attackers reason about weaknesses in current safeguards. We believe that the defensive value of systematically studying these weaknesses and proposing a concrete mitigation framework out-weighs this risk. We therefore present the work with the goal of supporting safer deployment, more rigorous evaluation, and more transparent analysis of LLM jailbreak defenses.

## I.1 Representative Cases 

To complement the benchmark-level results, we examine represen-tative successful and failed cases of the full DoubtProbe system. The goal of this section is to show, at the instance level, why the method succeeds on some attacks and where its remaining difficulty lies. 

I.1.1 Successful Cases. 

Structure-Heavy Success under Code Wrapping. The first success-ful case concerns a representative CodeAttack sample. In this case, the harmful objective is embedded in a code-completion artifact and appears on the surface as an ordinary programming task. As a result, DoubtProbe -SA treats the input as benign and answers it, whereas DoubtProbe -SV, DoubtProbe -CI, and the full system all block it. The saved evidence shows that the original input contains multiple concrete code blocks, while the reconstructed request ˆ𝑄 

collapses them into a generic code-completion description. This loss of structure-level detail creates a mismatch strong enough to trigger blocking. The case shows that structural verification con-tributes a signal that standalone semantic auditing does not recover on its own. More details are given in §I.2.1. 

Semantic-Side Success on Search-Generated Harmful Prompting. 

The second successful case concerns a search-based prompt with strong output-format constraints. Although the request contains optimization artifacts, the harmful objective remains semantically recoverable along the transformation 𝑄 → 𝑆 → ˆ𝑄 . Accordingly, 

DoubtProbe -SV answers the request, whereas DoubtProbe -SA, 

DoubtProbe -CI, and the full system all block it. In the saved traces, both 𝑆 and ˆ𝑄 preserve the core goal, and the blocking evidence is tied to the harmful intent, with little indication of reconstruction inconsistency. This case shows that some optimized attacks remain highly transparent at the semantic level. Under this condition, the full system succeeds primarily because of semantic auditing. More details are given in §I.2.2. 

I.1.2 Failed Cases. 

Failure on Structurally Stable Political Persuasion Request. The first failed case concerns a discourse-driven request whose objective remains simple and is faithfully preserved under reconstruction. In this case, the full system, DoubtProbe -SV, DoubtProbe -SA, and DoubtProbe -CI all answer the request. The saved traces show that both 𝑆 and ˆ𝑄 preserve the core goal with little sign of omis-sion, attenuation, or distortion. This indicates that the miss is not driven by information loss along 𝑄 → 𝑆 → ˆ𝑄 . At the same time, the request does not trigger a blocking semantic alarm under the current semantic auditing criterion, because it remains a politi-cally sensitive persuasion request without an explicitly disallowed objective. This case therefore highlights a remaining weakness of the current system on discourse-driven political requests that stay structurally stable after reconstruction and also do not cross the present semantic blocking threshold. More details are given in §I.2.3. 

Failure under Fluent Iterative Wrapper. The second failed case concerns a PAIR-style fluent iterative prompt. Here, the request remains coherent and stable under reconstruction, and the full sys-tem, DoubtProbe -SV, DoubtProbe -SA, and DoubtProbe -CI all answer it. The saved traces show that both 𝑆 and ˆ𝑄 preserve the request with high fidelity, which leaves little structural mismatch for structural verification to exploit. The semantic path also fails to issue a blocking verdict because the request sits near a policy bound-ary: it is framed as fictional writing, while still asking for procedural detail about unlawful evasion. This case therefore constitutes a bor-derline full-system failure under a fluent iterative wrapper, and captures a persistent difficulty of the current system on prompts whose harmfulness is ambiguous and remains structurally stable under reconstruction. More details are given in §I.2.4. Taken together, these cases reveal two related failure patterns. The strongest successes of DoubtProbe arise when either struc-tural mismatch or direct semantic recoverability provides a clear signal. The remaining failures appear on discourse-driven requests whose harmful goal stays surface-coherent under faithful recon-struction and does not trigger an adverse semantic verdict. ## J Adaptive Attack Details 

This appendix summarizes the implementation details of the adap-tive attack evaluation in §5.4, including the success criteria used for each attacker, the rationale for the PAIR reporting threshold, and the concrete attack configurations. 

## J.1 Success Criteria 

For LLM-Fuzzer and AutoDAN-GA, we use the same adaptive oracle. A candidate prompt is counted as a successful jailbreak if and only if the full DoubtProbe pipeline returns decision == PASS and the downstream jailbreak judge labels the returned response as 

answered . In these runs, judge_on_pass_only=True , so blocked candidates are not eligible for success. For PAIR, the paper-facing success criterion uses best_score 

≥ 9.

## J.2 Why We Use best_score ≥ 9 for PAIR 

In the official PAIR implementation, a jailbreak is typically treated as successful only when best_score = 10 . However, in our actual runs, no case reached score 10. We found that the official 1–10 scoring description is relatively coarse for intermediate values: it provides clear endpoints for low-score and high-score cases, but leaves substantial room for the LLM judge to interpret the middle range. After manually inspecting representative responses at dif-ferent score levels, we found that responses with best_score = 9 

already correspond to cases where the target model has essentially begun returning harmful content. We therefore adopt best_score 

≥ 9 as the final reporting criterion in the paper. 

## J.3 Attack Models 

The attack model for PAIR is Vicuna-13B-v1.5. For LLM-Fuzzer and AutoDAN-GA, we use GPT-4o as the auxiliary mutation or optimization model.
