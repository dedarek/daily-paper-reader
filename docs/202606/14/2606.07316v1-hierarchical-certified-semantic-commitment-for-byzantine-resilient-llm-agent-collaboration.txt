## Abstract 

Byzantine collaboration among large-language-model agents re-quires a finality-control primitive: given a delivered set of stochastic, structured natural-language proposals, the protocol must decide whether the round supports a commit, what kind of commit, or a typed safe abort. Naive aggregation hides this choice behind a single verdict; classical Byzantine fault tolerance hides it behind byte-identity that LLM proposals do not satisfy. We introduce Hier-archical Certified Semantic Commitment (H-CSC), a BFT-inspired protocol that converts embedding-derived finality signals over verdict-conditioned proposal groups into one of three typed out-comes: a semantic_commit (a 2𝑓 +1 within-verdict semantic core backs the verdict, and the protocol emits a parameter-bound di-gest over the quantised aggregate), a verdict_commit (the verdict has a strong margin but the within-verdict semantic rationale is dispersed, and the protocol emits a verdict-level certificate with-out claiming a semantic aggregate), or an explicit abort with a typed reason. The contribution is typed finality, not raw com-mit accuracy. We evaluate H-CSC on two complementary bench-marks. On a controlled semantic-poisoning diagnostic (BCS_v1, 120 

episodes), the H-CSC implementation commits with low angular deviation on the BFT-feasible buckets ( 0.31 ◦–2.04 ◦, sub-degree on three of four buckets; the upper bound is a no-Byzantine diagnostic floor), validating the controlled semantic-separation branch. On beyond-BFT diagnostic buckets ( 𝑛 < 3𝑓 +1), the implementation guard aborts 100% of rounds, illustrating the intended refusal be-haviour outside the model’s feasibility region. On a real LLM-agent claim-verification benchmark (MVR-50, 50 tasks, Climate-FEVER fallback) under paired static and rushing Byzantine attacks, H-CSC commits 0.90 static / 0.92 rushing with honest-reference-invalid commit rates ( invalid_hmaj , see Definition 3.7) of 0.02 / 0.00 and is not statistically separated from a strong fair certificate-emitting verdict-only baseline (B3 certificate_wrapped_majority , com-mit rate 0.88 / 0.92 ) on the headline coverage and safety axes under the reported task-level 95% bootstrap CIs (CI widths 0.14 –0.28 at 

𝑛 =50 ). What the verdict-only baselines cannot produce, however, is the embedding-backed semantic_commit digest: H-CSC emits one on 74% /72% of rounds, supplying typed semantic provenance the verdict path is silent on. A strict-semantic ablation of the same framework commits only 0.54 /0.48 , demonstrating that the verdict-level fallback is necessary for coverage ( +0.36 /+ 0.44 ) at the same 

≤ 0.04 safety floor. A 100-task cross-model robustness check across four agent LLMs further preserves invalid_hmaj within 0.00 –0.03 .

## 1 Introduction 

Multi-agent systems built on large language models (LLMs) are increasingly used for tasks where the output of agreement is a 

natural-language artefact : a verdict on a scientific claim, a triage recommendation, a high-level plan. Such artefacts are not the byte-identical values that classical Byzantine fault tolerance (BFT) takes for granted. Two honest agents asked the same question, given the same evidence, and prompted with the same profile, will routinely emit textually distinct strings whose semantic content is the same. A protocol that needs to commit to one “answer” for the round must therefore reason about proposals as semantic objects, not as bytes. Classical BFT protocols (PBFT [ 5], HotStuff [ 29 ], and successors) provide deterministic finality for discrete values: a quorum of 2𝑓 +1

distinct signers on a single byte-string, which under |B| ≤ 𝑓 con-tains at least 𝑓 +1 honest signatures, is sufficient. Recent work on multi-agent LLM voting and debate offers the dual extreme [ 6, 10 ,18 ]: a soft aggregation step (majority verdict, confidence-weighted verdict, or a learned judge) with no certificate, no abort behaviour, and no formal connection between the aggregation and the under-lying semantic content. Neither extreme is adequate for LLM-agent systems where downstream consumers need a verifier-checkable handle on which verdict was committed under which parameters by which signer set, and where a Byzantine fraction of the agents may produce plausible but semantically poisoned proposals [ 7, 12 ]. 

Byzantine semantic poisoning is a real failure mode. A Byzan-tine LLM agent does not need to be “broken”; it can simply be prompted to produce proposals that are fluent, on-topic, and lexi-cally close to the honest mass while conveying the opposite verdict. The textual-attack literature has long shown that small, semanti-cally meaningful perturbations of fluent text can flip a downstream classifier [ 2 , 13 , 16 ], and prompt-injection studies show the same phenomenon propagates across agentic LLM workflows [ 7, 15 ]. We empirically show below that a rushing Byzantine (one that observes the honest agents’ broadcasts before submitting its own) can tar-get the gap left by the honest mass on every single round of our 50-task real-agent benchmark, and that the de-facto naive baseline, confidence-weighted voting, then commits a verdict that disagrees with the honest mass on 12% of rounds. 

The problem we address. Given 𝑛 LLM agents of which up to 𝑓 are Byzantine, a round of natural-language proposals, and a determinis-tic semantic encoder (e.g. a sentence-transformer style embedding model [ 8 , 27 ]), the protocol must decide what kind of finality the round supports and publish exactly one of three typed outcomes: (i) a semantic_commit : a quantised within-verdict embedding aggregate, its digest, the protocol parameters that produced it, and a 2𝑓 +1 distinct-signer quorum certificate; (ii) a verdict_commit : the certified candidate verdict (with a 

2𝑓 +1 quorum and a margin of at least one over the runner-up) and a 2𝑓 +1 distinct-signer certificate over a verdict-only digest, with no semantic aggregate claimed; or (iii) an explicit abort with a typed reason. The protocol’s typed-decision logic reads only embedding-derived and verdict-level finality signals : verdict-group sizes and margins, within-verdict semantic admissibility (the within-verdict embed-ding ball of radius 𝜃 𝛼 must contain a 2𝑓 +1 subset), and certificate feasibility ( ≥ 2𝑓 +1 distinct valid signatures over the candidate digest). It does not consult honest, gold, evidence- truth, source-reliability, or external-judge signals. The empirical honest-reference validity (reported as invalid_hmaj for backward compatibility; see Definition 3.7) of committed rounds is reported in Section 6.2; it is not promised unconditionally. 

Approach. We propose Hierarchical Certified Semantic Commit-ment (H-CSC), a finality-control protocol that operates on a de-livered set of structured agent proposals and produces a typed commit object. Each agent submits a proposal with a canonical verdict field, a confidence value, an evidence-id list, a rationale, and a claim text. The protocol is a four-stage pipeline plus a typed-decision step: (1) deterministic encoding of each proposal’s canon-ical text into a unit-norm semantic embedding; (2) grouping by verdict and selection of a candidate verdict group 𝐺 𝑣 ★ ; (3) extrac-tion of an admissible within-verdict semantic core (a 2𝑓 +1 subset of 𝐺 𝑣 ★ whose embeddings sit inside an angular ball of radius 𝜃 𝛼 ); (4) robust geometric-median aggregation [ 3, 21 , 26 ], lattice quanti-sation, and parameter-bound digest. The typed-decision step then produces one of three outcomes: a semantic_commit (the within-verdict semantic core is admissible and the protocol emits a 2𝑓 +1

certificate over the embedding-backed digest), a verdict_commit 

(the candidate verdict group has a 2𝑓 +1 quorum and a margin of at least one over the runner-up, and the protocol emits a 2𝑓 +1

certificate over a verdict-only digest with no semantic aggregate), or an explicit abort with a typed reason (Section 5). The hierarchical typed-commit shape is the key conceptual move. A binary commit-or-abort framing forces the protocol to abort whenever the embedding cluster across the full delivered view is too dispersed to admit a single 2𝑓 +1 semantic core, even when the underlying verdicts agree by a wide margin. H-CSC instead recognises that the verdict and the embedding geometry are two distinct finality signals. When both are admissible, the protocol commits with embedding-backed provenance ( semantic_commit ); when only the verdict quorum is admissible, the protocol commits with a verdict-only digest ( verdict_commit ); when neither is admis-sible, the protocol aborts. Each of the three outcomes is certified under the same 2𝑓 +1 distinct-signer envelope; only the underlying digest changes. The protocol never commits a digest without a quo-rum certificate, and never claims a semantic aggregate when the within-verdict semantic core is not admissible. H-CSC is therefore not majority vote: a vote selects an answer; H-CSC certifies the level of finality the system is justified to claim.

## 2 Related Work 

Our work is situated in the emerging regime where heterogeneous LLM agents collaborate in open environments, and the system must nevertheless commit to a single, auditable outcome. The closest threads of prior work address (i) multi-agent LLM collaboration and answer aggregation, (ii) security threats in agentic multi-LLM workflows, and (iii) Byzantine agreement / robust aggregation be-yond byte-identical values. We review them through the lens of what remains missing for protocol-grade semantic commit .

Multi-agent LLM collaboration and aggregation. A growing body of work studies multi-agent LLM collaboration (e.g., debate, delib-eration, and committee-style workflows) as a mechanism to im-prove task performance and reliability [ 10 , 17 , 18 , 25 ]. In practice, H. Xu et al. 

these systems frequently consolidate multiple stochastic outputs via voting or pooling to produce a single answer. Recent evaluation frameworks explicitly adopt majority vote / pooling over many LLM judgments, highlighting both the prevalence of aggregation and the operational impact of LLM non-determinism [ 30 ]. Con-currently, a line of work studies principled aggregation rules for combining multiple LLM outputs beyond naive majority voting, typically optimizing expected accuracy under statistical models of judge reliability [ 1]. These efforts, however, are not designed as distributed consensus protocols: they generally assume a trusted coordinator or benign participants and do not provide Byzantine safety/liveness-style guarantees for committing a single outcome in an open network. 

Threats in agentic multi-LLM systems. As LLM collaboration becomes agentic and tool-integrated, adversarial participation becomes a first-order concern. Recent studies demonstrate that prompt injection can propagate across interconnected agents (LLM-to-LLM infection), enabling persistent compromise of a multi-agent workflow [ 15 ]. Surveys and systematizations further categorize threats in LLM-agent ecosystems, including cross-agent injection and protocol-layer vulnerabilities, and argue for layered defenses and verification in deployed agentic systems [ 7, 12 ]. These works motivate the need for protocol mechanisms that remain correct under adversarially crafted semantic artifacts; however, they focus on attack surfaces and mitigations at the agent/workflow level, rather than providing a consensus primitive that deterministically commits to a single semantic outcome under Byzantine faults. 

Byzantine agreement and robust aggregation beyond exact equal-ity. Classical BFT protocols (e.g., PBFT, HotStuff) provide safety and liveness over byte-identical values, while relaxations such as approximate agreement address non-identical numeric vectors. In distributed learning, recent work emphasizes that robust aggrega-tion must be coupled with an agreement subroutine, and revisits multidimensional approximate agreement for geometric-median-based aggregation under Byzantine behavior [ 22 ]. Separately, initial attempts have begun to connect BFT-style mechanisms with multi-LLM collaboration, e.g., proposing blockchain-driven weighted BFT voting for multi-LLM networks [ 19 ]. More broadly, consensus-inspired formulations have been explored for robustness in com-municative multi-agent systems under communication attacks [ 20 ]. Nevertheless, these directions do not address the specific obstacle central to semantic commit : an LLM-agent round delivers stochastic natural-language proposals, and the protocol must decide what kind of finality the round supports rather than always producing one verdict. H-CSC differs by introducing a typed commit object 

(semantic_commit / verdict_commit / abort ): each typed outcome carries the same 2𝑓 +1 certificate envelope but binds to a different underlying digest, so verifiers can distinguish embedding-backed agreements from verdict-only agreements and from typed safe aborts. Earlier formulations of this work explored topology-based filtering as the central mechanism; we now report that exploration in Appendix C as a design-space ablation only, because on every measured benchmark it produces commit/abort decisions either identical to the H-CSC main path or strictly more conservative without improving honest-reference validity. 

## 3 System Model and Problem Formulation 

We consider a distributed system consisting of a set of 𝑛 nodes 

N = {1, . . . , 𝑛 }, where a subset B ⊂ N of size at most 𝑓 may be Byzantine. We assume 𝑛 ≥ 3𝑓 + 1, which is the tight bound for deterministic Byzantine agreement in partially synchronous sys-tems [ 11 , 14 ]. This bound is both necessary and sufficient: with fewer than 3𝑓 + 1 nodes, an adversary controlling 𝑓 Byzantine nodes can partition honest nodes into two groups that commit con-flicting values, violating safety [ 14 ]. The 3𝑓 + 1 threshold has been foundational since Lamport et al.’s seminal work on the Byzantine Generals Problem [ 14 ] and remains the standard fault tolerance limit for protocols requiring exact agreement under partial syn-chrony [11]. 

## 3.1 Network and Communication Primitives 

Network Model. We assume the standard partial synchrony model [ 11 ]. There exist an unknown Global Stabilization Time (GST) and an unknown delay bound Δ such that after GST, all mes-sages sent between honest nodes are delivered within time Δ. Nodes do not know GST nor Δ a priori and may use increasing timeouts. Nodes communicate via authenticated point-to-point channels and have access to a Public Key Infrastructure (PKI).

## 3.3 Adversary Model 

We consider a Byzantine adversary that controls the set of faulty nodes B with |B| ≤ 𝑓 . We evaluate the protocol under two ad-versary modes: static , where Byzantine submissions are generated independently of the honest broadcasts in the same round, and rush-ing , where the Byzantine reads the round’s honest broadcasts before submitting (subject to the network and cryptographic constraints below). Rushing is the strict worst case for our analysis; both modes are evaluated paired in Section 6. The semantic-poisoning capability formalised in Definition 3.8 is independent of the timing mode. 

Capabilities. Byzantine nodes may deviate arbitrarily from the protocol, including choosing arbitrary texts 𝑥 𝑏 ∈ X and coordinat-ing across corrupted nodes. We focus on semantic poisoning behav-ior, where the adversary crafts inputs that are intended to distort the collectively committed meaning while remaining embedding-proximal to honest inputs (i.e., difficult to filter using geometry alone). 

Cryptographic and communication constraints. Nodes commu-nicate over authenticated point-to-point channels and signatures are unforgeable. RBC is used for disseminating round inputs. RBC does not prevent Byzantine senders from attempting equivocation, but it guarantees that honest nodes cannot end up with conflicting delivered values from the same sender (Agreement), nor can only a strict subset of honest nodes deliver a value from that sender (Totality/Uniform delivery). Thus, any successful round proceeds from a consistent delivered view.

## 4.2 Proposal canonicalisation and encoding 

A proposal 𝑧 𝑖 = (𝑠 𝑖 , 𝜌 𝑖 ) is built per agent. The structured part 𝜌 𝑖 has the canonical fields 

(verdict , confidence , evidence ids , rationale , claim text ). The en-coder E maps 𝜌 𝑖 to a unit-norm embedding via a deterministic canonicalisation of 𝜌 𝑖 followed by a transformer-based sentence encoder [ 8, 27 ]; we use CRSE (a Byzantine-robust trained encoder) by default and document the encoder identity in the protocol parameters 𝜋 so that two honest signers reach byte-identical embeddings. Importantly, the encoder reads only the canonical proposal text (a deterministic serialisation of the structured fields); the evidence-id list is part of that serialisation but is not consulted by the protocol’s typed-decision step (Remark 3.3). 

## 4.3 Verdict grouping 

The protocol partitions 𝑉 (𝑟 ) into verdict groups {𝐺 𝑣 }𝑣 with 𝐺 𝑣 =

{𝑖 ∈ 𝑉 (𝑟 ) : 𝜌 𝑖 .verdict = 𝑣 }. The candidate verdict group is selected by the candidate_rule parameter, fixed to largest_verdict for the H-CSC main path: 𝑣 ★ = arg max 𝑣 |𝐺 𝑣 | with deterministic tiebreak by the fixed priority support > refute > insufficient (applied only on exact ties). The candidate group’s verdict_margin is the difference between the largest and the second-largest group counts. If |𝐺 𝑣 ★ | < 2𝑓 +1 the protocol immediately emits Oabort with typed reason verdict_below_quorum and halts. 

## 4.4 Within-verdict semantic finality 

Within 𝐺 𝑣 ★ , the protocol runs the angular_component core ex-traction at radius 𝜃 𝛼 : the largest connected component of the angular- threshold graph {( 𝑖, 𝑗 ) : ∠(e𝑖 , e𝑗 ) ≤ 𝜃 𝛼 }. If the largest component has size < 2𝑓 +1, the within- verdict semantic core check fails. Otherwise, the admissibility predicate of Definition 3.5 is evaluated on the selected component: there must exist a centre node ℎ ∈ b𝐶 such that ∠(eℎ, e𝑗 ) ≤ 𝜃 𝛼 for every 𝑗 ∈ b𝐶 . If the predi-cate fails, the within-verdict semantic core check fails. The H-CSC default uses 𝜃 𝛼 = 0.65 rad ( ≈ 37 .2◦), wider than the strict-semantic CSC’s 𝜃 𝛼 = 0.55 rad to accommodate honest agents that agree on the verdict but use different phrasings within the rationale. 

## 4.5 Hierarchical typed-decision step 

The typed-decision step combines the verdict-level and embedding-level signals: (1) Verdict-quorum gate. If |𝐺 𝑣 ★ | < 2𝑓 +1, output Oabort 

with reason verdict_below_quorum (already triggered in the verdict-grouping subsection above). (2) Semantic-path attempt. If the within-verdict seman-tic core is admissible (size ≥ 2𝑓 +1 and the radius predi-cate passes), proceed to the semantic-commit construction (§4.6). (3) Verdict-fallback gate. Otherwise, if verdict_margin ≥ 1

(the margin_1 fallback condition, fixed in the H-CSC main path), proceed to the verdict-commit construction. (4) Both paths failed. Otherwise, output Oabort with a typed reason recording which gates failed. The fallback condition margin ≥ 1 is a calibrated empirical operating point, not a deterministic honest-reference guarantee: with 𝑛 = 10 , 𝑓 = 2, and top_count (𝑉 ) ≥ 2𝑓 +1 = 5, margin ≥ 1

selects a unique top verdict group and empirically recovers coverage on real-LLM rounds whose honest agents agree at the verdict level but disperse at the rationale level. Deterministic honest-reference validity would require verdict_margin min > 𝑓 instead, as proven in Theorem 5.3 (Section 5). We retain margin_1 as the operating point because (i) it preserves coverage on rounds the deterministic 

𝑚 > 𝑓 condition would abort, and (ii) the empirical invalid_hmaj 

rate at 𝑚 = 1 stays at or below 0.04 across all MVR-50 and MVR-100 benchmarks (Section 6.2).

The previous CSC formulation ( candidate_rule disabled, fallback =

## 4.10 Why H-CSC is not majority vote 

H-CSC differs from majority_vote and from

for the verdict path; majority_vote commits regardless. ## 4.11 Topology as a design-space option 

For completeness, the implementation accepts an optional topology gate topology_gate ∈ {⊥ , jaccard , jaccard_kcore }. The H-CSC main path uses topology_gate = ⊥. We report the Jaccard variant in Appendix C as a design-space ablation only; on every measured benchmark and at every 𝜃 𝛼 in our sensitivity sweep, the topology gate produces commit/abort decisions identical to the H-CSC main path or aborts more without improving honest-reference validity. Topology is not part of the H-CSC contribution. 

## 5 Correctness Analysis 

We split correctness into five axes: Agreement , Semantic validity , Ver-dict validity , Safe abort , and Conditional commit-or-abort termination .The split mirrors the agreement / validity / termination decomposi-tion standard in classical Byzantine agreement [ 5, 9, 14 , 29 ], with the additional safe-abort and typed-validity axes that the hierarchi-cal commit object of Definition 3.3 requires. The semantic_commit 

and verdict_commit outcomes admit distinct sufficient conditions for honest-reference validity, and the termination guarantee is now a disjunction over the three typed outcomes rather than a binary commit/abort claim. H. Xu et al. 

## 5.1 Assumptions 

Assumption 5.1 (Authenticated channels and honest sign-once). The assumption has two clauses, both required by Theo-rem 5.1 and Lemma 5.2. 

Authenticated channels. Every honest agent’s signature is un-forgeable. Two distinct signatures from the same agent on distinct digests are detectable. 

Honest sign-once. In each round 𝑟 , an honest agent signs at most one typed digest. Before signing it recomputes Algorithm 1 locally on its delivered view 𝑉 (𝑟 ) and signs only the unique digest 𝐷 ★ (𝑟 ) returned by the deterministic H-CSC decision rule; any conflicting digest for the same round is rejected without signing. 

Assumption 5.2 (Partial synchrony). There is a global stabili-sation time after which message delivery is bounded; before that time, message order and timing are adversarial. This assumption is used only for Termination. 

Assumption 5.3 (Deterministic encoder and serialisation). 

E is deterministic: two honest agents that compute E ( 𝜌 ) for the same canonical proposal text produce identical e. The canonicalisation of 

𝜌 is byte-deterministic in the structured fields. The quantiser 𝑄 𝜂 is a fixed-point lattice round.

The four theorems above are conditional on the encoder E being able to separate honest from Byzantine proposals at least to within the within-verdict admissibility radius 𝜃 𝛼 .H. Xu et al.

𝜌 𝑏 .verdict and ∠(E ( 𝜌 ℎ ), E ( 𝜌 𝑏 )) = 0. Then no deterministic embedding-only commitment protocol that operates only on E can distinguish 𝜌 ℎ from 𝜌 𝑏 on the within-verdict admissibility predicate; any such protocol must either (i) admit 𝜌 𝑏 to the selected core, or (ii) abort.

Remark 5.5 . Lemma 5.3 explains why H-CSC does not fully elim-inate the B4-style on-topic-hallucination failure mode reported in Section 6.2: a Byzantine proposal with the same verdict and a near-identical embedding to honest text cannot be distinguished by the embedding filter. ## 5.5 What we do not prove 

We do not prove a closed-form bound on the angular distance from 

y★ to a ground-truth reference. We prove only the relative bound of Theorem 5.2 for the semantic_commit path; the empirical SCE numbers in Section 6 are the corresponding observation. We do 

not prove robustness against an adversary that manipulates E’s weights, an adversary that colludes across rounds, or an adversary that violates Assumption 5.1. We do not prove that H-CSC strictly dominates fair verdict-only baselines on commit rate or invalid-commit rate. A task-level bootstrap analysis ( 10 000 resamples, seed 42 ; see Appendix D) confirms that the H-CSC and B3 certificate_wrapped_majority 

95% CIs overlap on every headline axis (commit rate, invalid_hmaj ,valid-commit coverage, byz_infiltration ); on rushing mode the CIs are identical. On static invalid_hmaj , B3’s CI is [0.000 , 0.000 ]

and H-CSC’s is [0.000 , 0.060 ], making B3 uniformly safer in the bootstrap sense by a single task. H-CSC’s contribution over B3 is therefore not a coverage or safety improvement but the typed 

semantic_commit outcome and its embedding-backed digest, which B3 cannot produce by construction. We do not prove evidence-aware safety properties (evidence-id provenance soundness, source-reliability guarantees, claim– evidence entailment correctness). H-CSC’s correctness is silent on these axes; they are out of scope for the present paper (Remark 3.3).

Distinction from multi-agent debate / aggregation. The closest LLM-side prior is multi-agent debate and aggregation [ 1, 6, 10 , 18 ], which combines stochastic LLM outputs through soft-aggregation or learned-judge mechanisms. ## 7 Limitations and Threats to Validity 

We state the limitations of the work explicitly, in the order in which a sceptical reader is most likely to raise them. 

1. Climate-FEVER fallback rather than SciFact. The MVR-50 benchmark uses Climate-FEVER as the documented fallback dataset; raw SciFact files are not part of our current experimental pipeline. Climate-FEVER’s gold “claim_label” is a community judgment that exceeds what a strict evidence-bound reading of the visible evidence supports on 14 of 50 tasks ( 28% ). This is the principal reason we report evidence-bounded honest-reference validity (invalid_hmaj ;see Definition 3.7) alongside dataset-gold validity: the former is the protocol-side correctness signal; the latter is a dataset-quality diagnostic. The schema and loaders are dataset-agnostic; SciFact instantiation requires only the raw files and is future work. 

2. Benchmark scale. We evaluate at two complementary scales: MVR-50 ( 50 tasks × 10 agents × 2 attack modes, single LLM) as the calibration benchmark, and MVR-100 ( 100 tasks × 10 agents ×

2 attack modes, four LLMs) as the cross-model robustness bench-mark (§6.3). Strong-scale evaluation at ≥ 500 tasks and within-round Hierarchical Certified Semantic Commitment for Byzantine-Resilient LLM-Agent Collaboration 

multi-vendor heterogeneity (agents from different vendors partici-pating in the same round) remain future work. The bootstrap CIs reported in §6.2–§6.3 capture per-task variance but not per-seed variance. 

3. Between-run cross-model, not within-round heterogeneity. The cross-model evaluation in §6.3 covers four LLMs ( gpt-4o-mini ,

gpt-4o , claude-sonnet-4-5 , llama-3.3-70b-instruct ) but each round uses a single homogeneous agent population. The encoder is a single trained CRSE checkpoint built on sentence-transformer-style backbones [8, 27]. Within-round vendor hetero-geneity (e.g., mixing OpenAI and Anthropic agents in the same round) and encoder-class generalisation are explicit follow-ups; the schema and protocol both already accept heterogeneous agent populations, so this is an experiment-pipeline limitation rather than a protocol limitation. 

4. 𝜃 𝛼 = 0.65 (H-CSC main path) and 𝜃 𝛼 = 0.55 (strict-semantic ablation) are both calibrated, not universal. The H-CSC main path uses 𝜃 𝛼 = 0.65 rad with the margin_1 verdict fallback, calibrated on the MVR-50 20-task pilot to recover coverage on rounds whose honest agents agree at the verdict level but disperse at the rationale level. The strict-semantic CSC ablation uses 𝜃 𝛼 = 0.55 rad with no fallback; its sensitivity analysis (§6.6) identifies a ≈ 6◦-wide stable plateau on the 20-task pilot but is specific to that ablation, not to the H-CSC main path. We do not claim either threshold is universal; both are deployment- time calibration targets, and §6.6 reports the operating regions explicitly. A full 𝜃 𝛼 × fallback sensitivity sweep on H-CSC at MVR-100 scale is future work. 

5. Two validity notions can diverge. Definition 3.7 separates dataset-gold validity from evidence-bounded honest-reference va-lidity (the invalid_hmaj metric retains its bench-tooling name but “hmaj” denotes the honest plurality with deterministic tie-break, not strict majority; see Definition 3.7). The two diverge whenever the honest plurality verdict cannot match the gold label given only the visible evidence. The protocol cannot be held responsible for divergences driven by dataset label noise. We do not claim, and will not claim, that gold-validity numbers characterise protocol correctness. 

6. Representation boundary (Lemma 5.3). If two proposals embed to identical points in the encoder’s representation, the protocol cannot distinguish them on any function of the embeddings, in-cluding the admissibility predicate. The defence in such cases is not the embedding filter but the safe-abort branch. Empirically this manifests as B4 (on-topic hallucination) cases where Byzan-tine specifics are fabricated but lexically and semantically close to honest output; on a subset of MVR-50 tasks the honest cluster is borderline-admissible at 𝜃 𝛼 = 0.55 and the protocol aborts rather than committing a poisoned digest. 

7. Topology is not an accuracy-improving main contribution. An earlier formulation of this work made Jaccard graph and 𝑘 -core peeling the central filtering mechanism. Corrected experiments (Sections 6.1, 6.2, 6.6, and 6.7) show that on every measured bench-mark the topology gate produces commit/abort decisions identical to the main path at the operating point and aborts more than the main path elsewhere. We retain topology in Appendix C as a design-space transparency artefact only. We make no main-path claim that depends on topology. 

8. Some Byzantine generations hallucinated evidence ids. 3 of the 

100 rushing Byzantine records on MVR-50 cited an evidence_id 

not present in the parent task, despite the prompt’s explicit instruc-tion not to. A strict-validation audit traced these records through the commitment evaluation and confirmed that the certified pipeline aborted both affected tasks under both attack modes; the artefacts therefore do not change any §6.2 conclusion. The runner-side vali-dation has been hardened post-hoc to catch this at write time. 

9. Logical certificate, not threshold-signature cryptography. The implementation uses a logical signature simulator that preserves per-signer uniqueness and the 2𝑓 +1 distinct-signer threshold. Pro-duction threshold-signature schemes (e.g. BLS, FROST, threshold ECDSA) are a standard substitution. We do not implement, evalu-ate, or analyse cryptographic primitives; the security claim of §5 is about the protocol’s logical structure, not about its cryptographic instantiation. 

Out-of-scope. We do not address white-box adversaries with model-weight access, cross-task collusion, multi-round delibera-tion protocols where agents iterate on each other’s proposals, or protocol-level reductions of the certified commitment object to downstream consumers. These are explicit future work. 

## 8 Conclusion 

We introduced Hierarchical Certified Semantic Commitment (H-CSC), a finality-control protocol for Byzantine-resilient LLM-agent collaboration. Rather than collapsing the per-round decision into a single commit/abort axis, H-CSC produces a typed commit object: a semantic_commit when the within-verdict embedding cluster is admissible at radius 𝜃 𝛼 , a verdict_commit when the verdict-level quorum and margin are admissible but the embedding cluster is too dispersed, or an explicit abort with a typed reason. Both com-mit types are certified under the same 2𝑓 +1 distinct-signer enve-lope; the distinction lies in whether the underlying digest binds an embedding-backed quantised aggregate or only a verdict-level payload (Algorithm 1). We evaluated H-CSC on two complementary benchmarks. The BCS_v1 controlled diagnostic (§6.1) shows that the implementation guard aborts 100% of beyond-BFT diagnostic rounds, while commit-ted BFT-feasible buckets exhibit low angular deviation ( 0.31 ◦–2.04 ◦,sub-degree for byz_ratio ≥ 0.1; the 2.04 ◦ no-Byzantine value is the diagnostic floor). The MVR-50 real LLM-agent benchmark (§6.2) places the protocol under paired static and rushing Byzantine attack modes against six aggregation baselines. H-CSC commits 0.90 /0.92 

of MVR-50 rounds with invalid_hmaj = 0.02 /0.00 , recovering cov-erage by +0.36 /+ 0.44 over a strict-semantic configuration of the same framework (§6.4) while preserving the ≤ 0.04 safety floor. A task-level bootstrap analysis ( 10 000 resamples) shows H-CSC’s 95%

## B.8 Discussion: Lemma 5.3 (Representation Boundary) 

The proof of Lemma 5.3 given in Section 5 is direct (two proposals with identical embeddings cannot be distinguished by any function of the embeddings) and does not require additional details. We include here only an extension that connects the lemma to the empirical B4 (on-topic-hallucination) failure mode reported in Sections 6.2 and 6.7. If a Byzantine proposal 𝜌 𝑏 is engineered so that ∠(E ( 𝜌 ℎ ), E ( 𝜌 𝑏 )) is small but nonzero, the admissibility predicate may either admit 𝜌 𝑏 to the selected core (Stage 2 of Algorithm 1) or push the round across the predicate’s threshold and trigger abort (admissibility_failed ). In the empirical MVR-50 evaluation, the latter outcome dominates: Byzantine embeddings that approach an honest cluster’s boundary produce 

abort , not commit. The defence in such cases is the typed safe-abort branch combined with a tighter encoder, not the embedding filter itself. Strengthening representation quality (better encoder training, content-aware features beyond the embedding) is therefore the natural axis of future improvement. 

## C Topology Design-Space Analysis 

The original PODC submission of this work made a Jaccard support-overlap graph and 𝑘 -core peeling the central filtering mechanism for Byzantine agents. After the post-PODC audit, the topology mechanism is retained in the codebase as a design-space option but is not part of the main protocol path. We describe the original motivation, the corrected finding, and the disposition here for transparency. 

## C.1 Original motivation 

Two sources of intuition motivated the topology mechanism. First, two honest agents that base their proposals on overlapping evidence cite overlapping evidence_id sets, while a Byzantine agent that fabricates citations does not. Building a Jaccard graph 𝐺 𝐽 = (𝑉 , 𝐸 ) with edges 

(𝑖, 𝑗 ) ∈ 𝐸 iff 

|𝜌 𝑖 .evidence_ids ∩ 𝜌 𝑗 .evidence_ids ||𝜌 𝑖 .evidence_ids ∪ 𝜌 𝑗 .evidence_ids | ≥ 𝜏 𝐽 

should therefore tend to keep honest agents in a single dense component while pushing Byzantines to isolated nodes or disconnected fringes. Second, 𝑘 -core peeling of 𝐺 𝐽 at 𝑘 = 𝑓 +1 should remove fringe Byzantines while preserving the honest core. 

## C.2 Corrected experimental finding 

The post-PODC audit ran the topology mechanism alongside the main path on every benchmark. The summary observation, in three parts, is reported in Table 7; the underlying topology design-space outputs are recorded in the authors’ internal audit logs, which are planned to accompany a future reproducibility package. (1) No commit/abort decision change at the operating point. On BCS_v1 across 120 episodes, on the 20-task pilot across both attack modes, on MVR-50 across both attack modes, and at every 𝜃 𝛼 in the threshold sensitivity sweep, the topology gate produces commit/abort decisions identical to the main path on every task / every grid point. The selected core size shrinks slightly (the gate trims a small number of nodes), but the round-level decision does not change. (2) Topology aborts more in the loose region. For 𝜃 𝛼 ≥ 0.65 on the 20-task pilot, the topology gate aborts 5–20 percentage points more rounds than the main path, achieving “safety by extra aborts”. This does not reduce invalid_hmaj below the main path’s in any measured cell except a single MVR-50 cell where the gate lowers invalid_hmaj from 0.02 to 0.00 at the cost of an extra 4 aborts. (3) 𝑘 -core peeling is empirically a no-op. The post-PODC audit reported zero Hard-bin Byzantines on BCS_v1 — Byzantines that are admitted by the angular filter but excluded by the 𝑘 -core filter — under the published threshold settings. We therefore removed 

𝑘 -core from the main protocol path (decision-log entry D-11 ).

## C.4 What this appendix is not 

This appendix is not a competing main-path proposal. It does not support claims of the form “topology is necessary”, “topology improves accuracy”, or “topology is a safety gate that catches what the angular filter misses”. The corrected finding is the opposite: at the chosen operating point, the topology gate is decision-redundant with the main path; outside the operating point, it trades commit rate for extra aborts without improving honest-reference validity. We report the design-space ablation here for transparency; the corrected-decision rationale is recorded in the authors’ internal decision log, which is planned to accompany a future reproducibility package.

## D.1 CRSE Training Configuration 

The Certified Robust Semantic Encoder (CRSE) is fine-tuned from the publicly-released intfloat/e5-base sentence encoder. 

• Contrastive loss: a supervised contrastive objective with an annealed temperature schedule (start 𝜏 ≈ 0.20 , target 𝜏 ≈ 0.12 ); the exact schedule and the per-stage 𝜏 values are recorded in the authors’ training configuration, which is planned to accompany a future reproducibility package. 

• Hard negatives: GPT-4o-generated semantic poisons (see §D.2), plus a per-anchor Byzantine-quota cap to prevent over-representation of any one attack instance. 

• Optimisation: AdamW with a small base learning rate ( 5 × 10 −6 at the projector and sub-10 −5 at the backbone, with a Byzantine-introduction schedule that further reduces the backbone rate in later stages), batch size 32 .

• Infrastructure: a single training device (Apple Silicon / MPS backend) for the trained checkpoint; AMP / mixed precision is config-gated. The encoder checkpoint used in Section 6 is the single trained CRSE used in all reported evaluations; the authoritative training hyperparameters are recorded in our experiment logs, and the resolved per-run config is written alongside each experiment’s outputs. The training configuration and checkpoint are retained by the authors and are planned to accompany a future reproducibility package. Multi-encoder ablation is future work. 

## D.2 Semantic Attack Generation Prompts 

We use GPT-4o (for BCS_v1) and gpt-4o-mini (for MVR-50) to generate Byzantine proposals. The system prompt below is the canonical version used for the four primary attack types B1–B4 reported in Section 6.2. System Prompt for Semantic Attacker                         

> Role: You are a Semantic Byzantine Adversary. Your goal is to generate a “poisoned” version of the input text based on a specific Attack Strategy ID .
> General Constraints:
> •High similarity: Maximise lexical overlap (cosine similarity >0.9). Use the same sentence structure and entities where possible.
> •Length: Keep output length within ±5% of the original.
> •Stealth: The output must read fluently and appear authoritative.
> Attack Strategy Definitions:
> •B1: Polarity flip. Invert the truth value or stance of the central claim (e.g. “X increases Y” →“X reduces Y”).
> •B2: Evidence omission. Selectively remove qualifiers, conditions, or limitations (e.g. delete “in most cases”) to make the claim appear absolute or biased.
> •B3: Fake causality. Distort the causal structure. Convert correlations into direct causal statements or introduce spurious causal links between existing entities without changing the topic.
> •B4: On-topic hallucination. Fabricate specific, verifiable details (dates, numbers, institution names) that support the topic but are factually unsupported by the original text.
> Example (Strategy B1): Input: “The encryption protocol guarantees forward secrecy.”
> Output: “The encryption protocol fails to guarantee forward secrecy.”

## D.3 Bootstrap Confidence Intervals 

The MVR-50 confidence intervals reported in Tables 2, 3 and Section 6.2 are computed by task-level non-parametric bootstrap with 10 000 

resamples and seed 42 . Each bootstrap sample is obtained by sampling 50 tasks (with replacement) from the original 50 -task list and recomputing the per-method, per-mode metric on the resampled task set; the reported 95% interval is the 2.5th– 97 .5th percentile of the metric distribution. The bootstrap is reproducible CPU-only; the script and CI report are retained by the authors and are planned to accompany a future reproducibility package. 

## D.4 Strict-Validation Audit and Evidence-ID Hallucination 

A strict-validation audit applied a task-aware schema check to every Byzantine record at write time, after the discovery that the original lenient validator did not catch evidence_id fields hallucinated by the LLM. The audit found 3 of 100 rushing Byzantine records on MVR-50 that cited an evidence_id not present in the parent task (none in the static-attack set). All 3 records were traced through the commitment evaluation: in each case the certified pipeline aborted the affected task under both attack modes (the certified_absorbs_attack paired class), so the hallucinations did not change any commit-level result. The runner-side validator was hardened post-hoc to reject any record citing a nonexistent evidence_id at write time; the authors’ internal audit log records the affected record IDs and is planned to accompany a future reproducibility package. The certificate Cert (ℎ★) in Algorithm 1 is implemented by a logical signature simulator that preserves per-signer uniqueness and the 2𝑓 +1
