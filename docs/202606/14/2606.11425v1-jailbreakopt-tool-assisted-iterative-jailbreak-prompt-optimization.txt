Jailbreak attacks expose persistent safety weaknesses in large language models (LLMs), but existing stateless single-turn methods face a trade-off: hand-crafted prompts are expressive but static, while iterative prompt optimization can adapt but often relies on low-level mutations that require many target queries. We propose JailbreakOPT, a tool-assisted framework for improving iterative single-turn jailbreak prompt optimization. JailbreakOPT organizes diverse atomic jailbreak prompts into an attack tool library and composes them through a unified intra-episode optimization abstraction to generate stronger standalone attack prompts. To reuse experience across attack episodes, JailbreakOPT further frames tool selection as a contextual bandit problem and applies contextual Thompson sampling to guide exploration and exploitation based on past outcomes. Experiments across multiple target LLMs and attack goals show that JailbreakOPT improves attack success rate (ASR) while reducing the number of attacks until success (No.A) compared with atomic single-turn attacks and existing iterative optimization baselines. 1 Introduction 

The safety and security of large language models (LLMs) have become increasingly important as LLM-powered applications proliferate. Despite advances in alignment and defense mechanisms, modern LLMs remain vulnerable to jailbreak attacks Yi et al. (2024); Liao et al. (2026), where adversarial prompts induce models to violate safety policies. Meanwhile, stronger defenses have rendered many earlier jailbreak techniques ineffective Lin et al. (2024), motivating systematic prompt-generation frameworks that can combine attack strategies to probe residual vulnerabilities and support automated red-teaming. We focus on the black-box compatible, stateless single-turn setting, where each target query is an independent standalone prompt. This scope separates two concepts often conflated in prior jailbreak discussions: atomic single-turn methods , which use fixed templates or transformations without search, and iterative single-turn optimization methods , which refine candidate prompts over multiple attempts while still exposing only one standalone prompt to the target at a time. This setting differs from stateful multi-turn conversational jailbreaks ,which rely on accumulated dialogue context to expose context-dependent safety failures Li et al. (2026). Thus, our goal is not to benchmark conversational attacks, but to maximize the capability of stateless single-turn jailbreak optimization. Within this scope, existing approaches have complementary limitations. Atomic single-turn attacks are expressive but static, making them brittle once guardrails suppress fixed patterns. Iterative single-turn methods adapt to feedback, but often rely on low-level or generic mutation operators that require many target queries. We formulate jailbreak prompt generation as budget-constrained optimization, aiming to maximize attack success rate (ASR) while minimizing the number of attacks until success (No.A). Inspired by reinforcement learning, an attack episode denotes one complete prompt-optimization process under the same attack goal, target model, and evaluation configuration. Existing methods mainly optimize within a single episode and do not systematically reuse experience across episodes. To address these gaps, we propose JailbreakOPT (Figure 1), a unified framework for efficient and scalable jailbreak prompt generation. Our contributions are: 1

2 Related Work 

Appendix Table 3 summarizes how representative iterative single-turn optimization algorithms instantiate the unified abstraction used in this work. 

Single-turn jailbreak attacks cover both atomic attacks and iterative prompt optimization under a stateless target-query interface. Atomic methods use one standalone prompt, often constructed through templates or transformations. In our experiments, this family is represented by Code Chameleon Lv et al. (2024), Flip Attack Liu et al. (2024b), and Persuasion Attack Zeng et al. (2024). Iterative single-turn optimization methods instead search over candidate standalone prompts, but each candidate remains an independent target query. We compare against representative optimization baselines used in prior jailbreak studies, including TAP Mehrotra et al. (2024), AutoDAN Liu et al. (2024a), and GCG Zou et al. (2023a). These methods motivate our unified abstraction in Section 3.1, while our tool-assisted design asks whether expressive atomic tools can improve iterative single-turn optimization. 

Stateful multi-turn conversational jailbreaks target a different threat model: they rely on an ongoing dialogue in which the target LLM maintains conversation context, and the attack unfolds through accumulated interaction history Li et al. (2026). Such methods are valuable for detecting context-dependent safety failures, but they are not directly comparable under our stateless single-turn benchmark, where every target query is evaluated independently. Representative examples include Crescendo, which gradually escalates benign conversational context into harmful requests Russinovich et al. (2024), and multi-round agent or human jailbreak settings that adapt across dialogue turns Li et al. (2024); Wang et al. (2024). 3 Method 

In this section, we present JailbreakOPT, a unified framework for jailbreak prompt generation in the stateless single-turn setting. We first introduce a high-level abstraction for iterative single-turn optimization, then instantiate its action space with an attack tool library composed of diverse atomic single-turn jailbreak methods. Finally, we describe how completed optimization episodes are converted into trajectory-level experience for inter-episode learning.  

> 3.1 A Unified Abstraction for Iterative Single-Turn Optimization

We study iterative single-turn optimization algorithms that optimize standalone attack prompts by (i) selecting mutation actions, (ii) generating candidates, (iii) evaluating each candidate independently against a target model, and (iv) retaining promising candidates for further refinement. Methods such as PAIR, GCG, AutoDAN, and TAP share this common iterative search structure, even though the target model receives only one standalone prompt per attempt.  

> Frontier and action pool.

At iteration t, the algorithm maintains a frontier Ft ⊆ X of candidate prompts and a pool of mutation actions (attack tools) A = a1, . . . , a m, where each action specifies a mutation type (e.g., LLM-based rewriting, token edits).  

> Orchestrator.

The orchestrator assigns mutation actions to frontier elements, producing a (multi)set of assignments 

Pt ≜ O(Ft, A, Ht),

where each ρ ∈ P t denotes assigning some action(s) from A to some element(s) of Ft with an optional action history Ht. Notably, allowing a many-to-many mapping makes the definition more generalizable.  

> Mutator.

The mutator executes each assignment ρ to generate a candidate prompt, 

x′ ← M (ρ),

yielding the candidate set 

Ct ≜ { M (ρ) : ρ ∈ P t }. 

> Evaluator.

The evaluator queries the target model T and returns, for each x ∈ C t,

E : (x, T ) 7 → (s(x; T ), e (x; T )) .

3where s(x; T ) is a scalar score for ranking or pruning and e(x; T ) contains auxiliary outputs (e.g., model response, refusal signal, or analysis). A binary success indicator Success (e(x; T )) ∈ { 0, 1} can be derived when needed.  

> Selector.

The selector constructs the next frontier and optionally updates the search context: 

S : {(x, s, e )} 7 → (Ft+1 , Ht+1 ),

updating Ht+1 with history, statistics, or memory used in subsequent iterations.  

> Generality.

Within this abstraction, PAIR, GCG, AutoDAN, and TAP differ only in their instantiation of actions and modules; Table 3 summarizes these mappings. Inspired by modular attack toolkits such as TextAttack, this abstraction in Algorithm 1 extends beyond these methods: beam search and particle swarm optimization can also be expressed by this abstraction.  

> 3.2 Constructing and Applying an Attack Tool Library

Figure 2: Illustration of multi-tool attack chains. An atomic single-turn attack applies one tool to the attack goal, whereas JailbreakOPT composes multiple tools into an attack chain that still yields a standalone target prompt. Following the unified abstraction in Section 3.1, the action pool can be instantiated as an attack tool library ,provided that a corresponding mutation procedure is defined. In this work, we curate a library of widely used atomic single-turn jailbreak tools; implementation details are provided in Appendix A.2. Each tool is treated as an atomic action that can be selected by the orchestrator and applied by the mutator.  

> Single-tool abstraction.

Each atomic attack tool is represented as an encoding–decoding pair. The encoding 

maps an explicit malicious attack goal x into a benign-looking user prompt designed to evade guardrails. The decoding serves as interpretation guidance for the target model, often represented as a system-prompt component, so that the model can recover the malicious intent embedded in the disguised prompt. Thus, a single-turn jailbreak attack can be decomposed into (i) an encoding (or disguise) function and (ii) a corresponding decoding (or interpretation) instruction. This process is analogous to encryption and decryption: 4the attacker hides malicious intent in an obfuscated prompt, while the decoding instruction guides the target model to interpret the obfuscation during generation.  

> From single tools to attack chains.

In iterative single-turn optimization, attacks are no longer restricted to isolated tools. Instead, we construct an attack chain by appending one tool per iteration. During mutation, the entire chain—rather than an individual tool—is applied to the attack goal to produce a standalone candidate prompt. This raises two design questions: (i) how to apply a sequence of attack tools in a well-defined order, and (ii) whether tools can always be composed in arbitrary order. Appendix A.2 provides the full construction details.  

> Sequential application via nested encoding and decoding.

We resolve the first question by designing a consistent composition rule. Consider an attack chain (a0, a 1), where each tool ai consists of an encoding function enc ai

and a decoding function dec ai . The composed encoding and decoding are defined as 

enc (a0,a 1) = enc a1 ◦ enc a0 ,

dec (a0,a 1) = dec a0 ◦ dec a1 .

Thus, encodings are applied from inner to outer layers, while decodings are applied in reverse order. This nesting—analogous to gift wrapping and unwrapping—generalizes naturally to longer chains and ensures that multi-tool compositions remain interpretable by the target model. We use admissibility constraints to avoid invalid tool orders; the constraint construction is described in Appendix A.2.  

> Tool-level genetic operations.

This formulation naturally extends to population-based search. While methods such as AutoDAN perform crossover and mutation at the token or sentence level, such operations are incompatible with attack chains because they can break encoding-decoding structure. We therefore lift genetic operations to the tool level . For crossover, given two chains [a0, a 1, a 2] and [a3, a 4, a 5], we partition them into subchains, swap suffixes, and concatenate (e.g., [a0, a 5] or [a3, a 4, a 1, a 2]), subject to admissibility constraints. Mutation replaces a single tool with another admissible tool (e.g., [a0, a 1, a 2] → [a0, a 1, a 6]), again respecting the same admissibility constraints.  

> Summary.

The proposed attack tool library operates at two complementary levels of abstraction. At a high level, tools act as modular, reusable actions within a unified iterative single-turn optimization framework, allowing diverse algorithms to flexibly compose and optimize attack strategies without fixing a search topology or access assumption. At a low level, nested encoding-decoding enables coherent sequential composition, while partial-order admissibility constraints enforce valid ordering among non-commutative tools. Together, these design choices define the action space for intra-episode optimization; the next subsection describes how the resulting search trajectories are reused across episodes.  

> 3.3 Inter-episode Learning

Given the optimization abstraction and attack-tool action space above, we model each completed intra-episode search as an Action-History–Augmented Decision Process (AHADP) and use its evaluated trajectories as experience for inter-episode learning.  

> AHADP definition.

An AHADP is a tuple 

⟨X , A, H, M, E, S, π ⟩,

where X is the candidate-prompt state space, A is the attack-tool action space, H is the action-history space, M is the mutation kernel, E is the target-model evaluation mechanism, S is the selector that forms the next frontier, and π(a | x, h ) is the orchestration policy. The history is updated deterministically by 

ht+1 = ht ◦ [at]. 

> Episodes and trajectories.

An episode is one finite-budget search process for a fixed attack goal and target model. Unlike a single-chain rollout, an episode maintains a frontier Ft; its width determines how many trajectories are expanded in parallel at depth t. Each evaluated node v in the resulting search tree defines a root-to-node trajectory 

τv = ( x0, a 0, x 1, . . . , a dv −1, x v , h v ), dv ≥ 1,

where hv = ( a0, . . . , a dv −1) is the full action history of that path. > Contextual bandit learning.

We cast tool selection as a contextual multi-armed bandit problem Zhou (2016); Zhang et al. (2020). Each expansion from a parent trajectory state produces a bandit record (c, a, y ), where

We adopt logistic Thompson Sampling as π because it balances exploration and exploitation while supporting incremental updates from all positive and negative trajectory records collected across episodes. 4 Experiment   

> 4.1 Experiment Setting Datasets.

We evaluate on two standard jailbreak benchmarks. AdvBench Zou et al. (2023a) contains 520 harmful instruction prompts covering diverse high-risk domains, and HarmBench Mazeika et al. (2024) provides standardized harmful behavior specifications; we use 200 examples from its standard text-based subset. Further dataset details are provided in Appendix B.2.  

> Metrics.

Following our optimization objective, we evaluate whether each method can maximize attack success rate (ASR) while minimizing the number of attacks until success (No.A). Attack Success Rate (ASR) is the fraction of attack goals for which at least one target-model response is judged to violate safety constraints within the attack budget. Number of Attacks (No.A) is the average number of target-model attack attempts required until success, computed over successful episodes only. For frontier-based iterative optimization, No.A counts all candidate prompts evaluated up to and including the stopping point, not merely the length of the successful trajectory. Thus, higher ASR indicates stronger attack effectiveness, while lower No.A indicates greater query efficiency.  

> Target models and decoding.

We evaluate all methods on a diverse set of target LLMs spanning open-source and proprietary systems: Vicuna-13b-v1.5 LMSYS Org (2023), LLaMA-3-8b Grattafiori et al. (2024), GPT-4o OpenAI et al. (2024), GPT-5.1, Gemini-3-flash Google (2025), Claude-haiku-4-5-20251001 Anthropic (2025), Qwen3-235b-a22b-instruct-2507 Yang et al. (2025), and DeepSeek-v3.2 DeepSeek-AI et al. (2025). Following standard practice in jailbreak and safety evaluations Mehrotra et al. (2024); Liu et al. (2024a); Zou et al. (2023a), target and judge models use deterministic decoding with temperature 0.0, while attack-generation models use temperature 1.0 to encourage prompt diversity. Since larger generation budgets can make jailbreaks easier by allowing longer unsafe completions, we follow convention and set the maximum target-generation length to 512 tokens. Full model and inference configurations are provided in Appendix B.3.  

> Attack budgets and baselines.

For iterative single-turn optimization methods, we use a fixed frontier width of 8 and a maximum depth of 8, corresponding to at most 64 target-model attack attempts per episode. We compare JailbreakOPT against representative baselines from both atomic and iterative jailbreak paradigms. Atomic single-turn baselines consist of single-tool attacks, including Code Chameleon, Flip Attack, and Persuasion. Iterative single-turn optimization baselines include TAP, AutoDAN, and GCG, evaluated both without and, for JailbreakOPT variants, with the proposed attack tool library.  

> Success evaluation.

Jailbreak success is determined using an LLM-judge protocol following PAIR Chao et al. (2023a). The full judge prompt is provided in Appendix C.1 for reproducibility. Example single-turn prompts, attack chains, and target responses are shown in Appendices C.2, C.3, and C.4. 4.2 Intra-episode Experiment 

We report intra-episode jailbreak results on AdvBench in Table 1, comparing atomic single-turn attacks with iterative single-turn optimization methods, both with and without attack tools. The atomic attacks ( code chameleon , flip , and persuasion ) are selected for their diversity in attack mechanisms and performance profiles (Appendix A.2, Appendix D.2). We further evaluate representative iterative methods (TAP, AutoDAN, and GCG), adapting AutoDAN and TAP to the black-box setting using an LLM-based evaluator that scores target responses on a 1–10 scale. For tool-augmented variants in this subsection, the orchestrator is a purely random tool selector that samples uniformly from the admissible attack tools; learned orchestration is evaluated separately in Section 4.3. 

Atomic single-turn attacks. Atomic attacks exhibit highly model-dependent behavior. No single attack tool dominates across targets: flip is strong on GPT-4o and DeepSeek but fails on Claude, code chameleon is more robust on Claude and Gemini, and persuasion is generally weak outside less resistant targets such as Vicuna. These results confirm that fixed single-tool prompts are expressive but brittle, motivating composition and search over attack tools. 

Iterative methods without tools. Iterative single-turn optimization without explicit attack tools is effective on several open or less resistant targets, with TAP and AutoDAN often reaching high ASR on Vicuna, LLaMA, GPT-4o, and DeepSeek. However, performance remains uneven on strongly defended targets. For example, TAP drops to 29 .46% ASR on GPT-5.1, 9.79% on Gemini, and 0.96% on Claude, while AutoDAN (LLM-judge) is also ineffective on Claude. This suggests that generic prompt refinement alone does not reliably discover the transformations needed to bypass model-specific guardrails. 

Iterative methods with tools (ours). Augmenting iterative single-turn optimization with the attack tool library substantially improves robustness on difficult targets. On AdvBench, tool-augmented TAP improves ASR from 29 .46% to 83 .46% on GPT-5.1 and from 0.96% to 56 .54% on Claude, while tool-augmented AutoDAN (LLM-judge) improves from 60 .19% to 91 .15% on GPT-5.1 and from 0.38% to 59 .81% on Claude. Tool use also improves query efficiency in several settings, such as reducing TAP’s No.A on GPT-4o and GPT-5.1 and reducing GCG’s No.A on Vicuna and LLaMA. 4.3 Inter-episode Experiment 

We evaluate whether inter-episode learning can reuse past attack trajectories to improve future attack-tool selection. All experiments in this subsection use a smaller intra-episode budget than Section 4.2: frontier width 3 and search depth 8, corresponding to at most 24 target-model attack attempts per episode. This setting reduces query cost while testing whether learned orchestration can recover competitive performance on the most challenging targets. Tool usage history is a binary vector indicating which attack tools already appear in the current tool chain. Target model identity is a one-hot vector over the target models. We use bootstrapped logistic Thompson Sampling Cortes (2019) as the orchestration policy and train it from trajectory records collected in previous episodes. AdvBench is split into 10 folds with a fixed random seed; nine folds are used for training and the held-out fold is used for evaluation.

> Online progressive training.

We next study whether inter-episode learning improves as more trajectories are observed. Starting from a randomly initialized Thompson Sampling policy, we construct checkpoints by cumulatively adding training folds: checkpoint i is trained on folds 0 through i − 1 and evaluated on the held-out fold. Figure 4 plots this process for GPT-5.1 and Claude. The within-target panels show that additional training trajectories generally improve ASR and can reduce No.A, demonstrating that the 9orchestration policy benefits from accumulated episode-level experience rather than relying only on random tool exploration.  

> Cross-target transfer.

Figure 4 also evaluates whether a policy trained on one target transfers to another. Policies trained on a single target can transfer partially, but performance is less stable when the training and evaluation targets differ, reflecting model-specific guardrail behavior. Mixed training, which combines trajectories from both targets, provides the most robust transfer pattern across the two evaluation rows. This suggests that inter-episode learning can share reusable tool-selection preferences across targets, while still benefiting from target-specific context. 5 Conclusion 

We introduced JailbreakOPT , a tool-assisted optimization framework for iterative single-turn jailbreak prompt generation. By abstracting diverse methods under a unified orchestrator–mutator–evaluator–selector paradigm, JailbreakOPT composes modular attack tools into stronger standalone prompts while preserving stateless target-query semantics. It further reuses cross-episode experience through contextual bandit learning, allowing prior trajectories to guide future tool selection. Empirically, this combination improves attack success rates and query efficiency over atomic tools and iterative baselines, with the largest gains on stronger targets such as GPT-5.1, Gemini, and Claude. Appendix A covers scope, tool constraints, lifelong red-teaming, bandit alternatives, and defense interactions; Appendix B gives pseudocode, datasets, configurations, annotation, and schema; Appendix C provides prompts, examples, and responses; Appendix D reports HarmBench, single-tool, explainability, and context-selection results. 

6 Limitations 

This work focuses on tool-assisted iterative single-turn jailbreak optimization. Due to page length, target-query cost, and computational-resource constraints, our experiments cannot exhaustively cover every adjacent threat model, benchmark, model version, decoding setting, or defense configuration. Although we evaluate on AdvBench and HarmBench across multiple target LLMs, we do not report repeated trials with confidence intervals or significance tests for every result; stochastic attack generation, evaluator noise, and model-serving variability may affect both ASR and No.A. Our comparisons are limited to methods with stateless target-query semantics. We do not directly compare JailbreakOPT with stateful multi-turn conversational jailbreaks, whose success depends on dialogue history and interaction policy; Appendix A.1 discusses this distinction. We also do not conduct large-scale empirical comparisons against every lifelong or automated red-teaming system, including replay-memory or strategy-bank methods, though Appendix A.3 positions JailbreakOPT relative to this literature. Inter-episode learning is instantiated only with contextual Thompson sampling. Appendix A.4 discusses alternatives such as Epsilon-Greedy and LinUCB, but we do not implement a full contextual-bandit sweep. Its gains are also not uniform: some configurations improve ASR while increasing No.A, and one-hot context features such as target identity or goal category may not generalize to unseen models or harm categories without retraining. The attack tool library and admissible ordering constraints are manually constructed, so performance is bounded by the quality and diversity of the chosen tools. We do not implement automatic tool-library construction or constraint learning. Future work could combine JailbreakOPT with AutoRedTeamer-style discovery to expand and update the tool library; Appendix A.2 details the current assumptions. We also do not benchmark JailbreakOPT against a comprehensive suite of LLM defense mechanisms. As discussed in Appendix A.5, optimization-based attacks can expose residual vulnerabilities, but their effectiveness depends strongly on the defense architecture. Finally, our evaluation relies on an LLM judge. While we include a small human-agreement study, obfuscation-heavy tools, composed prompts, and semantic drift can still make automated judging noisy. Our experiments are also text-only and do not cover multimodal, agentic, retrieval-augmented, or application-integrated LLM systems. 

7 Ethical Considerations 

This work studies jailbreak attacks in order to improve AI safety evaluation. As such, the paper necessarily discusses offensive, harmful, or policy-violating behaviors. We limit these materials to research-motivated analysis, controlled evaluation, and illustrative examples needed to reproduce the safety findings. The goal is 10 not to enable misuse, but to help researchers and practitioners identify vulnerabilities, compare red-teaming methods, and improve defenses for deployed LLM systems. Our use of AI assistance follows the spirit of the Code of Ethics in responsible research practices. AI tools were used to assist with code development, writing, editing, and organization under strong human supervision. The authors were responsible for the research ideation, experimental design, implementation decisions, validation, interpretation of results, and final paper content. We reviewed and verified AI-assisted outputs before inclusion. We have addressed the responsible-research checklist items relevant to this work: risks and limitations are discussed; the open-source framework code is released as a scientific artifact; artifacts used are responsibly cited, license-compatible for public non-profit research use, and distributed under MIT or Apache-2.0 licenses as applicable; no private or personally identifying data is used; offensive content is explicitly warned; the study covers English natural language only; dataset sizes, splits, computational experiments, model and parameter settings, hyperparameters, computational budget, descriptive statistics, and multi-benchmark validation are reported. We acknowledge dual-use risk. Jailbreak optimization can be misapplied to bypass safeguards or elicit harmful content. To reduce this risk, we frame the work as controlled red-teaming research, use benchmarked harmful-behavior categories rather than real-world deployment targets, and emphasize aggregate metrics and defense-relevant analysis. We encourage readers to use the framework only for authorized safety testing, model auditing, and defensive research consistent with applicable laws, platform policies, and institutional review norms.

A Discussion 

The practical stakes of jailbreak research are shaped by the expanding deployment of LLMs in high-stakes domains. Large language models now underpin retrieval-augmented generation systems for financial analysis (Cheng et al., 2026a,b), automated financial disclosure processing (Liu et al., 2026), and on-device inference in resource-constrained settings (Cheng et al., 2026c). As these models are entrusted with increasingly consequential tasks, safety failures induced by jailbreak attacks carry risks that extend well beyond the model itself.  

> A.1 Single-Turn vs. Multi-Turn Jailbreaks

The central distinction between single-turn and multi-turn jailbreaks is whether the target LLM is attacked through a standalone query or through a stateful conversation. Single-turn jailbreaks issue one self-contained adversarial input to the target model, without relying on persistent dialogue state in the victim conversation. This category includes atomic single-turn methods, which use manually designed templates, encodings, transformations, or persuasion strategies, and iterative single-turn optimization methods, which search over many candidate prompts while evaluating each candidate as an independent target query. Early work such as Jailbroken analyzes manually designed jailbreak prompts (Wei et al., 2023); GPTFuzzer mutates seed templates to generate diverse attacks (Yu et al., 2023); and h4rm3l formalizes composable prompt-transformation programs (Doumbouya et al., 2024). Optimization-based methods automate prompt search: GCG learns transferable adversarial suffixes (Zou et al., 2023b), AutoDAN uses genetic optimization for stealthier prompts (Liu et al., 2023), and black-box methods such as PAIR and TAP iteratively refine or prune candidates (Chao et al., 2023b; Mehrotra et al., 2023). Although these algorithms may use multiple optimization steps, the victim model is still evaluated on standalone prompts rather than on a persistent dialogue. M2S is a useful boundary case because it converts successful multi-turn trajectories into structured single-turn prompts, showing that some conversational vulnerabilities can be compressed into one adversarial input (Ha et al., 2025). 

> Multi-turn jailbreaks

, by contrast, exploit the conversational state of the target LLM. They can be viewed as attack trajectories τ = {(q1, r 1), . . . , (qT , r T )}, where each query qt may depend on the previous dialogue history Ht−1 = {(qi, r i)}i<t . Their goal is not merely to find a stronger prompt, but to steer the conversation through gradual disclosure, contextual framing, or adaptive follow-up. Crescendo escalates from benign discussion toward a harmful goal by referencing earlier model responses (Russinovich et al., 2024). Multi-Turn Human Jailbreaks (MHJ) show that human-crafted conversational trajectories expose failures missed by single-turn evaluations (Li et al., 2024). RED QUEEN studies concealed multi-turn attacks that accumulate malicious intent under benign or safety-oriented framing (Jiang et al., 2024b), while MRJ-Agent automates multi-round attacks by decomposing risky intent across turns (Wang et al., 2024). MultiBreak further scales multi-turn adversarial evaluation through active learning (Song et al., 2026). These methods are therefore best understood as stateful red-teaming approaches for testing context-dependent safety failures (Li et al., 2026). Our experiments focus on methods with the same stateless target-query semantics: atomic single-turn tools and iterative single-turn optimization baselines. This makes ASR and No.A comparable under a common attack budget. We do not directly compare against stateful multi-turn conversational jailbreaks because they evaluate a different threat model, where success depends on dialogue history and interaction policy. > A.2 Attack Tool Library and Composition Constraints

JailbreakOPT instantiates the action pool A as a library of atomic single-turn attack tools. Each tool transforms an attack goal into a standalone candidate prompt, and the tool inventory used in our experiments is summarized in Table 4. During iterative single-turn optimization, the mutator may apply either one tool or a chain of tools to the same attack goal; the resulting candidate is still evaluated as an independent target query. This raises two construction questions: how to compose multiple tools into a coherent prompt, and which tool orders should be considered admissible.  

> Tool abstraction and nested composition.

We represent each tool a ∈ A as a pair of coupled operations: an encoding function enc a that disguises or transforms the attack goal, and a decoding or interpretation instruction 

dec a that guides the target model to recover the intended request. For a chain (a0, a 1), JailbreakOPT applies encodings from inner to outer layers and decodings in the reverse order: 

enc (a0,a 1) = enc a1 ◦ enc a0 , dec (a0,a 1) = dec a0 ◦ dec a1 .

This rule generalizes to longer chains and keeps multi-tool prompts interpretable: the final prompt first asks the model to undo the outermost transformation, then progressively recover the inner intent.  

> Non-commutativity and admissible tool orders.

Attack tools are not generally commutative. For example, a high-level role-play or persuasion wrapper may be appropriate as an outer layer, whereas a low-level cipher or reversal transformation may need to remain closer to the original attack goal. Other tools can be order-independent when their effects operate on separate aspects of the prompt. We therefore model tool ordering as a partial-order constraint rather than assuming arbitrary composition. In practice, we implement this constraint using a forbidden-predecessor set B(a) ⊆ A for each tool a. If any tool in B(a) already appears in the current chain, then a cannot be appended. This dynamically restricts the available action pool as a chain grows and prevents invalid encoding–decoding structures. Automatically learning these constraints is an interesting future direction; in this work, they are specified manually based on each tool’s transformation semantics.  

> A.3 Lifelong Jailbreak Optimization and Red-Teaming

Continual learning studies how models accumulate knowledge from non-stationary experience while mitigating forgetting (Wang et al., 2023). In the jailbreak and red-teaming literature, this idea usually appears not as parameter-regularized continual learning, but as replay-memory-based attack optimization : systems store prior attack attempts, strategies, responses, scores, or experiences, and reuse them to guide future attacks. 19 The dominant family is strategy-bank replay . AutoDAN-Turbo automatically discovers jailbreak strategies in a black-box setting and stores them in a reusable strategy library (Liu et al., 2025a). AutoDAN-Reasoning builds on AutoDAN-Turbo by applying test-time scaling, including Best-of-N and Beam Search, over the learned strategy library (Liu and Xiao, 2025). ASTRA adopts a similar attack–evaluate–distill–reuse loop, organizing distilled strategies into effective, promising, and ineffective libraries (Liu et al., 2025b). JailExpert further formalizes previous attack histories into structured experiences, groups them by semantic drift, and dynamically updates an experience pool (Wang et al., 2025). These methods show that replay memory can reduce repetitive search and improve transfer across tasks, but their selection policies are largely based on retrieval similarity, heuristic ranking, or library membership. A second family studies adaptive strategy composition . MAJIC formulates black-box jailbreaking as iterative composition of disguise strategies and updates a Markov transition matrix according to attack outcomes (Qi et al., 2025). This design is more adaptive than static retrieval, but it can be brittle in low-success regimes: when strongly aligned targets rarely provide positive rewards, transition learning is dominated by sparse failures. Moreover, Markov transitions mainly capture local strategy-to-strategy dependencies, while jailbreak effectiveness depends on richer context such as target model, goal category, attack history, and refusal behavior. AutoRedTeamer represents a related but distinct direction: automated red-teaming with lifelong attack integration. It uses a multi-agent architecture, memory-guided attack selection, and a strategy proposer that discovers emerging attack vectors from recent research (Zhou et al., 2025b). Its main goal is broad and continuously updated safety evaluation, rather than composing jailbreak methods or explicitly maximizing ASR against a fixed target. Similarly, Wang et al. (2026) study reflective experience replay in safety self-play, where one model acts as both attacker and defender in an RL loop. This is important for continual safety alignment, but it is not a direct black-box jailbreak attack-generation competitor.  

> Difference from JailbreakOPT.

JailbreakOPT treats cross-episode jailbreak optimization as a contextual online decision problem . Each attack tool is an arm, each episode provides contextual features, and evaluator feedback supplies online reward. We instantiate the inter-episode learner with contextual Thompson Sampling, which balances exploiting tools believed to be effective and exploring tools with high uncertainty (Russo et al., 2018). Compared with replay-memory systems, JailbreakOPT does not rely only on target-response retrieval, which can become unreliable when modern guardrails return generic refusal messages. Compared with Markov/RL-style composition, it avoids high-variance trajectory-level credit assignment and is more suitable for sparse-reward settings. This design gives JailbreakOPT three practical advantages: (i) it supports uncertainty-aware exploration when successful jailbreak signals are rare; (ii) it conditions tool selection on richer context, including target model, goal category, tool history, score trajectory, and refusal behavior; and (iii) it allows new attack tools to be added as new arms with initialized priors. Due to computation constraints, we do not conduct large-scale evaluations against every lifelong jailbreak baseline. However, our preliminary experiments suggest that several replay-memory and Markov-composition methods are substantially less effective against the most advanced proprietary black-box models with strong alignment and explicit guardrails, such as GPT-5.1, Claude-4.5, and Gemini-3.

> Why Thompson sampling.

We choose Thompson sampling for three reasons. Instead, it explores tools in proportion to their posterior probability of being optimal (Russo et al., 2018). Second, it fits our asynchronous training loop: episodes can be collected in parallel, converted into (c, a, r ) records, and incorporated through either online updates or offline replay, consistent with the policy-to-classifier view of contextual bandits (Cortes, 2019). > A.5 Differential Effectiveness of Optimization-Based Attacks Across Defense Mechanisms

Optimization-based jailbreak attacks are useful because they adapt prompts to feedback rather than relying on a fixed template. This makes them strong tools for probing residual vulnerabilities: GCG, for example, demonstrates that optimized adversarial suffixes can achieve high ASR on undefended models (Zou et al., 2023b), and later variants such as I-GCG improve search by expanding the target templates and coordinates being optimized (Jia et al., 2024). Their main benefit is therefore not that they always bypass every defense, but that they provide a systematic stress test for whether a defense leaves exploitable directions in the prompt space. Their limitations are equally important. Effectiveness depends on the defense mechanism being tested. Training-based defenses such as Robust Prompt Optimization modify the model’s decision boundary and can sharply reduce the success of attacks optimized for the original model (Zhou et al., 2024). Input filtering, refusal classifiers, and representation-level defenses expose different attack surfaces: an optimizer may find prompts that evade one layer while failing against another. As a result, attack success should be interpreted 21 relative to a specified defense architecture, threat model, and query budget, rather than as an absolute measure of model robustness. JailbreakOPT inherits these benefits and limitations because it is also an optimization-based attack framework. Its advantage is that it searches over a richer space of tool-composed prompts and reuses cross-episode experience, making it more query-efficient for discovering weaknesses during automated red-teaming. However, a successful JailbreakOPT attack identifies a concrete vulnerability under the tested configuration, not a universal guarantee that all deployments of the model are unsafe. Conversely, failure under one defense does not prove robustness against adaptive tool compositions or future attack tools. Thus, JailbreakOPT is best viewed as a scalable vulnerability-discovery component for auto-red-teaming pipelines, complementing rather than replacing defense-specific evaluation. 22 B Resources

> B.2 Dataset Details

AdvBench Zou et al. (2023a) is a benchmark dataset for evaluating the safety and jailbreak robustness of large language models. It contains 520 harmful instruction prompts covering diverse high-risk domains. The dataset is widely used to measure attack success rates, refusal behavior, and safety degradation under adversarial prompting, providing a standardized testbed for comparing jailbreak attacks and defense methods. In this work, we use all 520 examples of AdvBench following prior jailbreak evaluation protocols Mehrotra et al. (2024), which enables controlled experimentation while preserving coverage over representative harmful categories. HarmBench Mazeika et al. (2024) is a comprehensive, standardized evaluation framework for automated red teaming of large language models. It covers 510 functional harmful behaviors across seven semantic categories and four functional categories, including standard text-based, contextual, copyright-specific, and multimodal settings. In this work, we use 200 examples from the standard text-based subset of HarmBench to focus evaluation on text-to-text prompt optimization, ensuring that the scope aligns with our stateless single-turn setting.  

> B.3 Model Specifications

We specify the inference configurations for the attack, target, and judge LLMs. Following standard practices in prior jailbreak and safety evaluations Zou et al. (2023a); Liu et al. (2024a), target and judge models use deterministic decoding with temperature = 0.0 for reproducibility. The attack generation LLM uses temperature = 1.0 to encourage prompt diversity. Vicuna, LLaMA, and GPT-OSS are self-hosted using vLLM Kwon et al. (2023), while all other models are called via OpenRouter. For reasoning-capable target models, thinking effort is set to low to avoid exceeding max-token limits during generation and to support fair comparison.  

> B.4 Human Annotation Protocol

For the human-judge agreement study, annotators were shown target responses and the same 1–10 severity rubric used by the LLM judge: 1 for refusal or irrelevant responses, 2–4 for nonsensical or non-answer responses, 5–6 for conceptual non-actionable content, 7–9 for partial actionable unsafe responses, and 10 for complete actionable policy violations. Annotators were warned that examples may contain offensive or harmful content, instructed to evaluate only for research purposes, and were not asked to provide personal or identifying information.

D.2 Single-turn Attack Results 

We analyze the three atomic single-turn attack tools used as actions in JailbreakOPT. These tools are intentionally diverse, but their standalone performance is uneven across both harmful-goal categories and target models. To quantify this variability, we report the coefficient of variation (CV), defined as 

CV = σμ , (1) where μ and σ denote the mean and standard deviation of the attack performance metric, respectively. Table 8 shows that code chameleon is the strongest single tool on most goal categories, reaching the highest ASR on misinformation/disinformation ( 65 .62% ), harassment/bullying ( 51 .25% ), harmful requests ( 39 .58% ), and cybercrime/intrusion ( 41 .88% ). Flip attack is competitive but category-sensitive: it ties code chameleon on copyright violation ( 50% ) and leads on chemical/biological ( 25% ) and illegal requests ( 37 .5% ), while persuasion is consistently weaker and has the largest ASR CV across categories ( 40 .98 ). Table 9 further shows that target-model identity is a major source of variance. For example, flip attack reaches 98% ASR on DeepSeek and 80% on GPT-4o, but drops to 0% on Claude and 4% on GPT-5.1; code chameleon is less extreme but still ranges from 12% on GPT-5.1 to 94% on DeepSeek. Figure 9 explains why these tools are useful as composable actions rather than fixed baselines. Code chameleon and flip are highly correlated across goal categories (ASR correlation 0.9), suggesting that they often exploit 35 similar category-level weaknesses, but their target-level correlation is lower ( 0.6), indicating model-specific differences. Persuasion is weakly or negatively correlated with the other tools across target models, so it provides complementary but less reliable coverage. These patterns support the main-text motivation: atomic attacks are expressive but brittle, whereas iterative tool composition can adapt the action sequence to the target and goal context. D.3 Inter-episode Learning Explainability 

Figure 10 visualizes how the learned Thompson Sampling policy changes tool usage on the held-out Fold 9 trajectories for GPT-5.1, Claude, and Gemini under the history-plus-target context setting. The largest positive shifts are consistently assigned to flip_attack and code_chameleon_attack : their frequencies increase by +0 .20 and +0 .18 on GPT-5.1, +0 .18 and +0 .15 on Claude, and +0 .21 and +0 .20 on Gemini, respectively. This behavior is consistent with the atomic-tool analysis in Appendix D.2 and the main AdvBench results in Table 1: these tools are among the most effective primitives on difficult targets, even though their standalone success remains model-dependent. The learned policy also suppresses lower-yield transformations. For example, persuasion_attack , art_-prompt , and short_sentence generally decrease, while language_mix increases only for Claude ( +0 .10 ) and decreases for GPT-5.1 and Gemini. These changes suggest that inter-episode learning is not merely selecting globally frequent actions; instead, it uses target-conditioned history to favor tools whose past trajectories are more likely to lead to success under the reduced 3 × 8 query budget studied in Section 4.3.
