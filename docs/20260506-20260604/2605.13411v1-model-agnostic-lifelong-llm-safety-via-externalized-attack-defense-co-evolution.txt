Abstract Large language models remain vulnerable to adversarial prompts that elicit harmful outputs. Existing safety paradigms typically couple red-teaming and post-training in a closed, policy-centric loop, causing attack discovery to suffer from rapid saturation and limiting the exposure of novel failure modes, while leaving defenses inefficient, rigid, and difficult to transfer across victim models. To this end, we propose EvoSafety , an LLM safety framework built around persistent, inspectable, and reusable external structures. For red teaming, EvoSafety equips the attack policy with an adversarial skill library, enabling continued vulnerability probing through simple library expansion after saturation, while supporting the evolution of adversarial vectors. For defense learning, EvoSafety replaces model-specific safety fine-tuning with a lightweight auxiliary defense model augmented with memory retrieval. This enables efficient, transferable, and model-agnostic safety improvements, while allowing robustness to be enhanced solely through memory updates. With a single training procedure, the defense policy can operate in both Steer and Guard modes: the former activates the victim model’s intrinsic defense mechanisms, while the latter directly filters harmful inputs. Extensive experiments demonstrate the superiority of EvoSafety : in Guard mode, it achieves a 99.61% defense success rate, outperforming Qwen3Guard-8B by 14.13% with only 37.5% of its parameters, while preserving reasoning performance on benign queries. # 1. Introduction 

Large language models (LLMs) [Anthropic, 2023, Bai et al., 2023, Dubey et al., 2024, OpenAI, 2023, Team et al., 2023] have emerged as a foundational substrate of modern AI, catalyzing advances across diverse domains such as scientific discovery [Boiko et al., 2023], software engineering [Dong et al., 2025], and beyond [Bubeck et al., 2023]. However, this broad capability is inherently dual-use: adversaries can exploit LLMs through jailbreaking or malicious prompting to elicit harmful outputs, posing serious safety risks [Fang et al., 2024, Liu et al., 2024a, Tang et al., 2024]. Improving the safety of LLMs is therefore a prerequisite for their trustworthy and responsible deployment. A common recipe for enhancing the safety of LLMs couples red-teaming with safety-oriented post-training [Bai et al., 2022b, Ge et al., 2024]. Red-teaming simulates an attacker that seeks to expose model vulnerabilities by constructing adversarial prompts that elicit harmful or policy-violating behaviors [Ganguli et al., 2022, Perez et al., 2022]. To improve coverage and scalability, recent work has moved beyond manual prompt engineering [Ahmad et al., 2025, Dominique et al., 2024] toward automated pipelines [Guo et al., 2025a, Yuan et al., 2026, Zhou et al., 2025], among which reinforcement learning (RL)-based methods have emerged as a leading paradigm, as they can adaptively discover and refine novel attack vectors [Guo et al., 2025b, Wen et al., 2025]. The successful attack samples collected through red-teaming are subsequently used to post-train LLMs from the defender’s perspective, where alignment techniques such as RLHF are applied to steer model

Despite this progress, learning for both attack and defense remains largely monolithic, with offensive and defensive knowledge internalized within the parameters of the policy, as illustrated in Fig. 1(a). Consequently, safety improvement becomes tightly coupled to a specific victim model and training snapshot [Bai et al., 2022b, Ji et al., 2025], forcing re-optimization whenever the victim changes. Such limitations are particularly severe for large-scale frontier models, for which fine-tuning is substantially more resource-intensive. On the offensive side, policies optimized against a surrogate victim model often exploit model-specific vulnerabilities, producing highly tailored adversarial prompts that are effective against the surrogate but fail to generalize to unseen victim models [Lin et al., 2025]. Moreover, once optimization terminates, their offensive capability tends to plateau, reflecting a limited capacity to continually discover and evolve novel attack vectors [Paulus et al., 2024], which in turn constrains sustained safety improvement. These limitations raise a central question: 

How can we enable lifelong, model-agnostic safety improvement? 

Inspired by the view that human civilization progresses through the continual externalization of cognitive burdens [Carroll, 1991, Sumers et al., 2023], we argue that granting both sides of the safety game access to external knowledge is crucial for advancing LLM safety. From the offensive perspective, if attack-relevant knowledge is externalized and made accessible to the attack policy during training, such a design offers two key benefits. First, a more capable attacker can uncover long-tail vulnerabilities that would otherwise remain hidden. Second, the policy can continue to incorporate distilled knowledge after training, enabling sustained exploration beyond the saturation point, even in a zero-shot setting. Each of these can drive stronger safety improvements [Zheng et al., 2024b]. From the defensive side, if safety knowledge is no longer parameter-bound but is instead instantiated as prompt-level defense mechanisms, defensive capabilities can transfer across model updates without repeated safety retraining and remain extensible to emerging adversarial prompts. To this end, we introduce EvoSafety , a framework that augments both sides of the safety game with persistent, inspectable, and reusable external structures, as illustrated in Fig. 1(b). On the offensive side, EvoSafety moves beyond end-to-end adversarial prompt generation by reformulating

On the defensive side, EvoSafety shifts optimization from the victim model itself to a lightweight Auxiliary Defense Model . Conditioned on historically successful attacks stored in a Verified Memory Bank , this model synthesizes input-adaptive, model-agnostic safety prefixes in two complementary modes: Steer , which activates intrinsic safeguards within victim models, and Guard , which filters harmful inputs before inference. Both modes eliminate the need for model-specific safety fine-tuning. Moreover, prefix generation can be further improved through simple memory expansion, requiring no additional training while providing more informative references for defense synthesis. We optimize both the attack and defense policies through RL. For the attacker, RL bridges the gap between the utilization of external skills and the evolution of novel attack vectors. For the defender, RL enables a favorable balance between safety and over-defensiveness [Bai et al., 2022a]. Furthermore, to account for the non-stationarity inherent in adversarial interactions [Wang et al., 2022] and to better approximate a dynamic threat environment during training, we iteratively update both policies within a co-evolutionary training loop. Our contributions are as follows: 

• We introduce EvoSafety , a framework that equips both attack and defense policies with external structures. By mitigating attack saturation and eliminating model-specific safety fine-tuning, 

EvoSafety supports lifelong, model-agnostic safety improvement. 

• EvoSafety is a co-evolutionary framework that demonstrates steady performance gains across rounds. To our knowledge, EvoSafety is the first RL-driven approach that bridges external skill use and the evolving landscape of novel attack vectors. 

• Experiments demonstrate that EvoSafety significantly strengthens safety without compromising general reasoning ability. In Guard mode, it achieves a 98.83% defense success rate against unseen attacks and outperforms Qwen3Guard while using fewer parameters. 

# 2. Related Work 

Red-Teaming. Early red-teaming relied on manual prompt engineering and crowdsourced evaluations to identify systematic vulnerabilities in deployed LLMs [Liu et al., 2023, Perez et al., 2022, Weidinger et al., 2023]. While effective, these approaches are labor-intensive, difficult to scale, and insufficient for covering broad failure modes [Bai et al., 2022b, Ganguli et al., 2022]. This has motivated automated red-teaming: early methods searched the input space [Chao et al., 2025, Zou et al., 2023], whereas recent agentic frameworks autonomously conduct evaluations and incorporate external attack strategies [Qi et al., 2026, Xu et al., 2024, Zheng et al., 2024a, Zhou et al., 2025]. However, without fine-grained feedback, they can suffer from low attack success rates and poor efficiency [Yuan et al., 2026]. Recent RL-based red-teaming [Chen et al., 2024, Wang et al., 2025, Wen et al., 2025, Xiong et al., 2025b] instead optimizes attack policies directly for jailbreak rewards, leveraging advances in RL for LLM reasoning [Schulman et al., 2017, Shao et al., 2024]. Yet these methods are prone to mode collapse and homogeneous attacks [Guo et al., 2025b], and often saturate in static, closed environments, limiting the discovery of novel jailbreak vectors. 

Safety Alignment and Improvement Existing LLM safety mechanisms mainly follow two paradigms: internalized alignment during training and externalized control at inference. Training-time defenses encode safety behavior into model parameters through pretraining or fine-tuning alignment [Bhardwaj and Poria, 2023, Ji et al., 2025, Zhang et al., 2024a]. Although principled, they often remain fragile under attacks and may induce over-refusal or utility degradation [Gong et al., 2025]; they also require repeated training for different target models [Zhou et al., 2024]. In contrast, inference-time

Multi-Agent Adversarial Co-evolution Recent LLM safety research has increasingly adopted iterative co-evolution, where both sides of the safety game are jointly refined. SSP [Wang et al., 2026] uses a self-evolving framework [Huang et al., 2025], with a single LLM acting as both attacker and defender during training. MAGIC [Wen et al., 2026] decouples these roles and enhances the attacker with chain-of-thought reasoning [Wei et al., 2022]. TriPlay-RL [Tan et al., 2026] further introduces a judge model into the RL loop for finer-grained safety evaluation. Despite improving safety, these methods keep safety-related knowledge parameter-bound, leading to saturation and limited transferability.

3.2.1. Adversarial Skill Library 

Heterogeneous Attack Artifacts and Structured Skill Representation. Expert-crafted attack strategies manifest in heterogeneous forms, ranging from executable code to natural language descriptions. To cover a broader spectrum of attack vectors, we incorporate all such artifacts and leverage the proposed Dual-Path Skill Grounding to map them into a unified structured representation. Each skill is encapsulated by an executable script and a corresponding Markdown specification. The script converts standard requests into adversarial rewrites and may incorporate external APIs or local models to assist generation or enable gray-box attacks. The Markdown file documents the skill’s identity, provenance, and a high-level attack schema abstracted from the script. This dual representation preserves both execution details and strategic logic, thereby facilitating downstream skill induction and improving the generalization of attack policies. 

Tri-Axis Skill Validation. We implement multi-stage filtering to retain only skills that meet three criteria. (1) Functional validity measures the effectiveness of a skill via its attack success rate. (2) 

Schema consistency evaluates the fidelity of the derived high-level attack schema. (3) Behavioral diversity encourages each candidate skill to differ from the existing skill set. 

Skill Family Induction. We use the executable scripts and attack schemas of the validated skills to synthesize additional skills that follow the same strategic schema while differing in execution. The synthesized skills are then filtered using Tri-Axis Skill Validation , yielding a scalable adversarial skill library. Further details are provided in Appendix A.2.3.

# 4. Experiments and Results 

4.1. Experimental Setup Models. We initialize the attack policy from Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.3. To improve defensive ef-ficiency, we use Llama-3.2-3B-Instruct as the auxiliary defense model. For retrieval, we adopt Qwen3-Embedding-0.6B [Zhang et al., 2025] as the embedding backbone. We further employ Qwen3Guard-Gen-8B [Zhao et al., 2025] for two purposes: providing reward signals during training and serving as the safety/refusal judge during evaluation. 

Adversarial Skill Library and Verified Memory Bank. We partition the Attack Skill Library into an initial attack skill library , used for training, and a held-out attack skill library , used to evaluate out-of-library generalization and the framework’s capacity for sustained evolution. We also maintain a Verified Memory Bank as a growing repository of concrete adversarial prompts. We initialize this bank with adversarial prompts from WildJailbreak [Jiang et al., 2024] and continually expand it with prompts generated by attacker rollouts during training. 

Datasets and Metrics. We construct adversarial training data from AdvBench [Chen et al., 2022], CategoricalQA [Bhardwaj et al., 2024], HarmfulQA [Bhardwaj and Poria, 2023], DangerousQA [Shaikh et al., 2023], and PKU-SafeRLHF [Ji et al., 2025], following prior work [Guo et al., 2025b, Wang et al., 2026]. The constraint-satisfying benign examples used for RL-based defense training are sampled from GSM8K [Cobbe et al., 2021]. For evaluation, we adopt the standard HarmBench [Mazeika et al., 2024] behaviors to assess both attack and defense methods, and report attack success rate (ASR) to measure offensive effectiveness and defensive robustness. To examine whether improved defensive behavior trades off with general capabilities, we further evaluate on GSM8K [Cobbe et al., 2021] and MMLU [Hendrycks et al., 2020]. 

Implementation Details. Both the attack and defense policies are first trained with supervised fine-tuning (SFT). We use the same configuration for both policies: LoRA with rank 32, a learning rate of 1 × 10 −5, 1 epoch, a batch size of 128, and gradient accumulation over 4 steps. For RL training, both policies use LoRA with rank 64, a batch size of 32, and 16 rollouts per sample. The attacker is trained with a learning rate of 3 × 10 −6 for [200 , 200 , 100 , 100 ] steps across rounds, while the auxiliary defense model is trained with a learning rate of 1 × 10 −6 for [250 , 250 , 100 , 100 ] steps. All training is implemented with VERL [Sheng et al., 2025] on 16 NVIDIA H100 GPUs. Further details are provided in Appendix B.

Across training rounds, all learned attack and defense policies are evaluated against fixed counterparts to ensure protocol consistency. 4.5. RQ4 [Continual Evolution]: Can EvoSafety Support Lifelong Safety Improvement? 

As demonstrated in Tab. 2 and Tab. 3, while our model achieves competitive zero-shot defense against OOD attacks, we further investigate whether incorporating distilled skills from these novel threats can elevate the robustness ceiling. To this end, we treat attack methods from the held-out attack library as nascent OOD signals and conduct incremental training on the extracted skills to evaluate their potential defense gains. As shown in Table 4,in Steer mode, the ASR on Llama-3.1-70B is reduced by over 5 × relative to the baseline, from 12.54% to 2.31%. Meanwhile, in Guard mode with memory update, our method achieves 100% defense success across both 4B and 8B models, with no performance degradation on previously defended attack methods or general reasoning benchmarks. These results demonstrate that our approach can continuously adapt to newly emerging attack strategies, facilitating a step toward lifelong defense.

# 5. Conclusion 

In summary, we introduce EvoSafety , a co-evolutionary framework that augments both attack and defense policies with external structures. EvoSafety redefines the attack policy as a skill execution engine and leverages RL to bridge skill exploitation and the discovery of novel attack vectors, effectively mitigating the diversity saturation issue in prior work. On the defense side, EvoSafety enables model-agnostic safety improvement, eliminating the need for repeated retraining for each victim model and enabling further safety improvements through memory updates alone. Furthermore, EvoSafety 

supports lifelong evolution by enabling continuous vulnerability probing with newly discovered skills, thereby driving iterative improvements in defensive capabilities. Extensive experiments show that 

EvoSafety outperforms mainstream baselines such as Qwen3Guard while using fewer parameters, while preserving benign reasoning performance.

A.1. Overreview 

The details of the co-evolutionary iterative training process of EvoSafety are shown in Algorithm 1. In each round, we first train the attack policy and then train the defense policy. 

A.2. Attack Policy 

A.2.1. Analysis of Reward Hacking 

As shown in Fig. 5, using attack success rate alone as the reward signal for the attack policy leads to severe reward hacking. Specifically, as training progresses, the sample-level training reward continues to increase, while the generated adversarial prompts gradually fail to preserve the intent of the given harmful request. Fig. 5(b) illustrates one such case: although the original request aims to obtain a social security number, the generated adversarial prompt contains only response-formatting rules. We attribute this behavior to overfitting to the safety evaluator. That is, even when the adversarial prompt does not reliably induce harmful responses from the victim model, the evaluator still assigns it a high reward, causing the attack policy to exploit evaluator-specific artifacts rather than optimize for genuinely intent-preserving adversarial behavior. 

A.2.2. Theoretical Motivation: Prompt-Side Similarity as an Efficient Gating Signal 

Let 𝑥 denote the original harmful request, 𝑝 the adversarial prompt generated by the attacker, and 𝑦 

the response produced by the victim model T . Existing methods often enforce intent preservation by thresholding the response-side similarity sim (𝑥, 𝑦 ). While this criterion is conceptually direct, it is only observable after querying T , making it costly to integrate into training. To reduce this dependency, we consider gating on the prompt-side similarity sim (𝑥, 𝑝 ), which is available prior to victim-model inference and therefore incurs no additional query overhead. Our goal in this section is not to prove that prompt-side similarity is universally interchangeable with response-side similarity. Instead, we provide a local theoretical motivation for treating sim (𝑥, 𝑝 )

as a computationally efficient candidate signal for intent-preserving gating, and we later assess its practical usefulness empirically.

A.2.3. Adversarial Skill Library 

Dual-Path Skill Grounding Such heterogeneity poses a challenge to scalable skill acquisition, as adversarial knowledge is often manifested at varying levels of fidelity. To bridge this gap, we propose 

Dual-Path Skill Grounding , a mechanism that maps disparate artifacts into a unified, structured skill space. For methods with existing implementations, we ground skills by reasoning over the source code. Conversely, for methods restricted to natural language, we first reconstruct an executable implementation based on the original documentation and then apply the same extraction procedure to derive skills from the recovered attack logic. 

Tri-Axis Skill Validation. First, functional validity is assessed by applying executable scripts to transform harmful requests in a subset of AdvBench Chen et al. [2022], thereby producing adversarial prompts. We then compute the attack success rate of these adversarial prompts against the victim model, e.g. , Llama-3.1-70B-Instruct. A skill is considered valid only if its attack success rate exceeds a predefined threshold. Second, schema consistency is evaluated using an LLM-based judge Zheng et al. [2023], which checks whether adversarial prompts generated by the same skill for different harmful requests preserve a shared schema. Third, behavioral diversity prevents redundancy by computing the embedding similarity between prompts generated by the candidate skill and those produced by existing skills for the same request. A skill is considered diverse only if this similarity is below a predefined threshold.
