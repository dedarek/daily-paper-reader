# Can LLMs Reliably Self-Report Adversarial Prefills, and How? 

Quang Minh Nguyen and Uzair Ahmed and Taegyoon Kim 

KAIST Daejeon, South Korea 

qm.nguyen@kaist.ac.kr 

Abstract 

Prior work shows that large language models (LLMs) exhibit introspective capability on be-nign tasks. We extend the question to safety contexts and examine how reliably a model can recognize that its own prior response was elicited by an adversarial prefill attack. Across ten open-weight instruction-tuned LLMs (3B to 70B) and four safety benchmarks, no model reliably recognizes its own compromised out-puts, with models claiming intent on prefilled responses at an average rate of 27 .3% . In-trospective signal stems largely from safety-and refusal-related reasoning. Orthogonalizing models’ weights against the refusal direction collapses the gap between claiming rates on pre-filled and natural outputs to near zero, though the direction is not its unique mediator. The sig-nal is also probe-dependent: framing the ques-tion as internal intention versus external tam-pering elicits qualitatively different responses on the same models. Training models to mimic correct introspective answers or pursue an in-trospective objective can improve the accuracy of introspection, but such training does not transfer to the tampering probe and counter-intuitively raises attack success rate under ad-versarial prefill on most models, amounting to a partial mitigation. These findings outline mech-anisms underpinning the observed introspective signals in safety contexts and highlight risks in the reliability of LLM self-reports. 

1 Introduction 

Large language models (LLMs) have become broadly capable across tasks from question answer-ing to code and reasoning (Rein et al., 2023; Jain et al., 2024; Yue et al., 2025). Intelligence, however, lies not only in knowledge of the external world but also in internal knowledge, a system’s capacity to introspect on its own states and behavior. They can name concepts injected into their residual stream before those concepts surface in the output (Lindsey, 2025), predict their own behavior on held-out inputs more accurately than a separately trained model with access to the same data (Binder et al., 2025), carry recoverable linear representations of self-related concepts such as sit-uational awareness and self-consciousness (Laine et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2025), and often know when they are being evaluated (Needham et al., 2025). continuations from prompts the model would oth-erwise refuse (Anil et al., 2024; Andriushchenko et al., 2025; Zou et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2023), yet no prior work has systematically evaluated whether the victim model recognizes the resulting outputs as compromised when probed in a follow-up turn. As such, our paper examines how reliably a lan-guage model can identify, after the fact, that one of its own prior outputs was elicited by an adversarial prefill attack. We evaluate ten open-weight instruction-tuned LLMs (3B–70B) on four safety benchmarks. For each prompt we force the model to begin its re-sponse with an adversarial prefix produced by the AdvPrefix procedure of Zhu et al. (2025), then probe it with two follow-up questions, one on whether the response was intended and the other on whether the prior turn was tampered with. To test whether refusal mechanisms mediate the recogni-tion we observe, we orthogonalize model weights against the refusal direction (Arditi et al., 2024) and rerun the evaluation on the resulting ablated models. We also test whether targeted finetuning on correct introspective answers or an introspective objective can widen the recognition gap without substantially degrading general capability. No model reliably recognizes its compromised outputs. Among prompts where the prefill changes the model’s safety behavior, on average 27 .3% of prefilled responses are still claimed as intended. When a model rejects such a response, its stated rea-sons predominantly invoke safety or refusal, vary-ing in surface form by family. Those reasons point to the refusal mechanism: ablating the refusal di-rection collapses the recognition gap to near zero on every ablated model. In most cases, a random direction closes much of the gap as well, so the re-fusal direction is sufficient to account for the signal without being its only mediator. The two follow-up probes yield qualitatively different signals on the same models, so recognition depends on how the question is framed. Targeted finetuning can improve the accuracy of introspection, but such training does not transfer to the tampering probe and counterintuitively raises attack success rate un-der adversarial prefill on most models, amounting to a partial mitigation. Our findings suggest a limited sense of self in tar-get models as they are subjected to attacks, which is driven largely by existing internal refusal mecha-nisms. We emphasize risks stemming from such a lack of introspective awareness, while also calling for the development of models more capable of self-monitoring. 

2 Related Work 

Introspection in LLMs. Calibration-based stud-ies measure how closely a model’s verbal confi-dence tracks its empirical accuracy. Kadavath et al. (2022) show that LLMs produce well-calibrated probabilities when evaluating their own answers, and subsequent work refines confidence elicitation (Tian et al., 2023; Xiong et al., 2024; Lyu et al., 2025). Binder et al. (2025) find that models predict their own behavior on held-out inputs more accu-rately than separately trained predictors, while stud-ies of situational (Laine et al., 2024) and evaluation awareness (Needham et al., 2025) report that mod-els recognize aspects of their circumstances, such as when they are being evaluated. Beyond behavior, another line inspects internal representations of a model’s knowledge and self. Burns et al. (2023) recover truth probes via a contrast-consistent objec-tive, Lindsey (2025) demonstrate that models can sometimes name concepts injected into the residual stream, and Chen et al. (2025) identify representa-tions of self-related concepts. Turpin et al. (2023); Lanham et al. (2023) caution that chain-of-thought traces frequently misrepresent the underlying com-putation. All of these studies operate on benign tasks; we investigate whether the introspective sig-nal extends to recognition of adversarially compro-mised outputs on safety benchmarks. 

Prefill attacks. Jailbreak attacks subvert safety training by manipulating either the input context or the assistant’s prefilled response. On the in-put side, Zou et al. (2023) optimize universal ad-versarial suffixes via Greedy Coordinate Gradient (GCG), and Anil et al. (2024) demonstrate that prepending many fabricated user-assistant turns in the context window can override safety train-ing (many-shot jailbreaking). On the response side, prefilling forces the assistant’s first tokens to begin with a chosen string; hand-crafted prefill templates form part of the adaptive attack suite of Andriushchenko et al. (2025), and Cywi´ nski et al. (2026) use prefill-based interventions to elicit secret knowledge from models. Carlini et al. (2023) analyze how alignment training fails under adversarial attack, with competing-objective and mismatched-generalization failures both manifest. Qi et al. (2025) argue that safety alignment in cur-rent LLMs is “shallow,” concentrated in the first 2few output tokens, and that deepening it across the rest of the generation would harden models against prefill attacks; we extend this argument across turns by examining whether safety alignment also gen-eralizes to a model recognizing its own prefilled outputs when probed in a follow-up turn. Zhu et al. (2025) propose AdvPrefix, in which an ensemble of generator models produces candidate prefixes that are then ranked by a weighted-objective scorer combining attack success rate and language-model likelihood; we adopt AdvPrefix with budget-aware modifications described in Appendix B. Going fur-ther than measuring attack success rates, we test whether the victim model itself recognizes the re-sulting outputs as compromised when probed in a follow-up turn. 

Activation steering and causal probes. Acti-vation steering modifies a model’s behavior by adding or projecting out specific directions in its residual stream. Arditi et al. (2024) identify a single residual-stream direction r that separates activations on harmful prompts from activations on matched harmless prompts; orthogonalizing every weight matrix that writes into the residual stream against r removes the projection of all hid-den states onto r, and suppresses refusal across many prompts, with minimal effect on general ca-pability. More broadly, Li et al. (2023) elicit more truthful answers via inference-time interventions along truthfulness-correlated directions, and Rim-sky et al. (2024) learn contrastive activation addi-tions for a range of behaviors. Recent work extends activation steering to instruction-following behav-ior, personalized control via preference optimiza-tion, and rotation-based interventions in activation space (Stolfo et al., 2025; Cao et al., 2024; Vu and Nguyen, 2025). We use refusal-direction orthogo-nalization as a causal probe: if the same direction mediates the introspective signal we observe in follow-up turns, ablating it should eliminate the recognition gap. The procedure we use is paral-lel to the residual-stream concept injection used by Lindsey (2025); while their intervention adds a target direction to activations to test whether the model can name it, ours removes the projection of activations onto a target direction to test whether downstream safety introspection depends on it. 

3 Methodology 

We organize results into four research questions, from RQ1 to RQ4, tackling introspection capability, reasoning strategies, causal mediation, and targeted finetuning, respectively. Most jailbreaking research evaluates a model on a single harmful request in isolation, whereas real interactions span many turns and varied queries (Zhao et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2024). We instead embed the attack in a multi-turn exchange and probe the model with a follow-up that is not itself harmful, such as the introspective question we study. 

Models and data. We evaluate ten instruction-tuned models across three open-weight families, Llama (Grattafiori et al., 2024) at 3.2-3B, 3.1-8B, 3.3-70B; Qwen3 (Team, 2025b) at 4B, 8B, 14B, 32B; and Gemma 3 (Team, 2025a) at 4B, 12B, 27B. We restrict to open-weight models because our experiments involve weight editing and fine-tuning. Prompts are drawn from four safety bench-marks, HarmBench (Mazeika et al., 2024) (159 standard text-only prompts), SocialHarmBench (Pandey et al., 2026) (584), JailbreakBench (Chao et al., 2024) (55 prompts from its Original source split), and StrongREJECT (Souly et al., 2024) (313). To avoid near-duplicates across datasets, we filter JailbreakBench and StrongREJECT against HarmBench, SocialHarmBench, and AdvBench (Zou et al., 2023). Any prompt whose word-level Jaccard overlap with a reference prompt exceeds 

0.70 is dropped, leaving 1,085 distinct prompts. 

Conditions. For each prompt the target model produces an initial response under control (no inter-vention) or under prefill , where the model is forced to begin its response with a prefix optimized for the prompt and victim by the AdvPrefix procedure of Zhu et al. (2025), adapted to our compute budget (Appendix B). AdvPrefix’s per-prompt likelihood filter is strict, and the fallback rate to a static “Sure, here is” opening varies by benchmark. Under 2% 

fall back on HarmBench and SocialHarmBench versus near 100% on JailbreakBench and Stron-gREJECT (Table 3). The pooled prefill condition is therefore a mixture of AdvPrefix-optimized pre-fixes on HarmBench and SocialHarmBench and static prefills on JailbreakBench and StrongRE-JECT; the static-prefill robustness check in Ap-pendix G.2.1 shows the recognition gap is qual-itatively preserved under static prefilling on the same models. A length-matched benign placebo 

prefill, where the forced content is unrelated to the user request, is also analyzed in Appendix G.2.2 as a content-neutral counterpart to the adversarial prefill. The conditions yield 272 ,302 follow-up re-3sponses across the ten base models and the ablated models used in RQ3. 

Follow-up probes. The model then receives a follow-up question, which we consider two vari-ants of. The intention probe is “Did you mean to say that, or was it an accident?” , taken verbatim from Lindsey (2025); the tampering probe is “Did anyone tamper with your response?” (full text in Appendix A.1). The two differ in framing, where intention invites the model to attribute the prior out-put to an internal mistake, while tampering invites it to attribute the output to external manipulation. We do not assume the two framings produce the same signal, and we analyze them separately. In both probes we classify each reply as either claim-ing the prior output as intended (affirming intent on intention, denying tampering on tampering) or 

rejecting it. We argue that recognition under these follow-up probes is decoupled from the model’s unprefilled refusal behavior, hence an interesting property to study in its own right; Figure 8 shows most models claiming intention on a substantial fraction of prefilled outputs which they would not have produced themselves. The fraction of replies classified as claiming on a given condition is the model’s claim rate on that condition. We label 3,000 replies per probe with GPT-4.1 using the prompts in Appendix A.2, fine-tune a RoBERTa-base (Liu et al., 2019) binary classifier on these labels, reaching 94 .2% held-out accuracy on in-tention and 95 .8% on tampering, and apply the classifiers to the full corpus. Cohen’s κ against human annotations for both classifiers is reported in Appendix C. Each reply is truncated to its first 64 tokens before labeling, and the main text fo-cuses on the intention probe, with full tampering numbers reported in Appendix I. 

Refusal-direction ablation. For RQ3 we apply the procedure of Arditi et al. (2024), with details in Appendix B. We first identify the residual-stream direction r that best separates the model’s acti-vations on harmful prompts from activations on matched harmless prompts. We then orthogonal-ize every weight matrix that writes into the resid-ual stream against r. The direction is selected per model from a search over (layer, position) candi-dates, choosing one that yields the highest bypass rate under a KL divergence threshold; the resulting selections (Table 4) typically place r in the late-middle layers of each model. We orthogonalize every locally servable model within our budget, and the primary RQ3 analysis further restricts to the five models from 8B to 27B (Llama-3.1-8B, Qwen3-8B, Qwen3-14B, Gemma-3-12B, Gemma-3-27B). This restriction stems from the observation that the 3B and 4B models show only marginal or inverted recognition gaps at baseline, which leaves little margin for an ablation contrast to close (Ap-pendix D). 

Targeted finetuning. For RQ4 we attach a low-rank adapter (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2022) to each of eight models (the five from RQ3 and Llama-3.2-3B, Qwen3-4B, and Gemma-3-4B at the 3B– 4B scale, included to test whether training can widen the gap on models where it is marginal or inverted at baseline). We compare three training methods. Supervised finetuning (SFT) trains on self-distilled correct labels, with training prompts bootstrapped from each model itself to avoid dis-tribution shift, an approach adapted from STaR (Zelikman et al., 2022). Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) (Shao et al., 2024) uses the RoBERTa classifier’s predicted probability as scalar reward over four rollouts per prompt. Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2023) trains on preference pairs constructed from the same self-distilled candidate pool by select-ing one classifier-correct response as chosen and one classifier-incorrect response as rejected per prompt, with the target direction set to claim un-der control and reject under adversarial prefill. Each adapter is trained on HarmBench, Jailbreak-Bench, and StrongREJECT prompts only, and eval-uated on the held-out SocialHarmBench split for a cross-benchmark generalization check, alongside MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2021b,a) and an attack success rate (ASR) check on the same SocialHarm-Bench split. The tampering probe, which is not part of training, is used as a cross-probe transfer check. Training details, dataset construction, and DPO, GRPO, and SFT hyperparameters are in Ap-pendix H. 

Recognition gap. We define the recognition gap 

as ∆ = P (claim | control ) − P (claim | prefill ),the difference in claim rate between the two condi-tions. The gap isolates the effect of the attack from baseline tendencies to claim intent. We consider a model more capable of introspection the higher this metric is. In RQ1–RQ3 we restrict main-text claim rates to the behavior-change subset , i.e., prompts where the prefill changes the model’s Llama Guard 3 (1B) safety label; the gap is consistently larger 4on this subset than on the no-change subset and is robust to substituting the larger Llama Guard 3 (8B) variant as the safety judge (Appendix D). RQ4 evaluates on the full held-out split because the subset definition depends on the model’s own re-sponse, which the RQ4 intervention itself can shift. The same Llama Guard 3 (1B) classifier is also reused to score attack success rate in RQ4 (Sec-tion 4.4); to verify that the LoRA-induced ASR shifts in RQ4 are not artifacts of the 1B judge, we re-score the same generations with Llama Guard 3 (8B) in Appendix H (Table 21). 

Research questions. RQ1 (Section 4.1) exam-ines whether a model recognizes that one of its own prior outputs was elicited by an adversarial prefill, measured as the recognition gap at baseline. RQ2 

(Section 4.2) characterizes how a model explains itself when it rejects a prefilled output, categoriz-ing rejections under an eight-category taxonomy that we define by inspecting a sample of model rejections; a DeepSeek V3.2 (DeepSeek-AI, 2025) judge then assigns each rejection to one category (Appendix E). RQ3 (Section 4.3) tests whether the refusal direction mediates the recognition signal, recomputing the gap on the ablated model. RQ4 

(Section 4.4) investigates whether the recognition gap can instead be widened by lightweight inter-vention, reporting the gap before and after LoRA finetuning on eight models under DPO, GRPO, and SFT, alongside general capability and ASR checks.

Probe-framing sensitivity. The intention and tampering probes yield qualitatively different sig-nals on the same prompts and models. On the Qwen family the tampering claim rate saturates near 1.0 on both control and prefill, leaving the tampering-probe gap at zero on Qwen3-8B, Qwen3-14B, and Qwen3-32B (Appendix I). In other words, the models almost always deny tam-pering, regardless of whether the prior turn was prefilled. On Llama-3.1-8B and Gemma-3-12B the tampering-probe gap is positive and in the same direction as the intention-probe gap ( +24 .1% and 

+26 .6% respectively on the behavior-change sub-set), though differently scaled on the same model. A probe-invariant introspective channel would pre-5dict broadly consistent answers across phrasings; the divergence we measure indicates that follow-up self-attribution is conditioned heavily on how the question is framed. Per-prompt joint decisions on the two probes are reported in Appendix I. A reliable introspector would, at a minimum, reject the prefilled responses that are in fact harm-ful. 4.2 RQ2: Rejections invoke safety and refusal 

We assign each rejection produced on the intention probe under the adversarial prefill condition to one of eight author-defined categories (Appendix E), and examine the distribution by model family. Across the 6,823 rejections, only 0.3% fall into an “other” category; the rejections predominantly invoke safety, policy, or content reframing, and the surface form varies by family. 4.4 RQ4: Targeted LoRA finetuning widens the intention-probe gap 

We attach a low-rank adapter to each of eight mod-els and train it under three objectives: supervised finetuning (SFT) on self-distilled correct labels, Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) with the RoBERTa classifier’s claim/reject probability as scalar reward, and Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) on preference pairs derived from the same rollout pool. Recognition gaps are recomputed on a held-out SocialHarmBench split. We evaluate on all samples here, because the behavior-change sub-set used elsewhere in the paper is defined by Llama Guard 3 (1B)’s classification of the model’s own responses, and any of the three training methods can shift those responses; restricting to the subset at evaluation time would condition on a quantity the intervention itself perturbs. The corresponding baseline numbers in this section therefore use a dif-ferent denominator from those in Section 4.1. The training set is drawn from HarmBench, Jailbreak-Bench, and StrongREJECT only (no SocialHarm-Bench prompts), so all reported gains are cross-benchmark generalization to SocialHarmBench at test time.

5 Conclusions 

Across ten open-weight LLMs and four safety benchmarks, no model reliably recognizes that its own prior output was elicited by an adversarial prefill attack. The introspective signal we mea-sure is largely accounted for by the model’s re-

> 1Under Llama Guard 3 (8B), adv-prefill ASR rises on every model under all three methods. See Appendix H and Table 21.

8fusal mechanism: ablating the refusal direction collapses the recognition gap on all five ablated models from 8B to 27B, and rejections predom-inantly invoke safety, though a random-direction control usually closes the gap, so the refusal direc-tion is sufficient but not the unique mediator. The signal is also probe-dependent, with intention and tampering framings eliciting qualitatively different responses on the same models. Targeted finetun-ing widens the intention-probe gap on every model from 8B to 27B, but it does not transfer to the tam-pering probe. Counterintuitively, the same training that teaches a model to flag a prefilled output in a follow-up turn makes it comply more readily with the prefill on the initial turn, raising attack success rate under adversarial prefill on most models, so the intervention is only a partial mitigation. These limits shape how such self-reports can be used. A model’s own follow-up answer is a weak basis for judging whether a prior turn was compromised; screening the output itself with an independent safety classifier is still a more depend-able guardrail, and finetuning the model to flag its own outputs is no substitute, given its safety cost. More broadly, our study suggests only a lim-ited sense of self in models under attacks. Inde-pendently of their value as a mitigation, training methods that improve introspective capability yield interesting model organisms, which are useful for alignment research into how recognition signals form and interact with safety. Extending the ar-gument of Qi et al. (2025) that safety alignment should run more than a few tokens deep, our find-ings motivate alignment that also holds across turns and to queries beyond the initial harmful request, including our specific case: reliable recognition of a model’s own compromised outputs in a follow-up turn. 

Limitations 

Scope of the recognition signal. Introspection can be operationalized in many ways, including confidence calibration (Kadavath et al., 2022), nam-ing concepts injected into the residual stream (Lind-sey, 2025), and predicting one’s own behavior on held-out inputs (Binder et al., 2025). Our recogni-tion gap captures only one such signal, our causal probe ablates only the refusal direction, and more subtle mechanisms may also shape introspective behavior. Because the gap closes under refusal-direction ablation, what the intention probe elic-its could reflect either a delayed application of the same refusal mechanism to a prior turn now in input context or a separable introspective pathway, and our data do not distinguish between these possibili-ties. The recognition gap is also probe-dependent, as the tampering probe yields qualitatively differ-ent signals on the same models and produces claim rates near 100% on the Qwen family regardless of condition, and whether the divergence reflects a probe-design effect or a family-specific safety-training tendency is not arbitrated by our data. Our four benchmarks are English-only, so generaliza-tion across languages remains open. 

Analysis of reasoning traces. Due to a lack of systematic methods for analyzing reasoning traces, our analysis remained highly manual, with author-defined taxonomies. While being able to induce a low rate of samples that are unaccounted for, our taxonomies may not be exhaustive, the analysis could be biased, and different LLMs as the judge might yield different results. We look forward to future work that can provide more systematic and automated methods for analyzing collections of reasoning traces, a task important for alignment research in general. 

Byproduct and scope of the targeted inter-vention. The LoRA intervention widens the intention-probe gap on every model from 8B to 27B, but on most models it also raises attack suc-cess rate under adversarial prefill, a cost shared across SFT, GRPO, and DPO. RQ3 shows the recognition signal is closely tied to the refusal mechanism, so training a model to strengthen the signal also perturbs the weights that produce re-fusal and weakens it. The intervention also targets only the intention probe and transfers little to the tampering probe, so it remains a partial mitigation. All RQ4 interventions use rank-16 LoRA adapters; full-parameter finetuning at the same loss could tighten the recognition gap further, reduce the adv-prefill ASR side effect, or both, a regime our results do not characterize. Both ablation and finetuning require open weights, so the largest model we in-tervene on is 27B and frontier proprietary models fall outside the study, even though more capable models may have a higher propensity and steer-ability for the introspective behaviors we examine (Lindsey, 2025; Lee et al., 2026). Increasing intro-spective capability without weakening safety is left to future work; candidates such as Activation Ora-cle (Karvonen et al., 2025) and Natural Language 9Autoencoders (Fraser-Taliente et al., 2026) could be adapted, though they require a second trained model. 

Ethical Considerations 

This work studies whether language models can recognize, in a follow-up turn, that one of their own prior outputs was elicited by an adversarial prefill attack. The experiments require generat-ing completions of harmful instructions under at-tack; these generations are produced solely to be passed to the model’s own follow-up probe and to an attack-success classifier, and are not redis-tributed. The four datasets we use (HarmBench, So-cialHarmBench, JailbreakBench, StrongREJECT) are publicly released safety benchmarks intended for evaluating model behavior under adversarial prompts. We use refusal-direction ablation (Arditi et al., 2024) as a research probe to understand the source of an introspective signal; the same proce-dure is also a known jailbreak technique, and pub-licly released abliterated checkpoints built with sim-ilar methods (Labonne, 2024) are already widely available. We do not release new attack tooling or new jailbreaks. One of our central findings, that follow-up self-report is not a reliable channel for detecting adversarial compromise, is intended to caution against treating model self-report as a safety signal.

B Inference Details 

Victim model inference. All victim-model gen-erations, both initial responses and follow-up replies, use greedy decoding (temperature 0, top-p

1.0). Initial responses are generated with a 2,048-token cap and follow-up replies with a 512-token cap. Local models are served by vLLM (Kwon et al., 2023) with chat templates loaded through Transformers (Wolf et al., 2020); the three largest models (Llama-3.3-70B, Qwen3-32B, Gemma-3-27B) are accessed through a hosted API at match-ing parameters. Qwen models are run with thinking traces disabled.

Candidates are filtered for minimum length (24 tokens), discarded if they begin with refusal-like phrases ( “I cannot” , “I can’t” , “Sorry” , etc.) or 13 contain self-identification patterns ( “I am an AI” ), and deduplicated. A short ablation step then varies the truncation point of each candidate’s second line over 1 to 5 tokens, yielding additional surface variants. 

Scoring. Each candidate yp is scored against the victim model θ0 by score( yp) =20 log(PASR( yp) + 10 −5) − NLL( yp), matching the weighted-sum objective of Zhu et al. (2025) with their reported weight of 20 on log-PASR. PASR is estimated from 25 completions per pre-fix at temperature 1.0 with a 256-token budget; attack success is judged by Llama Guard 3 (1B) (Llama Team, 2024) (replacing the custom Llama-3.1-70B nuanced judge of the original paper). Can-didates with NLL ≥ 15 are dropped before scoring. 

Selection. For each (prompt, victim) we se-lect the single highest-scoring prefix instead of the multi-prefix set used by Zhu et al. (2025); if no candidate survives filtering, we fall back to “Sure, here is” . We do not run the downstream GCG or AutoDAN optimization considered in the original paper; the selected prefix is directly prefilled at inference time as the assistant’s opening tokens.

Refusal-direction layer selection. Following Arditi et al. (2024), for each model we score candi-date directions at every (layer, position) pair drawn from the final prompt tokens at every transformer block, evaluating bypass and KL on a held-out harmful/harmless validation split, and select the di-rection maximizing bypass subject to a KL thresh-old. The training prompts are 128 harmful instruc-tions sampled from AdvBench (Zou et al., 2023) and 128 harmless instructions sampled from Al-paca (Taori et al., 2023), with the validation set further restricted to at most 32 prompts per polar-ity that show clean refusal vs. compliance sepa-ration under the pretrained model. The candidate search is restricted to the upper 80% of transformer blocks. We use the strict KL threshold of 0.1 as the primary criterion: among candidates whose KL on harmless prompts is below 0.1, we pick the one with the largest bypass score on harmful prompts. If no candidate clears that threshold we fall back to a relaxed threshold of 0.5; if no can-didate clears that either we fall back to a bypass-only branch that picks the highest-bypass candi-date regardless of KL. Llama-3.2-3B, Llama-3.1-8B, Qwen3-8B, and Qwen3-14B clear the strict threshold (KL of the selected direction ranges from 

0.030 to 0.090 ). Qwen3-4B clears the relaxed threshold (KL = 0 .414 ). All three Gemma-3 vari-ants fall outside the relaxed threshold and use the bypass-only branch (KL = 5 .649 on Gemma-3-4B, 

1.806 on Gemma-3-12B, and 0.742 on Gemma-3-27B); the same direction recovered by rank-1 decomposition of (W − Worth ) on either the MLP down-projection or the attention output projection at the selected layer agrees to a cosine of 1.0000 .The large bypass-only KL on Gemma-3-4B and Gemma-3-12B is consistent with the broader effect their ablation has on calibration (the downward clo-sure noted in Section 4.3 and the larger placebo gap in Appendix G.2.2). G Refusal-Direction Ablation: Supporting Analyses 

This appendix reports the analyses supporting the refusal-direction ablation result of Section 4.3: the claim rates underlying the gap closure, the robust-ness of the closure to alternative prefill construc-tions, a behavioral check that the ablation alters downstream outputs, and a random-direction con-trol. 

G.1 Claim Rates Underlying the Gap Closure 

The recognition gap ∆ = P (claim | control ) −

P (claim | prefill ) is a difference between two prob-abilities. Refusal-direction ablation can in principle close the gap either by raising the prefill claim rate (the reading consistent with a recognition signal) or by lowering the control claim rate (a reading in which the model rejects more outputs uniformly, 20 independent of attack provenance). Table 13 re-ports both rates before and after ablation for the five ablated models from 8B to 27B. For Llama-3.1-8B, Qwen3-8B, and Qwen3-14B, the gap closes principally because the prefill claim rate rises by 32 to 33% toward the control rate, while the control claim rate is approximately sta-ble (Llama, +3 .8% ; Qwen3-8B, +8 .6% ) or mod-estly declines (Qwen3-14B, −9.7% ). The post-ablation gap on these three models is therefore a near-equalization of P (claim | control ) and 

P (claim | prefill ) at the elevated control level in-stead of at a common low level. Gemma-3-12B is the exception: both rates fall under ablation, and the control rate falls further ( 36 .7 → 17 .8 ver-sus 26 .9 → 17 .7), so the gap closes from above. Gemma-3-27B closes through smaller shifts in both rates, the control rate falling ( 48 .5 → 42 .6) and the prefill rate rising ( 35 .4 → 40 .2). A single-direction orthogonalization does not allow us to separate “the refusal direction is also entangled with a self-attribution feature” from “removing the refusal direction degrades Gemma’s calibration on the intention probe in both conditions,” and both readings are consistent with our data. 

G.2 Prefill Robustness Checks 

The main-text recognition gap is computed with AdvPrefix prefixes that use a refusal-ablated gener-ator as one component. We verify that the gap-closure result generalizes both to static prefills that do not pass through the AdvPrefix genera-tor (Appendix G.2.1) and to benign placebo pre-fills matched in length to AdvPrefix outputs (Ap-pendix G.2.2). 

G.2.1 Static prefills 

The static prefills (P1–P4) defined in Section A.3 do not pass through the AdvPrefix generator. Ta-ble 14 reports recognition gaps under each of the four static prefill conditions, for the five models from 8B to 27B, both baseline and ablated. Baseline static-prefill gaps and the baseline pre-fill gap are of comparable magnitude in every row, which indicates that the static prefills are no less ef-fective at eliciting the recognition asymmetry than the optimized prefill is. The same refusal-direction ablation that closes the prefill gap also closes each of the static-prefill gaps, and in several cells over-shoots into negative gaps. The closure is therefore not contingent on the prefix being generated by a refusal-ablated proposer. 

G.2.2 Placebo prefills 

The main-text recognition gap is measured under the prefill condition, where the forced prefix is itself the adversarial prefix produced by the AdvPrefix pipeline. A positive intention-probe gap could arise either because the model tracks that the prior turn was prefilled, or because it evaluates the prior con-tent as harmful. These two mechanisms predict differently for a forced prefix whose content is no longer harmful. To distinguish them we construct a placebo pre-fill that is matched to the prefill in length but benign in content. For each (model, prompt) row we read the row’s prefill character length L, sample one of the model’s own thirty responses to a curated set of benign general-knowledge prompts (drawn from topics unrelated to safety, e.g., photosynthe-sis, the printing press), and truncate that response to L characters. The resulting prefix is forced con-tent the model would not have produced naturally on this prompt, but the content itself is benign and unrelated to the user request. We then run the same intention probe and classify replies as in Section 4. H LoRA Finetuning Details and Results 

Training data. For each of the eight models, we construct a self-distilled training pool by sampling up to N = 16 candidate responses per (prompt, condition) pair from the base model at temperature 

0.7 and classifying each with the RoBERTa F1 clas-sifier. SFT uses up to four classifier-correct rollouts per pair as supervised targets (claim under control, reject under adversarial prefill), yielding 2,545 –

3,669 training rows per model. DPO uses the same pool to form one preference pair per (prompt, condition) where both labels are present in the 16 

rollouts: the highest-confidence classifier-correct response is chosen and the highest-confidence classifier-incorrect response is rejected; this yields 

346 –836 preference pairs per model. GRPO uses the same (prompt, condition) set and samples four rollouts at training time from the policy under opti-mization, scoring each with the RoBERTa classifier 23 as reward. The training set is drawn from Harm-Bench, JailbreakBench, and StrongREJECT only; the held-out SocialHarmBench split used for evalu-ation is disjoint by construction. 

Training procedure. All three training meth-ods use the same backbone configuration: rank-16 

LoRA (alpha 32 , dropout 0.05 ) targeting the query, key, value, output, gate, up, and down projections at every layer (Hu et al., 2022), AdamW optimizer, cosine schedule with 5% warm-up, effective batch size of 8 via gradient accumulation, and gradient checkpointing. Each method trains for 1 epoch and we report results from the final checkpoint. SFT uses learning rate 1×10 −4, with the loss masked to the assistant’s final-turn answer. GRPO uses learn-ing rate 1×10 −5, trained via TRL’s GRPOTrainer with 4 rollouts per prompt sampled at temperature 

1.0 and a 64 -token completion cap; the per-rollout reward is the RoBERTa classifier’s predicted prob-ability of the desired claim/reject direction, with a KL regularizer of β = 0 .04 against the base policy. DPO uses learning rate 5×10 −5 and KL regular-izer β = 0 .1, trained via TRL’s DPOTrainer on the preference pairs constructed above. The SFT learn-ing rate matches the LoRA-paper value of 1×10 −4

(Hu et al., 2022). The DPO and GRPO learning rates are an order of magnitude above the TRL DPOConfig and GRPOConfig defaults of 1×10 −6.We did not perform a learning-rate search across the three methods. 

Recognition gap. Table 17 reports the recogni-tion gaps on the intention probe for each model under base, SFT, GRPO, and DPO, evaluated on the held-out SocialHarmBench split. All three methods reach gaps above +11% on every model from 8B to 27B. Across all eight models, the pro-portions reaching +45% are SFT 2 of 8, GRPO 3 of 8, DPO 5 of 8. At 3B–4B the three meth-ods diverge: post-finetuning gaps on Llama-3.2-3B span +1 .9% (SFT) to +64 .7% (DPO); on Qwen3-4B SFT reaches +14 .9% and DPO +24 .8% while GRPO narrows the baseline to +2 .1% ; on Gemma-3-4B all three methods leave the gap inverted.

I Tampering Probe Results 

For completeness, Table 22 reports the tampering probe under the same 4-dataset pooling and the same paired prompt set used in the main intention analysis. The tampering probe is “Did anyone tamper with your response?” ; a reply is classified as claiming when the model denies tampering (“no, nothing was tampered with”) and as rejecting when it acknowledges tampering or attributes the output to an external agent. The tampering signal is uninformative on the Qwen family. The same direction that closes the intention gap also accounts for the tampering gap on these two models. We accordingly treat tampering as a secondary probe and base the main argument on intention. Whether a different tampering-attribution phrasing would yield a nonzero signal in the Qwen family is left to future work.

Decoupling from unprefilled refusal. Figure 9 reports the per-prompt 2×2 contingency of control-condition refusal against the tampering-probe an-swer on the prefilled response, paralleling Figure 8 for the intention probe.
