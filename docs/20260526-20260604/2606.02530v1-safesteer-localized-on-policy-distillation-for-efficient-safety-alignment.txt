Abstract 

Aligning Large Language Models (LLMs) with human values often degrades their general ca-pabilities, termed the alignment tax. Existing methods mitigate this by balancing dual objec-tives, which heavily rely on massive general-purpose data or auxiliary reward models. In this paper, we argue that, because safety fea-tures are inherently sparse within the output distribution, alignment requires localized mod-ifications rather than global trade-offs. To this end, we propose SafeSteer , which performs on - policy distillation confined to safety tokens. First, we construct a safety teacher via activa-tion steering. Based on this teacher, we de-velop a safety token selection algorithm. Con-sequently, SafeSteer restricts the reverse KL penalty to these tokens during training to pre-serve general capabilities. Experimental results across diverse models show that our SafeSteer achieves a superior trade-off between safety and general capabil-ity compared with existing methods, attain-ing strong safety performance on seven safety benchmarks with only minimal degradation on five general capability benchmarks. No-tably, SafeSteer requires only 100 harmful samples without using any general-purpose data, less than 1% of what previous base-lines used, considerably reducing alignment cost. More details are on our project page at https://anjingkun.github.io/SafeSteer/.

Existing safety alignment methods often frame the problem as dual-objective optimization, forcing a trade-off between safety and general capabilities (Dai et al., 2024). Prior works have explored strate-gies such as mixing massive amounts of general-purpose data into the training set (Zhang et al., 2025; Zou et al., 2024), orthogonally projecting safety gradients into the null space of general capa-bility representations (Niu et al., 2025), or training auxiliary reward models (Dai et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2024). However, since safety features are inherently sparse within an LLM’s output distribu-tion, alignment demands not global trade-offs but rather localized modifications. This entails adjust-ing safety features while mitigating forgetting. On-policy distillation (OPD) (Gu et al., 2024; Agarwal et al., 2024) mitigates global distribu-tion shifts by training on self-sampled data via reverse-KL minimization. However, to provide

supervisory signals, standard LLM OPD typically necessitates an external, stronger teacher model (Lu and Lab, 2025). Therefore, recent studies (Zhao et al., 2026b; Shenfeld et al., 2026) further con-struct a self-teacher through expert demonstrations, but these approaches heavily rely on the model’s in-context learning capabilities. Inspired by prior research (Arditi et al., 2024), we construct a safety teacher via activation steering. Specifically, we ex-tract a refusal direction and inject it into the model’s residual stream activation at inference. This yields a steered teacher that provides stable safety signals, without the need for external stronger models or prompt engineering. Even with an effective teacher, standard OPD still applies a penalty to the entire vocabulary, which inevitably affects general capability tokens. Crucially, safety tokens are highly sparse in the out-put distribution and largely disjoint from those re-quired for general tasks; a global penalty therefore damages general capabilities. To achieve a truly sparse update, we mine safety tokens by contrasting the output distributions of the safety teacher and the base model. Specifically, rather than selecting to-kens with the highest logit difference, we introduce a voting-based aggregation algorithm utilizing con-trastive log probabilities to identify a safety token subset most sensitive to the refusal direction. Based on these designs, we propose SafeSteer ,a lightweight safety alignment framework that up-dates only the sparse safety subset of the output distribution. It requires merely 100 harmful sam-ples during OPD training, introducing no external general-purpose data, no auxiliary reward model, and no orthogonal projection. Figure 1 shows the performance of SafeSteer compared to other base-lines. Figure 2 illustrates the entire pipeline. Our contributions are as follows: • We propose SafeSteer , a lightweight safety alignment framework. It couples an activation steering-based safety teacher with safety to-ken selection to perform on-policy distillation, greatly reducing the alignment tax. • Experimental results across four models show that SafeSteer significantly outperforms exist-ing methods across seven safety benchmarks, with minimal performance degradation on five general capability benchmarks. • SafeSteer requires only 100 harmful data sam-ples for training, less than 1% of the data vol-ume demanded by previous baselines, which drastically reduces the alignment cost and makes it practical for rapid deployment. 

2 Related work 

2.1 Safety Alignment Training 

A fundamental challenge in safety alignment is mit-igating the trade-off between safety and general ca-pabilities (Huang et al., 2025). SFT-based methods (Qi et al., 2024; Dai et al., 2024) typically suffer from severe capability degradation. As a remedy, preference-based methods like BFPO (Zhang et al., 2025) highly depend on mixing large amounts of general-purpose data to prevent catastrophic for-getting. Alternatively, constrained optimization ap-proaches, such as SafeRLHF (Dai et al., 2024), Mo-CAN (Huang et al., 2024), and NSPO (Niu et al., 2025), maximize the expected reward subject to safety or general capability constraints. However, these methods introduce significant costs, requiring either auxiliary reward models or carefully curated general-purpose data to maintain performance. 

2.2 On-Policy Distillation 

OPD has become a popular approach for LLM training (Yang et al., 2025; DeepSeek-AI, 2026; Song and Zheng, 2026; Li et al., 2026), because it achieves higher training efficiency than outcome-based reward methods (e.g., GRPO) via token-level rewards (Lu and Lab, 2025), mitigates forgetting by training on on-policy rollouts (Agarwal et al., 2024), and avoids mode-covering behavior by min-imizing reverse KL (Gu et al., 2024). Recent works (Zhao et al., 2026b; Shenfeld et al., 2026) use ad-ditional expert demonstrations to allow students to act as teachers, but this approach heavily relies on the model’s in-context learning capabilities. 

2.3 Representation Engineering 

Many studies (Zou et al., 2023a; Bricken et al., 2023; Li et al., 2025; Zhao et al., 2026a) have shown that representations contain rich informa-tion, enabling us to steer the model’s behavior via activation. As demonstrated by previous research (Arditi et al., 2024), the model’s refusal behavior can be mediated by a single vector. Furthermore, prior works (Bricken et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2025; Lindsey et al., 2025) reveal that safety neurons are very sparse in LLMs. By rerouting representations responsible for harmful outputs, Circuit Breaker (Zou et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2025) trains models to 𝝅 𝟎 𝝅 𝒕

3 Method 

As Figure 2 illustrates, we propose SafeSteer , a lightweight safety alignment framework. It lever-ages activation steering to construct a safety teacher and restricts the distillation penalty to safety tokens. Through SafeSteer , we can update sparse safety features while preserving general capabilities. 

3.1 Preliminary: Activation-Steered Safety Teacher Model 

Building upon the base model π0, we construct the safety teacher model πt through activation steer-ing. Specifically, following the previous study (Arditi et al., 2024), we extract a refusal direction 

d ∈ Rdmodel by comparing the model’s hidden rep-resentations when run on harmful and harmless instructions and determine an injection layer ℓ. Let 

hℓ indicate the original input residual stream enter-ing the ℓ-th transformer block of πt. We register a forward pre-hook at this layer to replace hℓ with: 

h⋆ℓ = hℓ + d. (1) The modified representation h⋆ℓ is then fed into the subsequent layers. By continuously injecting this refusal direction at all token positions through-out the sequence, the resulting model πt is capable of providing highly stable refusal signals, consis-tently rejecting both harmful and harmless prompts. Further analysis is provided in Appendix D. 

3.2 Task Overview 

We initialize a student model πs and a safety teacher model πt from a base model π0 with vocab-ulary V. SafeSteer consists of two main stages: 1. Before training, we roll out πt on harmless instructions x ∈ D harmless , sampled from Al-paca (Taori et al., 2023). By contrasting the output distributions of πt and π0, we extract a sparse subset of safety tokens S ⊂ V .2. During alignment, πs generates trajectories on harmful instructions x ∈ D harmful , sampled from PKU-SafeRLHF (Dai et al., 2024). Then 

πs is optimized by restricting the reverse KL penalty to the safety token subset S.

4 Experiment 

4.1 Experiment Setup Models. To evaluate the effectiveness of our 

SafeSteer , we apply it to four widely used open-source LLMs: Llama-3-8B-Instruct, Llama-3.2-3B-Instruct (Llama Team, 2024), Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct (Qwen et al., 2025), and Qwen3-4B-Instruct-2507 (Yang et al., 2025). 

Baselines. We compare our SafeSteer against several baseline approaches: MoCAN (Huang et al., 2024), W-DOOR (Zhao et al., 2025), BFPO (Zhang et al., 2025), NSPO (Niu et al., 2025), and DPO-Mix. Following prior work (Niu et al., 2025), the DPO-Mix variant is configured with a 50/50 mix-ing ratio of helpfulness and safety preferences. For training data, our method uses only 100 instruc-tions randomly sampled from the unsafe subset of PKU-SafeRLHF(Dai et al., 2024), whereas NSPO samples 40% of the entire dataset and the remain-ing baselines are trained on the full dataset. 

Benchmarks. We comprehensively evaluate our 

SafeSteer across both safety and general capabili-ties. For safety benchmarks, we report the Attack Success Rate (ASR) on two categories: (i) Harm-ful queries , using AdvBench (Zou et al., 2023b), PKU-SafeRLHF (Dai et al., 2024), HarmBench (Mazeika et al., 2024), JailbreakBench (Chao et al., 2024), and SORRY-Bench (Xie et al., 2024); and (ii) Red-team queries , utilizing HarmfulQA (Bhard-waj and Poria, 2023) and ALERT (Tedeschi et al., 2024). To validate that SafeSteer can preserve the model’s general capabilities, we evaluate its perfor-mance across four dimensions: (i) General Knowl-edge on MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2020); (ii) In-struction Following on AlpacaEval (Li et al., 2023); (iii) Mathematical Reasoning on GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021) and MATH500 (Lightman et al., 2023); and (iv) Coding on HumanEval (Chen et al., 2021). 

Implementation Details. To extract the refusal direction, we utilize 160 harmful and 160 harm-less instructions provided by previous work (Arditi et al., 2024). During safety token selection, we use the same 160 harmless samples. Additionally, we set the safety token subset size to |S| = 50 . In the 

SafeSteer training stage, we apply a learning rate of 1e-6 to Llama models and 1e-5 to Qwen mod-els, with M = 8 rollouts. For safety evaluation, we employ Llama-Guard-4-12B as the default judge across all benchmarks, except SORRY-Bench (Xie et al., 2024), which relies on its natively fine-tuned scoring model. To measure general capabilities, we use DeepSeek-V4-Flash (DeepSeek-AI, 2026) to report the Win Rate compared to Alpaca-7B on AlpacaEval, while using lm-eval (Gao et al., 2024) for the remaining benchmarks. During model in-ference, we set do_sample=False at temperature 0. When temperature is 1.0, we run the evaluation three times and report the average. More details on training and evaluation are in Appendix A. 

4.2 Experiment Results 

Tables 1 and 2 report the evaluation results of our 

SafeSteer at temperature 0, and results for tem-perature 1.0 are shown in Appendix B. Overall, 

SafeSteer substantially improves safety with only a marginal impact on general capabilities. 

Safety. Under both temperature settings of 0 and 1.0, SafeSteer attains the lowest ASR among all methods on the Qwen family, by a clear margin over the strongest baselines. At temperature 0, 

SafeSteer pushes the average ASR down to 0.91% on Qwen3-4B-Instruct, compared to 1.13% for the next-best MoCAN and 2.87% for the base model. Likewise, it lowers the ASR to 1.48% on Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct, which represents a 2.5 × reduction against 3.75% for the strongest baseline BFPO. Similarly, for the Llama family, SafeSteer remains highly competitive: it not only reduces the ASR on Llama-3.2-3B-Instruct from 5.26% to 4.06%, but also achieves the second-lowest ASR on Llama-3-8B-Instruct. Notably, while some baselines slightly surpass SafeSteer in safety, most exhibit a signif-icant degradation in general capabilities. For ex-ample, W-DOOR collapses both Llama models to near half of their base capability, dropping the average score from 63.61 to 34.04 on Llama-3.2-3B-Instruct and from 63.64 to 37.82 on Llama-3-8B-Instruct. Consistent with the observation in NSPO (Niu et al., 2025), we find that DPO-Mix can even increase ASR on both Llama-3-8B-Instruct and Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct, indicating that the align-ment tax cannot be solved by naive data mixing. 

General Capability. Without relying on any general-purpose data necessitated by other meth-ods, SafeSteer can still maintain the model’s gen-eral capabilities. For the Qwen family, at both tem-peratures 0 and 1.0, SafeSteer preserves general capabilities with negligible loss: at temperature 0, the average score shifts slightly from 71.68 to 71.39 on Qwen3-4B-Instruct and from 68.90 to 69.17 on Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct, effectively retain-ing both models’ original ability. For the Llama family at temperature 0, SafeSteer limits the perfor-mance degradation to 0.75 points on Llama-3.2-3B-Instruct and outperforms all baselines on Llama-3-8B-Instruct. SafeSteer also ranks second among all methods on Llama-3-8B-Instruct at temperature 1.0. This demonstrates that our SafeSteer success-fully decouples the safety feature from the general capability space.

5 Analysis 

5.1 Representation Shift Analysis 

To further verify whether our SafeSteer success-fully preserves general capabilities during safety alignment, we examine the representation space of the base model π0, the student model πs, and the safety teacher model πt using 300 general-purpose prompts sampled from AlpacaEval. The last-layer hidden state at the final token of each prompt is then extracted and projected into a 2D space via Principal Component Analysis (PCA). Figure 3 visualizes the representation shifts on the Llama models. Although the refusal direction introduces a severe representation shift in πt, πs

trained through SafeSteer exhibits no such shift on general capabilities. The representations of πs over-lap almost entirely with those of π0. The marginal density plots along both axes confirm that their rep-resentations remain virtually identical. This visu-alization shows that despite learning safety behav-iors from πt, our SafeSteer effectively segregates these safety adaptations from the general capability space, thereby achieving safety alignment without incurring the alignment tax. 

5.2 Design Choices in Safety Token Selection 

The safety token selection in Section 3.3 relies on two key design choices: the rollout model and the response length . We empirically analyze both. 

Rollout model πt is necessary. Using the base model π0 to generate rollout trajectories for safety token selection causes a catastrophic degradation in safety capabilities. Specifically, training a Llama-3-8B-Instruct variant under this setting increases its JailbreakBench ASR from 1.0% to 75.0%. We observe that the resulting safety token subset S

is dominated by formatting tokens (e.g., “ \n\n ”and “ \r\n\r\n ”) rather than genuine safety tokens. Consequently, the model merely learns to insert meaningless paragraph breaks instead of refusing. This failure stems from the shallow safety align-ment of Llama-3-8B-Instruct. Because its safety guardrails are concentrated on the very first few output tokens, the unsteered π0 lacks the capabil-ity to maintain refusal behaviors at deeper posi-tions, causing the contrastive log probability com-puted along this trajectory to fail in capturing safety tokens. Conversely, using πt for rollouts avoids this vulnerability: the steered trajectory forces the (a) Response length = 1. (b) Response length = 7.

Response length transforms superficial align-ment into semantic safety. The response length

6 Conclusion 

In this paper, we challenge the conventional dual-objective trade-off in safety alignment, arguing that safety features are inherently sparse and only re-quire localized modification. We introduce SafeS-teer , an efficient on-policy distillation framework that mitigates the alignment tax. By combining an activation-steered safety teacher with automatically mined safety-critical tokens, SafeSteer restricts the OPD penalty precisely to safety features. Extensive experiments demonstrate that SafeSteer achieves state-of-the-art safety performance with little degra-dation in general capabilities across multiple LLMs. Remarkably, SafeSteer requires only 100 training samples without any auxiliary models or massive general-purpose data, reducing the alignment cost. 7 Limitations 

While SafeSteer is an effective and lightweight method for safety alignment, our study has the following limitations. 

Reliance on the base model’s refusal capabil-ity. SafeSteer constructs the safety teacher πt by activation-steering the base model π0 and mines safety tokens from the resulting rollouts. This pipeline therefore presumes that the base model already possesses a refusal capability, which is the case for modern instruction-tuned models but not for purely pretrained checkpoints. 

Model scale. Due to computational constraints, all of our experiments are conducted on mod-els with no more than 10B parameters (Llama-3-8B-Instruct, Llama-3.2-3B-Instruct, Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct, and Qwen3-4B-Instruct). Although the localized distillation objective is agnostic to model size in principle, the effectiveness of SafeSteer on larger models has not been verified, and the optimal choice of hyperparameters may shift at that scale. 

Modality and architecture coverage. The cur-rent evaluation is restricted to text-only autoregres-sive LLMs. We have not investigated how SafeS-teer transfers to vision-language models (VLMs) or diffusion-based language models (DLLMs) (Nie et al., 2026). Extending SafeSteer to these settings is a natural direction for future work.
